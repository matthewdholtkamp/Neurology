    Headache — overview & triage¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    The first job in any headache patient is not to diagnose migraine — it is to exclude a secondary headache that will hurt the patient if you miss it. Triage on red flags first, then treat the primary headache.

    Scale of the problem (GBD 2023): headache disorders affect ~2.9 billion people and are the 6th leading cause of health loss worldwide, with ~2× the disability burden in women. Tension-type headache is the most prevalent (~25%), but migraine causes ~90% of headache disability — and medication overuse drives a large share of both, so the MOH screen below earns its place.

    🚩 Red flags — the full SNNOOP10 screen (any hit changes your pathway)

    Flag Worry Do now
    S — Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) Meningitis, abscess, systemic illness Labs, LP (image first if focal/↓LOC/papilledema)
    S — Secondary risk factors (HIV, immunosuppression) Opportunistic infection, abscess LP + targeted imaging
    N — Neoplasm history Brain metastasis, primary tumor MRI brain with contrast
    N — Neurologic deficit / ↓ consciousness / seizure Mass, stroke, encephalitis MRI (CT if emergent)
    O — Onset sudden / thunderclap (max <1 min) SAH, RCVS, dissection, CVST, apoplexy Non-contrast CT (6-h rule below) → LP/CTA/MRV
    O — Older age (new/progressive >50) GCA, mass ESR/CRP (start steroids if GCA likely), MRI
    P — Pattern change / progressive New secondary process Re-work up as a new headache
    P — Papilledema ↑ICP (IIH, mass, CVST) MRI + MRV, then LP with opening pressure
    P — Positional — worse standing Low pressure (SIH — CSF leak) MRI (pachymeningeal enhancement/brain sag) → blood patch
    P — Positional — worse lying / on waking High pressure (IIH, mass) MRI/MRV → LP opening pressure
    P — Precipitated by Valsalva / exertion / sex SAH, RCVS, posterior-fossa lesion CT + CTA head/neck; MRI
    P — Pregnancy / puerperium Pre-eclampsia, CVST, RCVS, PRES, apoplexy See the pregnancy box below
    P — Painful red eye ± autonomic features Acute angle-closure glaucoma (fixed mid-dilated pupil, nausea — vision loss in hours), cavernous sinus, Tolosa-Hunt, pituitary Check IOP / ophthalmology STAT; MRI
    P — Post-traumatic onset Structural injury / PTH Head-CT rule; see PTH page
    P — Pathology of the immune system Opportunistic infection, PML, lymphoma LP + MRI with contrast
    P — Painkiller overuse or a new drug MOH; drug effect Medication review; count acute-med days

    Reassuring: an established, stereotyped, self-resolving headache with a normal exam and no red flags — that is where primary headache lives.

    Thunderclap workup — the 6-hour CT rule

    • Non-contrast CT within 6 h of onset is ~99% sensitive for SAH — a negative CT in a neurologically intact patient presenting <6 h reasonably excludes SAH without an LP (2023 AHA/ASA).
    • >6 h from onset: CT sensitivity falls (~86–97%) — do an LP for xanthochromia if suspicion remains, and CTA/MRV for RCVS/dissection/CVST.
    • The Ottawa SAH Rule (100% sensitive) can screen out low-likelihood patients before imaging.

    When NOT to image (AHS Grade A) — avoid the reflex MRI

    Neuroimaging is not needed for headaches consistent with migraine when the neurologic exam is normal and there are no atypical features or red flags (AHS 2020). The prevalence of a significant intracranial abnormality in that group is ~0.2%. Over-imaging drives cost, incidental findings, and anxiety — reserve it for a red-flag hit (relevant where imaging access is limited, e.g., deployed/austere settings).

    On the horizon (not clinic-ready): blood biomarkers (GFAP, neurofilament light) are being studied to separate serious secondary from primary headache — promising, not yet validated for use.

    Rapid approach¶

    1. Screen for red flags (SNNOOP10 table). Any hit → secondary work-up pathway.
    2. Normal exam + no red flags? Classify the primary headache by phenotype.
    3. Route to the diagnosis:
      • Recurrent, moderate–severe, disabling, ± photophobia/phonophobia/nausea/aura → Migraine (if it reached neurology, it is almost never "just tension")
        • Quick screen — ID Migraine (<2 min, AHS-endorsed): in the last 3 months, did headaches (1) limit your activities ≥1 day? (2) cause nausea? (3) make you sensitive to light? ≥2 "yes" → migraine (≈84% sensitive, 93% PPV).
        • "Sinus headache" is migraine until proven otherwise — up to 88% of self-diagnosed sinus headaches meet migraine criteria (recurrent, predictable, photophobia/nausea, normal exam → migraine, not antibiotics).
      • Headache during or after SARS-CoV-2 infection → treat by phenotype (usually migraine- or tension-type-like); COVID is a recognized trigger for NDPH — re-work up if the pattern changes.
      • Bilateral, pressing/tightening, mild–moderate, NOT worsened by activity, no nausea → tension-type headache — analgesics (ibuprofen, high-dose acetaminophen) ± amitriptyline if frequent (VA/DoD 2023). But if it's disabling or frequent enough to reach you, reconsider migraine.
      • Severe unilateral attacks with ipsilateral autonomic features (tearing, ptosis, rhinorrhea), restless/agitated → Trigeminal autonomic cephalalgias (cluster, paroxysmal hemicrania, SUNCT/SUNA)
      • Brief, electric, triggered stabs in a trigeminal division → Trigeminal neuralgia — and remember MS can present this way
      • Neck-driven pain with reduced cervical range of motion, reproducible from the neck → cervicogenic headache — manual therapy/PT ± nerve blocks (a top-3 secondary headache per VA/DoD; common in service members with neck trauma/load-bearing)
      • Headache ≥15 days/month with acute-med overuse → Medication overuse headache
      • Daily and unremitting from a remembered onset, no prior headache history → New daily persistent headache
      • Headache after head/neck trauma → Post-traumatic headache

    Don't-miss secondary headaches (quick recognition)¶

    RCVS — recurrent thunderclaps, and do NOT give steroids

    Reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome — the 2nd commonest cause of thunderclap headache after SAH. Recurrent thunderclaps over days–weeks, often triggered by vasoactive drugs, sexual activity, exertion, or the postpartum state; may cause convexity (not basal) SAH, and CTA/MRA shows "string of beads" vasoconstriction. Use the RCVS² score (≥5 → ~90% sensitive/~99% specific; ≤2 excludes it). Steroids worsen RCVS — the key distinction from primary CNS angiitis. Treat with nimodipine + trigger avoidance.

    IIH vs SIH — same "positional," opposite pressure and treatment

    • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH — high pressure): young, overweight woman, daily headache + transient visual obscurations + pulsatile tinnitus + papilledema. Dx: MRI/ MRV (exclude mass/CVST) → LP opening pressure >250 mm. Rx: weight loss + acetazolamide; surgery (ONSF, CSF shunt, venous stenting) for progressive vision loss (untreated → permanent visual loss in 15–20%). GLP-1 receptor agonists are an emerging option.
    • Spontaneous intracranial hypotension (SIH — low pressure): orthostatic headache (worse standing, better lying) from a CSF leak; MRI shows pachymeningeal enhancement + brain sag. Rx: epidural blood patch. (Opposite positional pattern and opposite treatment from IIH — don't conflate them.)

    GCA — don't wait for the biopsy

    New headache >50 with jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, visual symptoms, or PMR → ESR/CRP + temporal artery exam. If GCA is likely, start high-dose corticosteroids immediately (vision loss is preventable but irreversible) and arrange temporal-artery biopsy / vascular imaging.

    Pregnancy & postpartum headache — a broad, dangerous differential

    Secondary causes underlie >⅓ of acute headaches referred for neuro consult in pregnancy. Actively consider: pre-eclampsia/eclampsia, CVST, RCVS, PRES, cervical artery dissection, pituitary apoplexy, and post-dural-puncture headache.

    • Pregnancy-specific red flags: third trimester, elevated BP, thrombocytopenia, elevated liver enzymes or creatinine.
    • Imaging: MRI preferred (limit gadolinium); non-contrast CT acceptable in emergencies.
    • Activate a maternal stroke team (vascular neurology + maternal-fetal medicine + obstetric anesthesiology) for stroke-like symptoms (2026 AHA/ASA).

    History & exam essentials¶

    History — cover the core elements: onset & tempo, character, location, severity, duration & frequency, associated symptoms (aura, nausea, photophobia, autonomic), triggers, medications (including acute-med days/month — the MOH screen), and comorbidities (mood, sleep, prior head/neck trauma). Have every patient keep a headache diary (frequency, duration, acute-med days, triggers) for ≥1 month — it's the single most useful diagnostic and monitoring tool.

    Exam: vitals (including BP and temperature), fundoscopy for papilledema, cranial nerves, visual fields, focal motor/sensory screen, gait, neck stiffness, and temporal artery palpation in older patients. A normal neuro exam is a large part of what makes a primary headache diagnosis safe.

    The headache vertical is complete: Migraine, Cluster & TACs, Trigeminal neuralgia, Medication overuse headache, New daily persistent headache, and Post-traumatic headache.
