    Migraine¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: recurrent, disabling headache — usually lateralized, throbbing, moderate-to-severe, worse with activity, with photophobia/phonophobia, nausea, and sometimes aura. For a general provider: if a headache patient reaches neurology with a normal exam and no red flags, treat as migraine — it is almost never "just tension."

    🚩 Before you call it migraine

    Clear the headache red flags first. Thunderclap onset, fever/neck stiffness, focal deficit, papilledema, new headache after 50, new headache in pregnancy, or a change in an established pattern all mean work it up as a secondary headache first.

    Rapid approach¶

    1. Exclude secondary headache (red-flag screen). Normal exam + no red flags → proceed.
    2. Diagnose by phenotype (ICHD-3, below). Imaging is not required for typical migraine with a normal exam.
    3. Treat the attack — stratified care: match drug strength to attack severity, treat early.
    4. Decide on prevention — if attacks are frequent or disabling, or acute meds are overused.
    5. Screen for and unwind medication overuse.
    6. Disposition: almost always outpatient. Avoid admitting known migraineurs.

    Diagnosis (ICHD-3, summarized)¶

    Migraine without aura: ≥5 attacks lasting 4–72 h with ≥2 of {unilateral, pulsating, moderate/severe, aggravated by routine activity} and ≥1 of {nausea/vomiting, or photophobia and phonophobia}, not better explained by another diagnosis.

    Migraine with aura: ≥2 attacks with ≥1 fully reversible aura symptom (visual, sensory, speech/language, motor, brainstem, or retinal) and ≥3 of these 6 characteristics: (1) at least one symptom spreads gradually over ≥5 min; (2) two or more symptoms occur in succession; (3) each symptom lasts 5–60 min; (4) at least one symptom is unilateral; (5) at least one symptom is positive (e.g., scintillations, pins-and-needles); (6) aura is accompanied by, or followed within 60 min by, headache. Motor aura = hemiplegic migraine; brainstem aura — both are red flags to distinguish from TIA/stroke on first presentation and both contraindicate triptans/ergots.

    Chronic migraine: headache ≥15 days/month for >3 months, with migrainous features ≥8 days/month. Screen for medication overuse in every chronic patient.

    Focused H&P + exam¶

    • Attack profile: frequency (headache days/month), duration, disability (MIDAS/HIT-6 if outpatient), aura, triggers, and — critically — how many days/month they take acute medication (MOH screen).
    • Exam: vitals with BP, fundoscopy, cranial nerves, visual fields, focal screen, gait. Normal between attacks. Any fixed deficit → re-open the secondary work-up.
    • Diary: have the patient keep a headache diary; it drives both prevention decisions and (for service members) profile and disability documentation.

    Workup¶

    Typical migraine with a normal exam needs no imaging or labs. Image (MRI preferred) only for red flags, atypical/persistent aura, or a change in pattern. Do not order EEG for migraine.

    Acute (abortive) treatment — the ladder¶

    Four rules that matter more than drug choice: (1) treat early — at the first sign of pain, while it is still mild; (2) match strength to severity (stratified care — don't make a severe attack fail acetaminophen first); (3) give an adequate dose and route — under-dosing and a vomited pill are the two commonest "triptan failures"; (4) cap acute days to prevent medication-overuse headache (MOH) — and note the prevention cap is stricter than the diagnostic threshold:

    • Recommended cap (to prevent MOH): triptans, ergots, opioids, and combination analgesics ≤2 days/week (≈8–10 days/month); plain NSAIDs/simple analgesics ≤3 days/week (≈10–15 days/month).
    • ICHD-3 diagnostic threshold for MOH (a higher bar — this is when you label it, see MOH section): ≥10 days/month for triptans/ergots/opioids/combinations, ≥15 days/month for simple NSAIDs/acetaminophen.
    • Gepants are exempt — they are not associated with MOH, so no monthly cap applies. This makes them the acute agent of choice for patients who are already overusing or who need frequent treatment.

    Step 1 — mild-to-moderate attack (start here for most)

    Any one of these, taken early, with an antiemetic if nauseated:

    • Naproxen 500–550 mg, ibuprofen 400–600 mg, or aspirin 900–1000 mg PO.
    • APAP/aspirin/caffeine (250/250/65 mg, 2 tabs) for those who can't take an NSAID.
    • Antiemetic adjunct: metoclopramide 10 mg or prochlorperazine 10 mg — these treat the headache, not just the nausea, and speed oral-drug absorption.

    → No reliable relief across 2–3 attacks, or attacks are moderate-to-severe from the start → Step 2.

    Step 2 — moderate-to-severe attack: a triptan (first-line migraine-specific drug)

    Default: sumatriptan 50–100 mg PO at onset; may repeat once after ≥2 h (max 200 mg/day). Even better for moderate/severe attacks: sumatriptan 85 mg + naproxen 500 mg together (co-administration aborts more attacks than either alone) — a reasonable go-to.

    Pick the formulation to fit the attack:

    • Fast onset / vomiting / wakes with pain: sumatriptan 6 mg SC (works in ~10 min — the most effective route) or zolmitriptan 5 mg / sumatriptan 10–20 mg nasal.
    • Wants an oral that acts fast: rizatriptan 10 mg ODT or eletriptan 40 mg (most potent oral triptan).
    • Long attacks / high recurrence: naratriptan 2.5 mg or frovatriptan 2.5 mg (long half-life, lower recurrence — also used for menstrual mini-prophylaxis).

    "Triptan failure" means ≥2–3 different triptans tried adequately — full dose, taken early, ideally including a non-oral route — not one under-dosed pill. Optimize before escalating.

    Do NOT give a triptan (or ergot) if:

    Coronary, cerebrovascular, or peripheral vascular disease; uncontrolled hypertension; hemiplegic or brainstem-aura migraine; pregnancy (relative). In any of these, skip Step 2 and go straight to Step 3 — the non-vasoconstrictors.

    Step 3 — triptan failed (≥2 tried) OR contraindicated: gepant or ditan

    Neither class causes vasoconstriction, so both are safe in vascular disease; gepants do not cause MOH (useful when a patient is already overusing).

    • Gepants (small-molecule CGRP-receptor antagonists — oral/nasal):
      • Ubrogepant (Ubrelvy) 50–100 mg PO; may repeat once after ≥2 h (max 200 mg/day).
      • Rimegepant (Nurtec ODT) 75 mg — max 1/day; uniquely doubles as a preventive (every-other-day), so it's the pick when a patient needs both.
      • Zavegepant (Zavzpret) 10 mg nasal — fast, useful with vomiting.
    • Ditan — lasmiditan (Reyvow) 50 / 100 / 200 mg PO: effective with no vascular contraindication, but sedating with a hard ≥8-hour driving/operating restriction and CNS depression — poor fit for pilots, drivers, and duty. Don't stack with other serotonergic/CNS depressants.
    • DHE (nasal/SC/IV) for prolonged or refractory attacks — see the status-migrainosus order set below.

    TRICARE — the acute gepant is Ubrelvy, and it needs a PA

    On TRICARE, ubrogepant (Ubrelvy) is the preferred acute gepant, but it is not PA-free. The DoD prior-authorization criteria require: age ≥18, prescribed by or in consultation with a neurologist, and a contraindication/intolerance/2-month failure of ≥2 triptans (sumatriptan, rizatriptan, zolmitriptan, or eletriptan), and it cannot be combined with another gepant/Nurtec. Practically: document two failed triptans and loop in neuro, and the PA goes through. Rimegepant (Nurtec) is non-preferred (higher tier).

    Do not use — for acute migraine

    Opioids and butalbital-containing combinations (e.g., Fioricet). Poor efficacy, high dependence and MOH risk, and they blunt response to specific drugs. Don't start them; taper patients already on them. This holds in clinic, the ED, and on the ward.

    Preventive treatment — the ladder¶

    Who needs prevention (start it if ANY apply)

    • ≥4 migraine headache days/month, or
    • ≥2 days/month with significant disability despite good acute treatment, or
    • Acute treatment overused, failing, or contraindicated, or
    • Patient preference, or
    • Regardless of frequency: hemiplegic or brainstem-aura migraine, prolonged/persistent aura, migrainous infarction, or status migrainosus.

    Set expectations up front: every preventive gets an adequate trial — 8–12 weeks at target dose (oral) or ~3 months (CGRP) — before you call it a failure. Track with a diary, aim for ≥50% reduction in headache days, and taper after ~6–12 months of good control. Start low, titrate slowly, and change one thing at a time.

    The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)¶

    1. Everyone: lifestyle foundation (below) + a written acute plan + a headache diary.
    2. Start ONE oral first-line agent matched to the patient's comorbidities (Tier 1). Titrate to target, hold 8–12 weeks.
    3. Inadequate? Switch to a first-line oral from a different class (or move to CGRP now — AHS 2024 permits CGRP as first-line without failing orals; on TRICARE the frictionless CGRP entry point is Emgality, see below).
    4. ≥2 adequate oral trials failed, or disabling disease: go to a CGRP therapy (Tier 2).
    5. First CGRP fails after ~3 months: switch mechanism — receptor ⇄ ligand antibody (see the box) — or move to an oral gepant preventive.
    6. Chronic migraine (≥15 headache days/month): add onabotulinumtoxinA (PREEMPT); it can be combined with a CGRP therapy in refractory disease.
    7. Still refractory: neurology referral, combine Botox + CGRP, consider IV eptinezumab.

    Well-established, low-cost, on the TRICARE formulary with no PA. Let the comorbidity choose the drug — that's how you get a two-for-one and the best adherence:

    Patient / comorbidity Preferred agent Target dose Watch for
    HTN, palpitations, anxiety, tremor Propranolol (or metoprolol, timolol) Propranolol LA 80–160 mg/day (up to 240) Fatigue, bradycardia, bronchospasm; avoid in asthma; caution in depression
    Overweight/obese, comorbid seizure Topiramate Titrate 25 mg/week → 50–100 mg/day (divided) Paresthesias, cognitive slowing, kidney stones, teratogenic, flat soda taste
    Insomnia, comorbid depression/tension-type Amitriptyline / nortriptyline 10–50 mg qHS Anticholinergic effects, weight gain, morning grogginess (nortriptyline milder)
    Can't use a beta-blocker, wants once-daily Candesartan 8–16 mg/day Dizziness, hyperkalemia, renal function
    Frequent aura, not childbearing potential Valproate / divalproex 500–1000 mg/day Never in pregnancy potential (neural-tube defects); tremor, weight, LFTs
    Menstrual-pattern Frovatriptan / naratriptan perimenstrual mini-prophylaxis Frovatriptan 2.5 mg BID × 6 days around menses Short-term, timed to cycle

    Per the 2024 AHS position statement, CGRP therapies are a first-line option — there is no requirement to fail two older orals first clinically. (Insurers, including TRICARE, may still require step therapy for the non-preferred agents — see the TRICARE box.) Two formats:

    • Anti-CGRP monoclonal antibodies (injectable/IV; dosed monthly–quarterly). Best adherence, cleanest tolerability — watch constipation and BP with erenumab, injection-site reactions with all.
    • Oral gepants for prevention: atogepant (Qulipta) 10–60 mg daily, or rimegepant (Nurtec) 75 mg every other day — rimegepant uniquely doubles as acute + preventive. Good for needle-averse patients; gepants do not cause MOH.

    See the receptor-vs-ligand box below to choose the antibody and to switch intelligently.

    • OnabotulinumtoxinA (PREEMPT protocol): 155 units across 31 fixed sites every 12 weeks (up to 195 units / 39 sites with the optional "follow-the-pain" additions) — chronic migraine ONLY, not episodic.
    • Botox and CGRP therapies are both first-tier for chronic migraine and can be combined in refractory cases.
    • Regular sleep, meals, hydration, aerobic exercise; trigger awareness; limit caffeine and acute-med days; treat comorbid mood, sleep, and anxiety disorders.
    • Neuromodulation devices (e-TNS/Cefaly, remote electrical neuromodulation/Nerivio, sTMS, non-invasive vagus nerve stimulation) — useful when drugs are contraindicated, unwanted, or in pregnancy.

    CGRP antibodies: receptor vs. ligand — which to start, how to switch¶

    The user's key question, answered directly: only one CGRP antibody blocks the receptor; the other three bind the CGRP peptide (ligand) itself. The oral gepants are all small-molecule receptor antagonists.

    Agent (brand) Target Route / interval Notes
    Erenumab (Aimovig) CGRP receptor (the only receptor mAb) SC monthly (70 or 140 mg) Watch constipation and rising BP; no loading dose
    Fremanezumab (Ajovy) CGRP ligand SC monthly or quarterly Only mAb with a quarterly option — good adherence
    Galcanezumab (Emgality) CGRP ligand SC monthly (240 mg load, then 120 mg) Also FDA-approved for episodic cluster
    Eptinezumab (Vyepti) CGRP ligand IV quarterly Fastest onset; guarantees adherence; good for chronic/refractory
    Atogepant (Qulipta) · Rimegepant (Nurtec) Receptor (small molecule) Oral Preventive gepants; Nurtec = acute + preventive

    How to choose and switch

    • Efficacy is comparable across the antibodies — choose on route/interval, comorbidity, and formulary (on TRICARE that means start Emgality, see below).
    • If the first antibody fails after an adequate ~3-month trial, switch mechanism: a ligand mAb (Emgality/Ajovy/Vyepti) → the receptor mAb (Aimovig), or vice-versa. Switching within the same target is lower-yield than crossing to the other mechanism.
    • Antibody class failing entirely? Try an oral gepant (Qulipta/Nurtec) — different pharmacology, and a reasonable next mechanism before Botox/combination therapy.

    TRICARE — start Emgality; it's the one CGRP with no prior authorization

    Verified against the DoD Uniform Formulary and Express Scripts PA criteria (2025):

    • Emgality (galcanezumab) is the preferred CGRP antibody and needs NO prior authorization — $0 at an MTF/home delivery. This is your go-to first CGRP on TRICARE: you can start it without fighting a step-therapy PA, which also lines up with AHS's first-line stance.
    • Aimovig (erenumab) and Ajovy (fremanezumab) are non-preferred → PA + step therapy: the patient must have failed Emgality and failed ≥1 drug from two oral prophylactic classes (AEDs: valproate/topiramate · beta-blockers · TCAs/SNRIs), age ≥18, neurologist involvement. So Aimovig is exactly where you land when you need to switch a ligand→receptor mechanism after Emgality fails — the failed-Emgality trial is the PA justification.
    • Oral gepant preventive — Qulipta (atogepant): preferred but PA-required. DoD criteria: ≥18, neurologist consult, failed ≥1 drug from two oral classes, and failed ≥1 CGRP injectable — i.e., TRICARE seats Qulipta behind the injectable mAb. Nurtec ODT (rimegepant) is non-preferred.
    • Bottom line for a TRICARE panel: oral first-line (formulary, no PA) → Emgality (no PA) → Aimovig/Ajovy or Qulipta (PA, after Emgality) → Botox (chronic) / combination. Keep neurology named on the CGRP and gepant scripts — every one of these PAs requires it.

    Order set — status migrainosus / ER¶

    Graded to the 2025 American Headache Society guideline for parenteral migraine treatment in the ED (Robblee et al.). Two headline points: prochlorperazine and the greater occipital nerve block are now Level A "must offer," and opioids are to be avoided (IV hydromorphone is a Level A "must NOT offer"). Nerve blocks are opioid-sparing, fast, and safe in pregnancy — reach for them early, not just as a last resort.

    Escalation cadence: give the whole of Tier 1 together up front, then reassess pain every 30 minutes. At 30 min, if only partial relief, you may repeat the dopamine antagonist once (prochlorperazine 10 mg or metoclopramide 10 mg). If still inadequate at ~60 min, move to Tier 2; reassess again at ~30–60 min before Tier 3. Nerve blocks act within 10–15 min, so they help decide escalation quickly.

    Tier 1 — first-line (offer to everyone; exclude red flags first)

    Give a dopamine antagonist + an NSAID + a nerve block, not one drug in isolation.

    • Prochlorperazine 10 mg IV (Level A — first choice), or metoclopramide 10–20 mg IV (Level B — if prochlorperazine unavailable). Infuse each over ~15 min. May repeat once at 30 min if partial response.
      • Akathisia/dystonia: many co-give diphenhydramine 25 mg IV; note the 2025 guideline rates diphenhydramine "may not offer" as a migraine treatment and evidence that it prevents akathisia is mixed — use it for the reaction, not for the headache.
    • Ketorolac 15–30 mg IV (Level B) — or dexketoprofen 50 mg IV or diclofenac 75 mg IM where available.
    • Sumatriptan 6 mg SC (Level B) — if not already used and no vascular contraindication.
    • IV fluids — normal saline ~1 L only if the patient is dehydrated (or before chlorpromazine); fluids alone are not a migraine treatment.

    Tier 1 — nerve blocks (do these early; opioid-sparing, pregnancy-safe)

    • Greater occipital nerve block (GONB) (Level A — must offer). Bilateral, ~2–3 mL of bupivacaine 0.5% (or 1–2% lidocaine) per side over the greater occipital nerve — landmark: ~1/3 of the way from the occipital protuberance toward the mastoid, medial to the occipital artery pulse. Onset ~10–15 min. Strong adjunct (a 2025 RCT: ~95% vs ~48% ≥50% relief at 2 h as add-on); especially useful with occipital tenderness or when avoiding systemic drugs.
    • Supraorbital / supratrochlear nerve blocks (Level B) — for frontal pain; ~1–2 mL of bupivacaine 0.5% or 1–2% lidocaine per site.
    • Sphenopalatine ganglion (SPG) block (emerging; heterogeneous evidence) — transnasal 2–4% lidocaine via a long cotton-tipped applicator or catheter device (e.g., ~1–2 mL per nostril); simple and low-risk, reasonable as an adjunct.

    Tier 2 — add if inadequate at ~60 min; reassess again at 30–60 min

    • Magnesium sulfate 1–2 g IV over ~15–30 min — best evidence in migraine with aura.
    • Dexamethasone 10 mg IV (Level C) — modest acute benefit and reduces 24–72 h recurrence (NNT ~9); give before discharge regardless.
    • Valproate 500–1000 mg IV (Level C) — avoid in pregnancy/childbearing potential.
    • Chlorpromazine 12.5–25 mg IV (Level C) — alternative neuroleptic; give a ~1 L saline preload and watch sedation/orthostatic hypotension. May repeat after ~30 min.

    Tier 3 — refractory / status migrainosus (monitored; consider neurology)

    • Droperidol 2.5 mg IV (Level C) or haloperidol 2.5–5 mg IV (Level C) — potent neuroleptics (droperidol relieves ~90% of refractory attacks). Get a baseline ECG for QT (droperidol carries a QT black-box warning); co-treat akathisia. May repeat once after ~30–60 min.
    • Dihydroergotamine (DHE) 0.5–1 mg IV, preceded by an antiemetic (e.g., metoclopramide 10 mg or ondansetron 4 mg IV); may repeat 0.5–1 mg q8h. Contraindicated in coronary/ peripheral vascular disease, uncontrolled HTN, a triptan/ergot within 24 h, or pregnancy. For admitted status migrainosus, the inpatient Raskin protocol (IV DHE q8h over 2–3 days, with an antiemetic) can break the cycle.
    • Specialist/monitored infusions (procedural-sedation monitoring where relevant):
      • IV lidocaine — 1 mg/kg bolus, then ~2 mg/min infusion (do not exceed 4 mg/min; keep level < 5 µg/mL); status-migrainosus courses may run up to ~2 days.
      • Propofol — 0.5 mg/kg bolus, then 0.25 mg/kg every 10 min for up to 1 h.
      • Low-dose ketamine — subacute infusion ~0.1–0.4 mg/kg/h (note: a single low-dose ED bolus was negative in an RCT, so reserve ketamine for monitored refractory use).

    Eptinezumab (Vyepti) IV — not a routine ED drug

    The 2025 AHS guideline evaluated eptinezumab 100 mg IV for acute treatment: it beat placebo for 2-h headache freedom (23.5% vs 12.0%), but the pivotal RCT was run mostly outpatient. So the guideline rates it Level B ("should offer") only for patients matching that trial population, and Level U (no recommendation) for the general ED population. Practically: don't reach for it in a typical ED visit — it's slow-onset, costly, and unproven for acute ED use. Its real role is outpatient prevention (see the preventive ladder).

    Before discharge & what to avoid

    • Dexamethasone 10 mg IV/IM once if not already given — reduces early recurrence.
    • Start/adjust a preventive, give a written acute-medication cap, and arrange follow-up.
    • Avoid opioids and butalbital — Level A must-not-offer (hydromorphone); they worsen outcomes and seed medication-overuse headache.
    • Speak to the nurse directly to sequence meds; if admitted, add scheduled antiemetic and sleep hygiene.

    Medication overuse headache (MOH)¶

    Suspect when headaches are frequent and acute meds are used often — roughly ≥10 days/month for triptans/ergots/opioids/combination analgesics, or ≥15 days/month for simple NSAIDs/ acetaminophen. Management: start a preventive, cap or withdraw the overused agent, bridge symptoms (e.g., NSAID/antiemetic course ± short steroid taper), and counsel. Gepants do not cause MOH and are useful acute agents in this setting.

    Disposition¶

    Migraine is an outpatient diagnosis. Treat the attack, start/adjust prevention, arrange follow-up, and give a written acute-med limit. Admission is rarely helpful for a known migraineur.

    Military medicine — migraine

    Standards below are quoted from DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1 (accession) / Vol 2 (retention) and AR 40-501 (27 June 2019); the DES process is governed by DoDI 1332.18 / DoDM 1332.18. Confirm current editions and service-specific aeromedical policy before you counsel or document.

    • Deployability: most controlled migraine is deployable. Frequent/incapacitating attacks, or a regimen not maintainable in theater, can be deployment-limiting. Consider environment: topiramate (heat intolerance, cognitive effects), sedating TCAs, lasmiditan (≥8-h driving/duty restriction — poor fit for drivers/aircrew), and CGRP mAbs (cold-chain/ refrigeration and resupply) all matter downrange.
    • TRICARE prescribing (see acute/preventive boxes): acute — triptans are formulary/no-PA; Ubrelvy (acute gepant) needs a PA (≥2 failed triptans + neurologist). Preventive — orals are formulary/no-PA; Emgality is the only CGRP with no PA (start here); Aimovig/ Ajovy/Qulipta need a PA and neurologist, after Emgality. Name neurology on every CGRP/ gepant script.
    • Profile (DA Form 3349): temporary profile during titration or a flare; permanent profile for chronic/refractory disease with functional limits documented.
    • EPTS / LOD: migraine often predates service — document onset and pre-service history; it affects existed-prior-to-service and line-of-duty determinations.
    • Retention — DoD & Army: the joint standard (DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2, para 5.26.g) places migraine, tension, or cluster headaches with frequent incapacitating attacks below retention standards. AR 40-501 para 3-31g operationalizes "frequent" for the Army as incapacitating attacks interfering with duty or social activities ≥3 days/month → mandatory neurologist referral, up to 6 months of prophylaxis, and referral to the DES (MEB/PEB) if not adequately controlled at 6 months (or directly, if unlikely to respond).
    • Accession — DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1, para 6.26.e: for applicants, a history in the prior 24 months of headaches that caused missed activity >2×/12 months, required prescription meds

      2×/12 months, or needed prophylaxis is disqualifying for entry (relevant to recruiting/ waivers).

    • Document what the standard turns on: a headache diary showing ≥3 incapacitating days/month, the neurology referral, the prophylaxis trial and its outcome, and concrete duty impact.
    • Aeromedical / special duty: a history of chronic, recurrent, or incapacitating headaches — including ocular or acephalic migraine with visual disturbance — is disqualifying for flying duty (AR 40-501, ch. 4). Many migraine medications are independently disqualifying/waiver- dependent; confirm current aeromedical policy before prescribing.

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    Written from primary guidelines and trials; source articles are a reading list, not copied.

    What's changed and what matters now:

    • Stratified beats step care. Match acute-drug strength to attack severity from the start; a triptan + NSAID combination outperforms either agent alone for moderate/severe attacks.
    • CGRP therapies are now first-line for prevention. The 2024 American Headache Society position statement (Charles et al., Headache) makes anti-CGRP mAbs (erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab, eptinezumab) and oral gepants (atogepant, rimegepant) a first-line option and removed the prior requirement to fail two 8-week trials of older oral preventives first. They are "a first-line option," not "the" only first-line — choose between an oral agent and a CGRP therapy by patient factors and access.
    • Gepants and ditans expand acute options. Gepants (ubrogepant, rimegepant, zavegepant) and the ditan lasmiditan work without vasoconstriction, making them options in cardiovascular/ cerebrovascular disease where triptans are contraindicated. Gepants do not cause MOH.
    • Receptor vs. ligand antibodies. Only erenumab (Aimovig) blocks the CGRP receptor; fremanezumab (Ajovy), galcanezumab (Emgality), eptinezumab (Vyepti) bind the CGRP ligand. Efficacy is comparable, so choose on route/interval, comorbidity, and formulary — and when one fails, switch mechanism (ligand ⇄ receptor) rather than swap two agents with the same target.
    • Formulary steering is now part of the decision (TRICARE). On the DoD Uniform Formulary, galcanezumab (Emgality) is the preferred, no-PA CGRP antibody — the frictionless first CGRP — while erenumab/fremanezumab, atogepant (Qulipta), and ubrogepant (Ubrelvy) are PA-gated and require neurologist involvement; rimegepant (Nurtec) is non-preferred. This makes "start Emgality" both the clinically first-line and the administratively easiest move.
    • OnabotulinumtoxinA (PREEMPT) remains a first-tier preventive for chronic migraine only.
    • ED care leads with dopamine antagonists + nerve blocks, not opioids. The 2025 AHS parenteral-ED guideline (Robblee et al.) elevated prochlorperazine and the greater occipital nerve block to Level A "must offer," kept metoclopramide/ketorolac/SC sumatriptan as "should offer," and made IV hydromorphone a Level A "must-NOT-offer." Nerve blocks are fast, opioid-sparing, and safe in pregnancy.
    • Avoid opioids and butalbital — they worsen the disease and drive medication-overuse headache.
    • Prevention only works if trialed adequately — 8–12 weeks at target dose before judging failure.
