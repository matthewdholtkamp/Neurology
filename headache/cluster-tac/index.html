    Cluster headache & the trigeminal autonomic cephalalgias¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: the TACs are severe, strictly unilateral headaches with ipsilateral cranial autonomic features (tearing, conjunctival injection, ptosis/miosis, rhinorrhea, eyelid edema). They are separated from one another — and from migraine — mainly by attack duration, frequency, and treatment response. Get these right and the treatment falls out of the diagnosis.

    🚩 Red flags — image every new TAC at least once

    Despite the classic teaching that self-resolving attacks are benign, a new TAC deserves an MRI brain (with attention to the pituitary and cavernous sinus) to exclude a secondary mimic — pituitary lesions and posterior-fossa pathology can reproduce a TAC phenotype. Image urgently for: fixed Horner's/other cranial-nerve deficit, abnormal exam, side-locked but atypical features, or thunderclap onset.

    Diagnose by the clock (ICHD-3)¶

    Disorder Attack length Frequency Signature Treatment key
    Cluster headache 15–180 min 1 every other day → 8/day Agitated/restless; circadian & seasonal bouts O₂ + SC/nasal triptan; verapamil prevention
    Paroxysmal hemicrania 2–30 min >5/day (>half the time) Female-predominant Absolute response to indomethacin
    SUNCT / SUNA 1–600 sec ≥1/day, often many Cutaneous triggers (touch, chewing) Lamotrigine; IV lidocaine for crisis
    Hemicrania continua continuous continuous + exacerbations Background pain with autonomic flares Absolute response to indomethacin

    If a strictly unilateral headache with autonomic features responds completely to indomethacin, it is paroxysmal hemicrania or hemicrania continua — a diagnosis you don't want to miss because it is so treatable (the "indomethacin test").

    It's under-diagnosed — that's why the clock matters

    Mean delay from onset to correct diagnosis is ~5–6 years; cluster is routinely mislabeled as migraine (~25%), tension-type (~19%), or sinusitis (~14%), and women are misdiagnosed more often than men (~61% vs 46%). If a patient describes strictly unilateral, autonomic, clockwork attacks with agitation — call it cluster and treat it, don't recycle a sinusitis diagnosis.

    🚩 Screen for depression and suicidality — the "suicide headache"

    Cluster carries a markedly elevated suicide risk — attempted suicide ~3.9× the general population (95% CI 2.6–5.8), independent of comorbid depression, and a 2025 cohort confirmed elevated attempted and completed suicide across headache disorders (strongest in ED/inpatient settings). The driver is demoralization during attacks, not classic depression. Ask every cluster patient about mood and suicidal ideation, and treat the headache aggressively — control is the intervention.

    Rapid approach¶

    1. Confirm strictly unilateral pain with ipsilateral autonomic features and a normal interictal exam.
    2. Image once (MRI, pituitary views) to exclude a secondary cause.
    3. Time the attacks → place them in the table above.
    4. Cluster: at the same visit, start all three — an abortive (O₂ + SC/nasal triptan), a transitional bridge, and a preventive. Clusters are too disabling to titrate slowly.
    5. Short-lasting + autonomic: run the indomethacin trial before anything else.

    Cluster headache — the prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)¶

    1. Confirm + image once (MRI with pituitary views).
    2. Abortive: high-flow oxygen + sumatriptan 6 mg SC (or nasal triptan) — script both, including a home oxygen setup.
    3. Bridge: a prednisone taper or a greater occipital nerve block to cover the gap until the preventive works.
    4. Preventive: verapamil (with the ECG protocol) — start the same day.
    5. Episodic cluster not controlled on verapamil: add galcanezumab 300 mg SC monthly.
    6. Refractory / chronic: neurology referral; lithium, topiramate, or combinations; occipital-nerve stimulation in specialist hands.

    Acute (abortive) treatment¶

    Step 1 — abort the attack (give oxygen AND a triptan; they're complementary)

    Both are Level A. Oral drugs are useless — attacks peak in minutes.

    • High-flow oxygen: 100% at 12–15 L/min via a true non-rebreather mask, 15–20 min, sitting forward. NNT ≈ 2. The commonest "oxygen failure" is a setup failure — a standard nasal cannula or a mask without a reservoir bag won't work. Insist on a non-rebreather. A demand-valve (or Optimask) system may beat a simple mask — patient-preferred (~62%) and it cut rescue-medication use in a crossover trial — so try a demand valve before declaring oxygen a failure.
    • Sumatriptan 6 mg SC — fastest and most effective (relief in ~15 min). Max 2 injections/24 h.
    • Zolmitriptan 5–10 mg nasal or sumatriptan 20 mg nasal — when SC isn't available or is declined (10 mg zolmitriptan NNT ≈ 2–3).
    • Non-invasive vagus nerve stimulation (gammaCore) — evidence-based adjunct/abortive for episodic cluster; useful when triptans are contraindicated or overused.

    Do not use for acute cluster

    Oral triptans, opioids, and simple analgesics — all too slow for an attack that peaks in minutes. Opioids additionally worsen the course.

    Transitional bridge (start with the preventive, same visit)¶

    Step 2 — bridge the gap while the preventive ramps up

    A preventive takes 1–2+ weeks to work; a bridge suppresses attacks now. Pick one:

    • Greater occipital nerve block (GON block) — suboccipital corticosteroid ± local anesthetic on the symptomatic side; RCT-supported and fast. First choice if you can do it.
    • Prednisone taper — e.g., 60–100 mg/day × 5 days, then taper over ~2–3 weeks. Widely used and effective as a bridge; not for chronic maintenance (steroid toxicity).

    Prevention¶

    Step 3 — verapamil first-line, with a mandatory ECG protocol

    • Verapamil — start 240 mg/day, titrate by ~80 mg every ~1–2 weeks to effect (commonly 360–480 mg/day; specialists go higher). Use it for the whole bout.
    • ECG protocol (non-negotiable): baseline ECG, then before each dose increase and ~10–14 days after, watching for PR prolongation, heart block, and bradycardia. Verapamil conduction effects are dose-dependent and can appear late.
    • Constipation is the dose-limiting nuisance — start a bowel regimen.
    • Galcanezumab (Emgality) 300 mg SC monthly — the only FDA-approved CGRP mAb for cluster, and only for episodic cluster (300 mg, not the 120 mg migraine dose). Give at the start of the bout and continue through it.
    • Lithium — effective (esp. chronic cluster); needs level, renal, and thyroid monitoring.
    • Topiramate — alternative/add-on.
    • Melatonin 10 mg qHS — reasonable adjunct, exploits the circadian biology.

    CGRP mAbs have failed in chronic cluster — don't count on them

    Every CGRP mAb RCT in chronic cluster has been negative — galcanezumab, erenumab (CHERUB01), eptinezumab (ALLEVIATE), and fremanezumab (episodic + chronic trials stopped for futility); a 2026 network meta-analysis found no significant reduction in weekly attacks for any mAb (numerical signals only, mostly in episodic). Open-label eptinezumab (CHRONICLE) looked tolerable but had no placebo control. For chronic cluster, rely on verapamil/lithium and refractory options — not a CGRP antibody.

    Clusters remit — plan for it

    In episodic cluster, stop the preventive once the bout ends and the patient is attack-free for ~2 weeks; restart at the first sign of the next bout. In chronic cluster, maintain continuously.

    Refractory chronic cluster (specialist territory)

    When verapamil ± lithium/topiramate and galcanezumab (episodic) fail — refer to a headache specialist:

    • Occipital nerve stimulation — the best-supported neuromodulation for refractory chronic cluster in specialist hands.
    • Sphenopalatine-ganglion (SPG) stimulation — showed RCT-level efficacy (≈62–67% pain relief at 15 min vs sham), but is currently unavailable — the device manufacturer left the market; new devices are in development, so know it as a future option, not a present one.
    • Emerging — psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD): small trials and strong patient interest; a pulse-dosing psilocybin RCT extension signaled reduced attack frequency, and phase-2 LSD trials are ongoing — but the evidence is underpowered and this is not yet a clinical recommendation. Many patients self-treat — ask about it (interactions, and it's a candid conversation worth having).

    Paroxysmal hemicrania · hemicrania continua · SUNCT/SUNA¶

    Short-lasting/continuous TACs — run the indomethacin trial first

    • Paroxysmal hemicrania & hemicrania continua → indomethacin trial. Uptitrate 25 mg TID → 50 mg TID (up to 75 mg TID / ~225 mg/day), each step held a few days, with GI protection (PPI). A complete response confirms the diagnosis and is the treatment; taper to the lowest effective dose. Consider "indotest" (IM indomethacin 50–100 mg) where the oral trial is ambiguous.
      • If indomethacin isn't tolerated (GI, renal, or neuropsychiatric effects — common): the best-supported alternatives (case-report level, no consensus guideline) are melatonin 6–21 mg/day, celecoxib (or other COX-2 inhibitor), acemetacin, topiramate, greater occipital nerve blocks (relief lasting months), and non-invasive vagus nerve stimulation as an indomethacin-sparing strategy.
    • SUNCT / SUNA — do not respond to indomethacin. Lamotrigine (titrate slowly for rash), then gabapentin, topiramate, or oxcarbazepine. IV lidocaine (monitored) for a status-like crisis; occipital-nerve procedures in refractory cases.

    Order set — quick reference¶

    Acute cluster attack (copy-paste)

    • Oxygen 100% at 12–15 L/min via non-rebreather × 15–20 min, sitting forward
    • Sumatriptan 6 mg SC (or zolmitriptan 5–10 mg nasal if SC unavailable)
    • Before discharge: start a GON block or prednisone bridge + verapamil (with baseline ECG), script home oxygen, and arrange neurology follow-up.

    TRICARE — cluster prescribing

    • Verapamil, prednisone, lithium, topiramate are generic/formulary, no PA.
    • Galcanezumab (Emgality) is the preferred, no-PA CGRP on TRICARE and carries the episodic-cluster indication — the frictionless CGRP choice here too (dose 300 mg for cluster).
    • Home oxygen requires a prescription documenting the cluster diagnosis and flow rate (12–15 L/min, non-rebreather); arrange the setup explicitly — coverage/logistics are the usual failure point, not efficacy. Verify current DME coverage with the regional contractor.

    Disposition¶

    Outpatient. The wins are diagnostic (don't miss an indomethacin-responsive TAC) and logistical (get the patient home oxygen and a verapamil-plus-ECG plan started the same day).

    Military medicine — cluster & TACs

    Standards below are quoted from DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1/Vol 2 and AR 40-501 (27 June 2019); the DES process is governed by DoDI 1332.18 / DoDM 1332.18. Confirm current editions and aeromedical policy.

    • Deployability: an active cluster bout is severely disabling and typically deployment-limiting — the regimen (home/therapeutic oxygen dependence, verapamil with serial ECGs, injectable triptans, refrigerated CGRP) is hard to sustain in theater. Between bouts, many are functional.
    • Profile (DA Form 3349): temporary profile during a bout; permanent profile for chronic cluster or refractory TACs with duty impact.
    • EPTS / LOD: document onset and periodicity.
    • Retention — DoD & Army: both the joint standard (DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2, para 5.26.g) and AR 40-501 para 3-31g explicitly name "migraine, tension, or cluster headaches" — frequent incapacitating attacks (Army: interfering with duty/social activities ≥3 days/ month) require neurologist referral, permit up to 6 months of prophylaxis, and mandate referral to the DES (MEB/PEB) if not controlled at 6 months. A cluster bout easily meets the frequency threshold.
    • Accession — DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1, para 6.26.g: a history of cluster headaches is disqualifying for entry.
    • Aeromedical / special duty: active cluster and its medications (verapamil, triptans, lithium, topiramate) are generally disqualifying/waiver-dependent, and a history of chronic/recurrent/incapacitating headache is disqualifying for flying duty (AR 40-501, ch. 4).

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    • The indomethacin-responsive headaches (PH, HC) are the ones you must not miss — a complete response is both treatment and diagnosis.
    • Cluster acute therapy = oxygen + subcutaneous/nasal triptan (both Level A); oral agents are too slow. Oxygen fails most often for setup reasons — insist on a non-rebreather.
    • Verapamil is first-line prevention and mandates ECG surveillance for dose-dependent conduction block.
    • CGRP mAbs work only for episodic cluster — and only galcanezumab (300 mg). In chronic cluster the class has uniformly failed in RCTs (galcanezumab, erenumab/CHERUB01, eptinezumab/ ALLEVIATE, fremanezumab; 2026 network meta-analysis negative) — don't rely on a mAb there.
    • Transition with a GON block or a short prednisone taper while the preventive ramps up.
    • Under-recognized and dangerous: ~5–6-year diagnostic delay, frequent misdiagnosis (more so in women), and a ~3.9× suicide-attempt risk — screen mood/SI and treat aggressively.
    • Refractory: occipital-nerve stimulation (best-supported); SPG stimulation is RCT-proven but currently unavailable (manufacturer exited); psilocybin/LSD are investigational — ask, because patients self-treat. For indomethacin-intolerant PH/HC: melatonin, celecoxib, topiramate, GON blocks.
    • Always image a new TAC once to exclude pituitary/structural mimics.

    Key sources: ICHD-3 (TAC criteria); AHS 2016 & EAN 2023 cluster guidelines; galcanezumab episodic-cluster trial; negative chronic-cluster mAb RCTs (CHERUB01/erenumab, ALLEVIATE/eptinezumab, fremanezumab) + 2026 network meta-analysis; cluster-headache suicide-risk cohorts; demand-valve-oxygen crossover trial; indomethacin-alternative and psilocybin cluster series.
