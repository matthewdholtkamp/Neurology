    Military medicine — profiles, deployability & the MEB/MAR2 decision¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: the shared reference behind the Military medicine box on every clinical page. It answers the question providers actually get stuck on — do I board this Soldier, and if so, when, and am I the one who starts it?

    Who this is for: any credentialed provider who writes profiles or sees Service members — neurology, primary care, PT/OT, behavioral health. You do not need to be an MEB physician to start this process. In the Army you usually are the person who starts it.

    Verify before you counsel or document

    Standards change and vary by Service and mission. Treat this as an orientation to the framework, not the authority. Always confirm specifics against the current governing publications for the member's Service and duty status — and remember that the Soldier in front of you may be Reserve or Guard, which changes the pathway.

    The 30-second version

    1. Has the condition reached MRDP? Clinically stable with no further progress expected, or 12 months on a temporary profile. If no → keep treating, extend the temporary profile (linked!), reassess. Do not refer yet.

    2. Does the Soldier meet AR 40-501 Chapter 3 retention standards?

    • No → DES referral. Write a permanent 3 or 4 profile in e-Profile. That is the referral.
    • Yes, but they can't perform their PMOS → MAR2, not a board.

    3. Can you act on the first visit? Often yes — by two different routes:

    • Epilepsy — DoDI 6130.03 5.26.k: act upon diagnosis. The "persist despite treatment" gate explicitly does not apply. You are not waiting for anything.
    • Headache — the gate does apply, but AR 40-501 3-31g lets the neurologist refer directly without finishing the 6-month prophylaxis trial if the Soldier is unlikely to respond. Your clinical judgment is the authority.

    4. Who signs? You write it. A physician approval authority second-signs a 3/4 — and that signature starts the clock. You do not need the commander's agreement.

    The two-regulation rule¶

    Use both, in this order. They do different jobs.

    DoDI 6130.03, Volume 2 AR 40-501, Chapter 3
    Scope Joint — all Services Army only
    Role The DoD floor: what kinds of conditions fail retention The Army's implementation: the actual number
    Neuro paragraph 5.26 (sleep = 5.27) 3-31 (sleep = 3-32)
    Example — headache 5.26.g: migraine/tension/cluster "when manifested by frequent incapacitating attacks" 3-31g: "incapacitating attacks that interfere with duty or social activities three or more days per month"

    The pattern to remember: DoD says "frequent." AR 40-501 supplies the number. Cite both in your documentation — the DoD paragraph establishes that the condition is disqualifying at all, and the Army paragraph proves the Soldier crossed the threshold.

    Naming

    Volume 2 Change 2 (6 Feb 2026) renders as "DoWI 6130.03-V2" following the October 2025 Department of War redesignation. It is the same instruction. Cite it as DoDI 6130.03 — that is what everyone still searches, and what AR 40-501 and DoDI 1332.18 reference.

    The one question that decides everything¶

    Before you think about boards at all, answer one question: does the Soldier still meet medical retention standards? Everything forks from there — and the two branches go to completely different places.

    Situation Where it goes Governing text
    Permanent profile 3 or 4, and the Soldier meets retention standards (AR 40-501 ch. 3) but may not be able to perform their PMOS MAR2 — an administrative review AR 40-501 glossary; AR 635-40
    Permanent profile 3 or 4, and the Soldier does not meet retention standards DES (MEB → PEB) — a disability process AR 40-501 3-3, 3-4
    Any permanent limitation in Section 4 (Functional Activities) of DA Form 3349-SG DES referral (or the RC medical disqualification process) AR 40-502
    Meets retention standards; permanent limits block all physical tasks for the MOS per DA Pam 611-21, but no functional-activity impairment MAR2 AR 40-502

    The decision ladder, in order:

    1. Is it an "upon diagnosis" condition? In an active-duty population that means epilepsy (DoDI 6130.03 5.26.k). → Yes: refer upon diagnosis. Skip to step 5. No: continue.
    2. Has treatment been adequate? (drug, dose, duration documented) → No: keep treating on a temporary profile with linked extensions. Reassess. Yes: continue.
    3. Has MRDP been reached? Either clinically stable with no further progress expected, or 12 months on a temporary profile for this condition. → No: return to step 2. Yes: continue.
    4. Does the Soldier meet AR 40-501 Chapter 3 retention standards?
      • No → step 5.
      • Yes, but cannot perform their PMOS → MAR2. If MAR2 cannot retain or reclassify them, the case comes back here as a DES referral.
      • Yes, and can perform their PMOS → permanent profile with duty limits. No board.
    5. Write the permanent 3 or 4 profile in e-Profile. This is the referral.
    6. Approval authority (physician) second-signs. The clock starts here — MEB 72 days, PEB 82 days, transition 26 days.

    MAR2 (Military Occupational Specialty Administrative Retention Review) decides whether the Soldier stays in their PMOS or reclassifies into a new one. It replaced the MOS Medical Retention Board. Soldiers who cannot perform their PMOS and do not qualify for reclassification get referred onward to the DES.

    Do not confuse the two

    MAR2 is not a mini-MEB and it is not a disability process. Sending a Soldier who fails retention standards to MAR2 delays a board they are entitled to. Sending a Soldier who meets retention standards to the DES gets the packet bounced back to you. The MEB physician's job explicitly includes "help discern MAR2 versus DES referrals" (DA Pam 40-502) — ask them when you are genuinely unsure, but know the fork yourself.

    The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)¶

    1. Diagnose and treat adequately. For most conditions the disqualifying standard only applies after appropriate treatment has failed — that is the explicit gate in DoDI 6130.03 5.26.a. Document the drug, dose, and duration of every trial.
    2. Ask whether the gate even applies. For epilepsy it does not — refer upon diagnosis. For headache, check whether you can invoke the neurologist's direct-referral authority under 3-31g instead of running the full 6-month trial. Both are covered below.
    3. Determine whether the Soldier has reached MRDP — the Medical Retention Determination Point. Either the condition has clinically stabilized and no further functional progress is expected, or the administrative 12-month clock has run out.
    4. Compare against AR 40-501 Chapter 3 with the DoDI 6130.03 paragraph beside it. Does the Soldier meet retention standards, yes or no?
    5. Write the permanent profile in e-Profile with the correct PULHES designator and — this is the part providers skip — a specific, functional description of the limitation in Section 4.
    6. Route for second signature. Permanent 3/4 requires an approval authority (a physician). That signature is what actually starts the board.
    7. Counsel the Soldier on what happens next and roughly how long it takes.

    Step 1 → the profile is the referral

    The single most common misunderstanding: providers think an MEB is a separate packet they have to assemble and submit.

    It is not. In the Army, the mechanism for referring a Soldier to the DES is the permanent 3 or 4 profile in e-Profile. AR 40-501 3-4 states that the profiling officer and approval authority refer Soldiers to the DES "by submitting a permanent profile with a 3 or 4 designation for conditions that have met an MRDP, annotated in e-Profile."

    Write the profile correctly and the referral has happened.

    Who can initiate — and whose signature starts the clock¶

    Role Who What they do
    Profiling officer / profiling provider Any credentialed provider authorized to profile — including a neurologist seeing the Soldier in consultation Writes the profile, assesses retention standards, describes functional limits
    Second signature (permanent "2") A physician, and must not be the same person as the first signature Reviews and concurs
    Approval authority (permanent "3" or "4") Must be a physician, designated by the MTF commander, ARNG chief/state surgeon, or USAR command surgeon (USAR RSC surgeons hold it by position) Their signature initiates the administrative review or boarding action
    MEB physician Designated MEB approval authority Reviews every e-Profile MEB referral before signing: confirms MRDP was reached, bounces inappropriate referrals back, and discerns MAR2 vs DES
    PEBLO Assigned within 3 days of referral The Soldier's guide through the entire process
    Unit commander Reviews every profile in the unit; may raise concerns with the profiling officer and request a fitness-for-duty evaluation including a second opinion from another profiling officer — but cannot initiate a DES referral, and cannot block one

    The load-bearing sentence, from AR 40-502: "The approval authority ensures that profiles are appropriate for entry into DES; the signature of the approval authority initiates the administrative review or boarding action."

    DA Pam 40-502 adds the timeline consequence: "the application of the second signature for a permanent profile that does not meet retention standards initiates the requirement for DES... For AD Soldiers whose condition does not meet retention standards, the second signature on the permanent profile also initiates the DES timeline." For Reserve COMPOs 2 and 3, the clock instead starts when the MEB packet reaches the MEB Tracking Office.

    So — can a neurologist initiate?

    Yes. A neurologist seeing a Soldier in consultation is a profiling provider and can write the permanent profile that constitutes the DES referral. You will generally need an approval authority's second signature for a 3/4, but you do not need the Soldier's PCM's permission, and you do not need the commander's agreement. For headache and epilepsy specifically, AR 40-501 names the neurologist as the deciding clinician by title.

    When is a board required, when may you, and when should you not¶

    REQUIRED — you must refer

    • The Soldier has a Chapter 3 disqualifying condition and has reached MRDP. AR 40-501 3-4: Soldiers meeting the para 3-1 definition "will be referred to DES."
    • A temporary profile for the same condition has run 12 months. The Soldier is then administratively defined to have reached MRDP — no further clinical judgment required.
    • Any permanent limitation in Section 4 (Functional Activities) of DA Form 3349-SG. For active-duty Soldiers this means a disability evaluation referral; for RC Soldiers it may instead initiate the medical disqualification process, depending on duty status (AR 40-502).
    • Epilepsy — DoDI 6130.03 5.26.k: not compatible with retention, act upon diagnosis.
    • Headache at ≥3 incapacitating days/month not adequately controlled after a 6-month prophylaxis trial — AR 40-501 3-31g. (You may also refer before the 6 months are up — see the MAY box.)
    • Seizures recurring beyond 6 months after treatment was initiated — AR 40-501 3-31i(4): P-3 profile and DES referral.
    • Chronic insomnia not responding to CBT-I, or requiring sleep medication ≥3 nights/week, over 6 consecutive months — AR 40-501 3-32a.
    • Dementia — DoDI 6130.03 5.26.d: also upon diagnosis. Rare in an active-duty population, but it is the only other condition in 5.26 where the gate is waived.

    Do not wait for the other conditions to ripen

    DA Pam 40-502: "Once MRDP is met for one condition which does not meet retention standards, referral into the DES must commence regardless of the status of other co-existing conditions." Refer on the first qualifying condition. Others get added to the case file later — they do not need to be resolved first.

    MAY — clinical judgment, and you are allowed to act early

    These are the situations where AR 40-501 explicitly hands the decision to the specialist:

    • Headache, before the 6-month trial is over. 3-31g: "If the neurologist feels the Soldier is unlikely to respond to therapy, the Soldier can be referred directly to the DES."
    • Suspected epilepsy. 3-31i(1): the neurologist determines whether epilepsy exists and whether the Soldier gets a trial of therapy on active duty or is referred directly to the DES — weighing underlying cause, EEG, seizure type, duration, likelihood of compliance, substance use, and any other factor bearing on control or duty performance.
    • MRDP reached ahead of the administrative clock. DA Pam 40-502: "MRDP may occur before the 12-month administrative timeline if the condition is stable and no further functional progress is expected." You do not have to burn 12 months to prove a point.
    • TBI with residual impairment after adequate treatment — AR 40-501 3-31j.
    • A Soldier previously found fit who now has a deployment-limiting condition may go back through MAR2 for a second review (AR 40-501 3-3g).

    DO NOT — these get bounced, and they harm the Soldier

    • Before MRDP. The MEB physician's stated job is to confirm MRDP "has been achieved prior to initiating referral into the DES" and to route premature referrals back to you.
    • Conditions known and waived at accession that have stayed essentially the same in severity and do not meet the para 3-1 definition — AR 40-501 3-3c bars referral.
    • A Soldier who meets retention standards. That is a MAR2, not a DES referral.
    • Deferring because a non-disability separation or retirement is pending. AR 40-501 3-3d is explicit: profiling providers "should not defer initiating a DES referral until the Soldier is being processed for non-disability retirement."
    • Stacking unlinked temporary profiles. Creating a fresh temporary condition instead of linking the extension resets the days-on-profile count, hides the Soldier's progress from the commander, and delays the Soldier's access to the DES. Always link extensions to the original profile.

    Neurology conditions — the paragraph and the gate¶

    Both citations belong in your note. The gate column is what determines whether you can act today.

    Condition DoDI 6130.03 V2 AR 40-501 The gate
    Migraine / tension / cluster headache 5.26.g — "frequent incapacitating attacks" 3-31g ≥3 incapacitating days/month → mandatory neurologist referral; neurologist may trial prophylaxis up to 6 months; not controlled at 6 months → DES. May refer directly if unlikely to respond.
    Epilepsy 5.26.k — not compatible with retention; act upon diagnosis. 5.26.a does not apply. 3-31i Neurologist decides: trial of therapy on AD or direct DES referral. Seizure recurrence >6 months after starting treatment → P-3 + DES.
    Provoked seizures 5.26.j 3-31i Recurrent >6 months after treatment starts and medication effects impair duty, require significant follow-up, or require safety modifications
    Stroke / cerebrovascular 5.26.b (incl. SAH/ICH, stenosis, aneurysm, AVM, recurrent TIA) 3-31f Stroke: "when residuals affect performance." Recurrent TIA unless etiology identified and definitively treated
    TBI with persistent sequelae 5.26.h 3-31j After adequate treatment, residual symptoms/impairment meeting the para 3-1 definition → referral required
    Demyelinating disease / MS 5.26.f 3-31e Chronic or recurrent process (MS, transverse myelitis, recurrent optic neuritis). See also NMOSD and MOGAD — MOGAD is often monophasic and may retain
    Dementia (rare in AD) 5.26.d — not compatible with retention; act upon diagnosis. 5.26.a does not apply. 3-31k None — upon diagnosis
    Myasthenia gravis 5.26.l 3-31c Disqualifying unless confined to extraocular muscles
    ALS / progressive neurogenic atrophy (5.26.i, paralytic disorders) 3-31a Referral to DES
    Parkinson's / basal ganglia & cerebellar degeneration 5.26.m (tremor/dystonia with functional impairment) 3-31d Progressive degenerative disorders
    Muscular dystrophies 5.26.e 3-31b Progressive weakness and atrophy
    Peripheral neuropathy 5.26.i 3-31k Permanent functional impairment
    Narcolepsy 5.27.a 3-31h (see 3-32c) Neurologist or sleep specialist evaluation required to determine disposition
    Chronic insomnia 5.27.a 3-32a Fails CBT-I, or needs sleep medication ≥3 nights/week, over 6 consecutive months
    Binocular diplopia 5.4.e 3-7b Not surgically correctable, severe, constant, within 20° of primary position
    Recurrent/unexplained syncope 5.26.n 3-31k Interferes with duty

    The 5.26.a gate, stated once

    Everything in DoDI 6130.03 para 5.26 except epilepsy (k) and dementia (d) — and in an active-duty population that effectively means except epilepsy — is gated by 5.26.a: the condition "must persist despite appropriate treatment and impair function so as to preclude satisfactory performance of required military duties." Both halves are required — persistence and duty impairment. A well-controlled condition does not fail retention standards no matter how serious the diagnosis sounds, and an untreated one has not yet met the standard either.

    "Is 180 days a mandatory MEB?"¶

    No. This is the most common misconception in this space, and it comes from three real but unrelated numbers that all sit near six months.

    What people mean by "180 days" What it actually is
    A mandatory MEB trigger ❌ Does not exist. The mandatory administrative point is 365 days / 12 months of temporary profile for the same condition.
    180 days The threshold at which BDE (O-6) commanders must review the temporary profile monthly. A command-visibility rule, not a medical one.
    6 months of temporary profile Triggers referral to a physician or specialist for that condition (DA Pam 40-502) — the point at which someone like you gets consulted.
    180 days The IDES completion goal — DoD's target for finishing 80% of cases after referral. A performance metric, not a trigger.

    The genuinely mandatory six-month rules in neurology are condition-specific clinical gates, not a global clock: headache prophylaxis failure at 6 months (3-31g), seizure recurrence beyond 6 months after starting treatment (3-31i(4)), and insomnia refractory over 6 consecutive months (3-32a).

    The number to hold onto is 12 months. At 12 months on a temporary profile for the same condition, the Soldier has reached MRDP by definition. Exceptions are deliberately rare and require approval by the first general officer in the Soldier's chain of command, in consultation with the senior approval authority and senior medical officer — plus a written treatment plan, an explanation of why no board has been started, and an expected MRDP date.

    The clock: what actually happens, and when¶

    Point What happens Authority
    1–7 days DD Form 689 territory — short, self-limited illness. No profile. DA Pam 40-502
    >3 days of functional limitation Goes into e-Profile as a temporary profile DA Pam 40-502
    Up to 90 days Maximum length a single temporary profile may be written for DA Pam 40-502 4-4c(1)
    Every 3 months Updated functional assessment required, and required before any extension DA Pam 40-502
    120 days Battalion/squadron (O-5) commanders review monthly DA Pam 40-502 4-4c(3)
    180 days / 6 months BDE (O-6) monthly review + referral to a physician or specialist for that condition DA Pam 40-502
    240 days Senior general officer (above BDE / installation) review DA Pam 40-502
    365 days / 12 months Administrative MRDP. Maximum temporary profile duration. Transition to permanent profile; DES or MAR2 as applicable. AR 40-501 3-1c; DA Pam 40-502 4-4c(4)
    Any time MRDP is reached clinically Transition to permanent profile — do not wait for 12 months DA Pam 40-502 4-4c(1)

    What happens after you sign: the IDES timeline¶

    Once your referral lands, the case runs on published DoD goals. The overall target is to complete 80% of cases in no more than 180 days, measured from the date of referral to the date of return to duty, separation, or retirement (DoDM 1332.18 Vol 1, Section 6).

    Phase Goal Stages
    MEB Phase 72 days Referral (7d) → Claim Development (7d) → VA Disability Examination (31d) → MEB (20d) → IMR/MEB Rebuttal (7d)
    PEB Phase 82 days IPEB (11d) → Proposed Ratings (19d, concurrent) → Proposed Disposition (5d) → Elections (6d) → FPEB (24d) → FPEB Appeal (10d) → Rating Reconsideration (2d) → Final Disposition (5d)
    Transition 26 days Separation or retirement processing

    What the boards actually decide:

    • MEB — documents the conditions and determines whether the Soldier meets medical retention standards. If they do, the case can end there. If not, the case file goes to the PEB. The MEB decides medical, not fitness. Any MEB listing a psychiatric diagnosis must include a thorough psychiatric evaluation and the signature of at least one psychiatrist or doctoral-level psychologist.
    • PEB (IPEB, then FPEB on demand) — determines fitness for continued service and, if unfit, the disability disposition and rating.
    • IDES — the integrated pathway that runs the DoD process alongside the VA examination and proposed rating, so the Soldier leaves with a VA rating already in hand.

    A referral is not a discharge — say this to the Soldier

    Soldiers frequently hear "MEB" as "you're being kicked out," and that fear drives them to under-report symptoms to you. It is worth correcting explicitly.

    AR 40-501 3-4 opens by saying that possession of a disqualifying condition "does not mean automatic retirement or separation from the Service." The MEB only determines whether retention standards are met; the PEB decides fitness. A Soldier found unfit may still be continued on active duty (COAD) or continued on active reserve (COAR) under AR 635-40, or granted permanent limited duty or limited assignment status. And because this runs through the IDES, a Soldier who does separate leaves with a VA rating already adjudicated rather than starting a claim from scratch months later.

    Referring early and accurately serves the Soldier. Sitting on a referral does not.

    Time that doesn't count against the clock

    Excludable: administrative absences and accrued leave, cases pending non-disability administrative action, additional recovery or treatment time, continuation on active duty or limited duty for under 12 months, and awaiting SecDef concurrence for flag/general/medical officer cases. Not excludable: delays in gathering documentation you were responsible for (LOD determinations, DBQs, the commander's letter). That last one is squarely on the clinical side — a slow LOD or a missing commander's letter is a delay the DES is not allowed to write off.

    The five questions (the Military box)¶

    Every clinical page on this site answers these in order:

    1. Deployability — is the condition, or its required treatment/monitoring, compatible with the operational environment? Consider medications needing refrigeration, lab monitoring, or controlled-substance accountability, and anything impairing heat tolerance, alertness, or judgment. The baseline expectation is that a Soldier can deploy within 72 hours of receipt of mission (AR 40-501 3-1).
    2. Profile (DA Form 3349) — temporary or permanent? What PULHES designator, and what are the specific functional limits in Section 4?
    3. EPTS / Line of Duty — existed prior to service, or incurred/aggravated in the line of duty? Note the 6-month rule: for conditions predating enlistment, accession standards apply for the first 6 months of active duty (separation via EPSBD); after 6 months, retention standards govern.
    4. Retention (DoDI 6130.03 + AR 40-501) — does severity, frequency, or treatment response cross a threshold that ends retention?
    5. MEB/MAR2 referral — which one, and is it required or discretionary right now?

    Documentation that carries a board¶

    Boards are moved by objective, longitudinal, duty-relevant evidence. In practice:

    • Frequency and severity captured over time — headache diaries, seizure logs, encounter and ER records. A board cannot act on "frequent." It can act on "≥3 incapacitating days per month documented across 7 months of diary entries."
    • Adequate treatment trials that failed — name the drug, dose, and duration for each. An inadequate trial reads as an untreated condition, and 5.26.a will not be satisfied.
    • Specific duty impact — missed training, failed or deferred ACFT, non-deployability, work restrictions, safety limitations. Corroborate with the commander where possible; AR 40-501 3-1a expects duty interference "as substantiated by the Soldier's commander or supervisor."
    • Functional limitations written functionally. Section 4 of DA Form 3349-SG drives the fork between MAR2 and DES. "Migraines" is not a limitation. "Cannot wear protective headgear >2 hours; cannot perform sustained overhead work; no duty requiring uninterrupted alertness" is.
    • A clear EPTS/LOD determination and consistent, linked profiles that show the real days-on-profile count.
