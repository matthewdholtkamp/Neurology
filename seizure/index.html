    Seizure & status epilepticus¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: a seizure is sudden, hypersynchronous cortical firing; status epilepticus is the neurologic code — treat it on a clock, escalate on schedule, and don't under-dose the benzo (the single most common mistake). This page covers the code, the first-seizure work-up, and picking an antiseizure medication (ASM).

    The ER consult usually sounds like: "Known-or-unknown epileptic, two witnessed seizures, got 4 of Ativan, now not responding — should I load them with something, and you're admitting, right?" The answer to "load them?" is yes, now — and check whether that "not responding" is postictal or ongoing nonconvulsive status.

    🚩 Status epilepticus — recognize it and start the clock

    • Definition (ILAE two-timepoint): treat at t1 (the seizure won't self-terminate) — 5 min for a generalized tonic-clonic seizure, but 10 min for focal SE with impaired awareness and 10–15 min for absence; or ≥2 seizures without recovery between them. t2 (risk of long-term injury) is ~30 min for GTC. Bottom line: GTC at 5 min = treat now, and don't wait 30 minutes for anything.
    • "Stopped but won't wake up" = nonconvulsive status until proven otherwise — get urgent cEEG. Persistent altered consciousness after visible convulsions stop is NCSE in up to ~20%.
    • Under-dosing benzodiazepines (and then blaming the "refractory" seizure) is the classic error — give the full weight-based dose.
    • Find and fix the cause in parallel — a seizure from hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, a toxin, or eclampsia won't stop on ASMs until the cause is corrected.

    Status epilepticus — the timed escalation ladder (the code)¶

    Step 0 — Stabilize (0–5 min)

    • ABCs, O₂, position, suction; IV access ×2; continuous ECG, BP, SpO₂.
    • Point-of-care glucose — if low, D50W 50 mL IV + thiamine 100 mg IV (thiamine first/with glucose in the malnourished/alcohol-use patient).
    • STAT labs: CBC, BMP, Ca, Mg, glucose, ASM levels, ± LFTs, tox screen (blood + urine), ABG, β-hCG, PO₄; ECG.

    Step 1 — First-line benzodiazepine (5–20 min) — give the FULL dose

    Pick by access; these are frequently under-dosed — use the real weight-based dose and repeat once if still seizing.

    • IV lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg/dose), may repeat ×1 — first choice if IV is in.
    • No IV? IM midazolam 10 mg (>40 kg) — RAMPART showed IM midazolam is at least as good as IV lorazepam and faster to give; also intranasal/buccal midazolam.
    • IV diazepam 0.15–0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg), or rectal diazepam 0.2 mg/kg if no IV.

    Step 2 — Second-line ASM (20–40 min) — the three are EQUIVALENT (ESETT)

    ESETT (NEJM 2019) found levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, and valproate stop ~45–47% of benzo-refractory status — no winner. So pick by the patient, give the full load IV:

    • Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg) — fewest interactions/contraindications; a good default (esp. cardiac/hepatic disease, pregnancy).
    • Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV (≤150 mg PE/min) — avoid in hypotension/arrhythmia; watch the BP and QT.
    • Valproate 40 mg/kg IV (may add 20 mg/kg) — avoid in hepatic disease and pregnancy/childbearing potential.
    • (Phenobarbital 15 mg/kg IV if none of the above is available.)
    • Broadening landscape: IV lacosamide and brivaracetam are increasingly used as second-line (lacosamide was non-inferior to fosphenytoin for nonconvulsive seizures in TRENdS, with a favorable hemodynamic profile) — reasonable alternatives, though the formal evidence base still rests on the ESETT triad.
    • Continue the second-line agent as you escalate; arrange cEEG.

    Step 3 — Refractory status (>40 min, or 2 lines failed) — anesthetize + cEEG

    Intubate, admit to ICU, start continuous cEEG, and begin a continuous anesthetic infusion. Target seizure suppression, not routine burst-suppression — induced burst suppression wasn't associated with better outcomes (Fisch 2023), and seizure suppression alone tracked with better function; reserve burst-suppression for seizures recurring despite adequate anesthetic dosing.

    • Midazolam — load 0.2 mg/kg, then 0.05–2.9 mg/kg/h.
    • Propofol — load ~1–2 mg/kg, then 1–15 mg/kg/h (watch propofol-infusion syndrome — avoid in suspected mitochondrial disease or on a ketogenic diet; limit dose/duration).
    • Ketamine — increasingly used (NMDA-mediated late status), usually with a GABAergic agent.
    • Keep the second-line ASM on board for maintenance.

    Step 4 — Super-refractory (>24 h, or recurs on weaning)

    • Pentobarbital/thiopental infusion (load 5 mg/kg → titrate; hypotension is expected — pressors ready), titrated to seizure/burst suppression.
    • Hunt hard for the cause — autoimmune/paraneoplastic (NORSE/FIRES), CNS infection, PRES/RCVS, toxic-metabolic; consider immunotherapy (steroids/IVIg/PLEX) empirically in NORSE.
    • Ketogenic diet is used adjunctively in super-refractory SE (especially FIRES/NORSE).
    • Don't reach for unproven add-ons: hypothermia adds nothing beyond standard therapy, and the allopregnanolone (brexanolone) RCT was negative — no proven pharmacologic agent exists for super-refractory SE beyond anesthetics (+ immunotherapy for autoimmune causes).
    • Wean anesthetics slowly once seizure-free ~24–48 h; neurology + epileptology on board.

    Copy-paste — first 20 minutes

    • Glucose now; D50 + thiamine if low.
    • Lorazepam 4 mg IV (or midazolam 10 mg IM if no IV) — repeat ×1 if seizing at ~5 min.
    • Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV (or fosphenytoin/valproate by patient) as the seizure continues.
    • cEEG, ICU, and treat the cause. No sub-therapeutic benzos, no delay to second-line.

    Toxin-driven seizures — the antidote matters

    • INH (isoniazid): benzodiazepine + pyridoxine (vitamin B6) ~70 mg/kg, max 5 g.
    • Tricyclic antidepressants: wide QRS → sodium bicarbonate.
    • Organophosphates: atropine + pralidoxime (+ benzodiazepine).
    • Alcohol/benzo withdrawal: escalating benzodiazepines. Eclampsia: magnesium (+ deliver).

    Is it actually a seizure? (mimics)¶

    Before you commit to lifelong ASMs, exclude the imitators — syncope (convulsive syncope after a faint; get an ECG/cardiac workup — cardiac syncope can kill), psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES), TIA, transient global amnesia, migraine, and sleep disorders.

    • PNES tells (not diagnostic alone; confirm with video-EEG): side-to-side head shaking, eye closure (epileptic seizures usually have eyes open), pelvic thrusting, asynchronous flailing, a waxing–waning course, prolonged duration, crying/stuttering, preserved awareness despite bilateral movements, and resistance to eye opening. Manage with diagnosis delivery + behavioral health, not more ASMs.

    First seizure — work-up & whether to treat¶

    First unprovoked seizure — the work-up (outpatient is OK if back to baseline, no deficits, low infection concern)

    • Labs: CBC, BMP, Ca/Mg, glucose, LFTs, tox, ± ESR/CRP; β-hCG.
    • Imaging: MRI brain (with & without contrast) is the study of choice; CT in the ED (and before LP) to exclude a bleed/mass.
    • EEG — best within 48 h (raises yield); urgent if consciousness stays altered.
    • LP only if infection/SAH suspected.

    Start an ASM after a first seizure? — individualize (AAN/AES 2015)

    Immediate ASM reduces recurrence over the first 2 years but does NOT improve long-term remission or quality of life. Recurrence after a first unprovoked seizure is ~21–45% at 2 years.

    • Treat now if recurrence risk is high: a prior brain insult (stroke/tumor/trauma), an epileptiform EEG, a relevant lesion on MRI, or a nocturnal seizure.
    • Reasonable to wait in a low-risk, normal-work-up first seizure — with a shared decision (weigh ASM adverse effects, ~7–31%).
    • An acute provoked (symptomatic) seizure — fix the provocation; long-term ASM usually isn't needed.

    Picking an ASM (by seizure type & the patient)¶

    Match the drug to the seizure and the person

    SANAD II reset the defaults (Lancet 2021): lamotrigine is first-line for focal epilepsy (beat levetiracetam on treatment failure, tolerability, and cost), and valproate is the most effective for generalized epilepsy (with the childbearing caveat). Levetiracetam is the alternative when you need rapid titration (lamotrigine must be titrated slowly for rash) — e.g., the ED first seizure of unknown type.

    Situation Preferred → alternative
    Focal (most adults) Lamotrigine (best tolerated/cost) → levetiracetam (when rapid titration needed) or lacosamide; oxcarbazepine/carbamazepine
    Generalized (GTC) Valproate (most effective) → lamotrigine or levetiracetam (valproate off the table in childbearing potential)
    Absence Ethosuximide, valproate, lamotrigine
    Myoclonic (e.g., JME) Valproate, levetiracetam (avoid carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine/phenytoin — worsen myoclonus/absence)
    Childbearing potential Lamotrigine or levetiracetam — avoid valproate (neural-tube defects, low IQ)
    Older adults Lamotrigine or levetiracetam (fewer interactions, better tolerated)
    Comorbid depression/mood Lamotrigine (avoid levetiracetam's irritability); renal → lamotrigine; hepatic → levetiracetam/gabapentin
    • Screen HLA-B*15:02 before carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine in at-risk (Southeast/East Asian) ancestry (SJS/TEN); some guidelines also screen HLA-A*31:01 (European/Japanese ancestry — DRESS/SJS).

    Drug-resistant focal epilepsy — cenobamate is the standout newer ASM

    Among the newer adjuncts, cenobamate gives the best chance of seizure freedom (~32% at 12 months; out-performs brivaracetam, lacosamide, and perampanel on 50%-responder rate even after many failed ASMs). The trade-offs: titrate slowly (DRESS risk with fast titration) and a higher overall adverse-effect rate. Worth it for refractory focal epilepsy.

    • Refractory (failed 2 appropriate ASMs) = drug-resistant epilepsy → refer to an epilepsy center. The 2022 ILAE surgical-referral consensus is emphatic: offer a surgical evaluation to every drug-resistant patient up to age 70, regardless of epilepsy duration, seizure type, localization, or comorbidity (including psychiatric disease or comorbid PNES) — plus consider VNS/RNS/DBS and video-EEG.

    Classify it (ILAE 2017)¶

    Three levels: seizure type (focal / generalized / unknown onset; aware vs impaired awareness; motor vs non-motor) → epilepsy type (focal / generalized / combined / unknown) → epilepsy syndrome. Getting the type right drives ASM choice (e.g., don't give a sodium-channel blocker in a generalized/myoclonic syndrome).

    Counseling, rescue plan & driving¶

    • Driving is restricted after a seizure — the seizure-free interval is state/country-specific (commonly ~3–12 months; 6 months is typical); some states have mandatory physician reporting. Document the counseling.
    • Safety: no swimming/bathing alone, heights, or operating dangerous machinery until controlled; sleep, alcohol moderation, adherence.

    Give a seizure action plan with a rescue medication (for cluster-prone patients)

    Prescribe an outpatient rescue benzodiazepine and a written action plan — standard of care for epilepsy at risk of clusters/prolonged seizures. FDA-approved options:

    • Midazolam nasal spray (Nayzilam) — age ≥12.
    • Diazepam nasal spray (Valtoco) — age ≥6.
    • Rectal diazepam gel (Diastat) — age ≥2.

    SUDEP — discuss it early, not just when uncontrolled

    Sudden unexpected death in epilepsy is ~1/1,000 adults with epilepsy per year, rising to ~6–9/ 1,000 in drug-resistant epilepsy. Per the ILAE SUDEP task force, offer SUDEP communication to every patient at diagnosis and revisit it periodically (not only the uncontrolled). Modifiable risk factors: uncontrolled generalized tonic-clonic seizures (the biggest), nocturnal seizures, medication non-adherence, and lack of nocturnal supervision — target each.

    Disposition¶

    Status epilepticus → ICU with cEEG. A first seizure back to baseline with a reassuring work-up can be discharged with urgent neurology follow-up, safety/driving counseling, and a decision on ASM. Admit for a prolonged postictal state, an unremitting/nonconvulsive picture, a concerning cause, or an unsafe home situation.

    TRICARE — seizure prescribing

    Levetiracetam, lamotrigine, valproate, phenytoin, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, phenobarbital, gabapentin are generic/formulary, no PA — the whole core ladder is low-friction. Newer agents (brivaracetam, lacosamide, cenobamate, perampanel) are typically PA-gated (step therapy / specialist). Emergency IV agents are inpatient-stocked. Verify at the TRICARE Formulary Search.

    Military medicine — seizure / epilepsy

    Framework — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 6130.03, DoDI 1332.18, and aeromedical policy before counseling.

    • Retention (Army): AR 40-501 para 3-31i addresses seizure disorders/epilepsy — a diagnosis of epilepsy (recurrent unprovoked seizures), or seizures not controlled off medication, generally falls below retention → DES (MEB/PEB). The joint floor is DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.c (CNS conditions with persistent sequelae, incl. seizures). Document seizure type, frequency, provoking factors, work-up, ASM trials, and control.
    • Accession: a history of epilepsy/recurrent seizures is disqualifying for entry (DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1) — waivers hinge on a documented seizure-free, medication-free interval.
    • Deployability: active epilepsy, ongoing ASMs, and the need for lab/level monitoring and reliable sleep are deployment-limiting. A provoked seizure with a corrected cause may return to duty per neurology review.
    • Aeromedical / special duty: a seizure history (and most ASMs) is disqualifying for flying and many special-duty positions — waiver-dependent; confirm the standard for the class.

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    • Treat status on a clock, not on a hunch. The AES 2016 algorithm: stabilize (0–5 min) → full-dose benzo (5–20 min) → second-line ASM (20–40 min) → anesthetize + cEEG (>40 min).
    • The second-line three are equivalent (ESETT 2019) — levetiracetam ≈ fosphenytoin ≈ valproate (~45–47% each); choose by comorbidity, and give the full weight-based load. Levetiracetam is the cleanest default.
    • Benzos are under-dosed — that's the commonest "refractory status" that wasn't. RAMPART: IM midazolam ≥ IV lorazepam when there's no IV.
    • "Won't wake up" → cEEG for nonconvulsive status.
    • First seizure: immediate ASM cuts early recurrence but not long-term remission (AAN/AES 2015) — treat when recurrence risk is high (lesion, epileptiform EEG, nocturnal), otherwise a shared decision.
    • Second-line is broadening — lacosamide (non-inferior to fosphenytoin, TRENdS; kinder hemodynamics) and brivaracetam are increasingly used alongside the ESETT triad.
    • Refractory SE: target seizure suppression, not routine burst-suppression (Fisch 2023); no proven drug beyond anesthetics (allopregnanolone RCT negative; hypothermia adds nothing); ketogenic diet in FIRES/NORSE.
    • SANAD II reset the maintenance defaults — lamotrigine first-line for focal epilepsy, valproate most effective for generalized (levetiracetam is the rapid-titration alternative). For drug-resistant focal epilepsy, cenobamate gives the best seizure-freedom odds (slow titration, DRESS risk).
    • Two failed ASMs = drug-resistant → refer for surgical evaluation — the 2022 ILAE consensus: offer it to every such patient up to 70, regardless of type/duration/comorbidity.
    • Give a rescue plan (Nayzilam/Valtoco/Diastat) and discuss SUDEP at diagnosis (biggest modifiable factor: uncontrolled GTC seizures).

(burst-suppression, Neurology 2023); AAN/AES 2015 first-seizure guideline; SANAD II (Marson, Lancet 2021); cenobamate comparative-effectiveness (JAMA Neurol 2026); ILAE 2022 surgical-referral consensus; ILAE SUDEP task force (Epilepsia 2026); ILAE 2017 classification.
