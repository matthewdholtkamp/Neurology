    Nerve agent & organophosphate poisoning¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: poisoning of the synaptic enzyme — acetylcholinesterase inhibition floods every cholinergic synapse with acetylcholine, producing a cholinergic crisis: muscarinic secretions, nicotinic paralysis, and CNS seizures. Death is by respiratory failure. The antidotes are atropine and pralidoxime, and decontamination protects everyone else.

    Who this is for: the provider facing a cholinergic-crisis patient — nerve agent (sarin, VX, Novichok) in a CBRN or terrorism setting, or the far commoner organophosphate/carbamate pesticide poisoning. Same mechanism, same antidotes.

    🚩 Recognize the cholinergic toxidrome — and protect yourself first

    Two mnemonics, both worth knowing:

    • Muscarinic — DUMBELS: Defecation, Urination, Miosis, Bronchorrhea/Bronchospasm, Emesis, Lacrimation, Salivation. Bronchorrhea and bronchospasm are what kill — the patient drowns in secretions.
    • Nicotinic — "days of the week" MTWtHF: Mydriasis (can occur), Tachycardia, Weakness, Twitching/fasciculations, Hypertension, Fasciculations. Progresses to flaccid paralysis, including the diaphragm.
    • CNS: agitation, seizures, coma.

    The classic bedside signal is pinpoint pupils + copious secretions + fasciculations in a collapsing patient.

    Decontaminate before you treat — this is a contaminated patient

    Nerve agents and OPs are absorbed through skin and off-gas from clothing and vomit. A provider who treats an undecontaminated patient becomes the next casualty.

    • Don appropriate PPE. Remove all clothing (removes the majority of contaminant). Decontaminate skin (copious water; the RSDL kit for military nerve-agent decon).
    • This is a mass-casualty pattern — one index case with a cholinergic toxidrome and no trauma should raise a release/exposure event and trigger the CBRN chain.

    Mechanism in one line¶

    Acetylcholinesterase normally clears acetylcholine from the synapse. Inhibit it, and ACh piles up everywhere — at muscarinic (parasympathetic), nicotinic (neuromuscular junction and autonomic ganglia), and central synapses. This is the mechanistic opposite of myasthenia gravis (too little cholinergic transmission) — and, tellingly, an overdose of pyridostigmine or other cholinesterase inhibitors produces the same crisis.

    The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)¶

    1. Protect responders — PPE and decontamination first. Nothing else if the scene isn't safe.
    2. Airway and oxygen — suction the secretions; prepare for intubation.
    3. Atropine, immediately and aggressively — titrate to drying of secretions, not pupils or heart rate.
    4. Pralidoxime (an oxime) — reactivate the enzyme, before it "ages."
    5. Benzodiazepine — for seizures, and empirically in severe exposure.
    6. Escalate and support — mechanical ventilation, ICU, repeat/continuous atropine.
    7. Report — CBRN, public health, and (for occupational/pesticide) the appropriate authority.

    Treatment¶

    Step 1 — atropine, titrated to secretions

    • Atropine 1–3 mg IV (2–6 mg in severe cases), doubling every 3–5 minutes until bronchial secretions dry and ventilation eases.
    • The endpoint is drying of secretions and improved oxygenation — NOT pupil size or heart rate. Miosis and bradycardia can persist despite adequate atropinization; chasing them leads to under- or over-dosing.
    • Severe poisoning can require tens to hundreds of milligrams over the resuscitation, then a continuous infusion. Do not under-dose out of caution about "normal" doses.
    • Atropine blocks the muscarinic effects; it does nothing for nicotinic weakness/paralysis — which is why the oxime matters.

    Step 2 — pralidoxime (oxime), before the enzyme ages

    • Pralidoxime (2-PAM) 1–2 g IV over ~15–30 min (avoid bolus-induced hypertension), then a continuous infusion for severe poisoning.
    • Reactivates acetylcholinesterase — the only intervention that addresses the nicotinic (neuromuscular) weakness.
    • "Aging" is the clock: the agent–enzyme bond becomes irreversible over time and the oxime no longer works. Aging is fast for some agents (soman in ~2 minutes), slower for most OP pesticides (hours). Give the oxime as early as possible — do not withhold it waiting for atropine to work; they are complementary.

    Step 3 — benzodiazepine for seizures / severe exposure

    • Diazepam or midazolam for seizures; give empirically in severe nerve-agent exposure even before seizures, as it reduces seizure-related brain injury.
    • Midazolam IM is now the field/status-epilepticus standard (RAMPART) — see status epilepticus.

    Military autoinjectors — the field antidote

    Warfighter self-aid/buddy-aid for nerve-agent exposure delivers the same three drugs:

    • ATNAA / DuoDote — a single autoinjector combining atropine (2.1 mg) + pralidoxime (600 mg) (ATNAA is the military designation; DuoDote the civilian EMS equivalent; the older Mark I kit was two separate injectors).
    • CANA (Convulsant Antidote, Nerve Agent) — diazepam 10 mg autoinjector for seizures.
    • Doctrine: repeat atropine/2-PAM autoinjectors per symptoms and training, then evacuate to definitive care and continuous therapy.

    What not to do

    • Do not give succinylcholine — it is metabolized by the same cholinesterase the agent has inhibited, so its paralytic effect is massively prolonged. Use a non-depolarizing agent if paralysis is needed for intubation.
    • Do not delay the oxime waiting to see the atropine response — aging is irreversible.
    • Do not treat before decontamination unless you are in PPE — you will become a casualty.

    Military medicine — nerve agent / organophosphate

    Framework — this is a CBRN / operational topic first; retention is a downstream question. Verify against current CBRN medical doctrine, force-health-protection guidance, and — for residual deficit — AR 40-501 and DoDI 6130.03. See Profiles, MEB/MAR2 & deployability.

    • This is the one neurologic emergency that is also a weapons event. A nerve-agent casualty pattern (multiple cholinergic-toxidrome casualties, pinpoint pupils, no trauma) is a chemical attack until proven otherwise and drives the CBRN response, MOPP posture, and medical-intelligence reporting — not just individual resuscitation.
    • Antidote logistics are a readiness item: ATNAA/DuoDote and CANA autoinjector availability, currency, and self-aid/buddy-aid training are force-health-protection responsibilities, and pyridostigmine bromide pretreatment (a reversible cholinesterase protectant) may be fielded under specific threat conditions and medical direction.
    • Retention and deployability turn on residual deficit, not the acute exposure. Most who survive with prompt antidote recover; persistent neurologic sequelae (cognitive, peripheral neuropathy from chronic OP exposure, or an intermediate syndrome of proximal weakness days after exposure) are what would drive a profile and, if impairing duty, a DES referral under AR 40-501 3-31 and DoDI 6130.03 5.26.
    • Report through CBRN, public health emergency, and medical intelligence channels.

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    One enzyme, every cholinergic synapse. Acetylcholinesterase terminates cholinergic signaling by hydrolyzing acetylcholine in the synaptic cleft. Organophosphates and nerve agents phosphorylate the enzyme's active site, and while it is inhibited, acetylcholine accumulates at muscarinic, nicotinic, and central synapses simultaneously — which is why the toxidrome is so florid and multi-system. Atropine competitively blocks the muscarinic receptors (drying the lethal secretions), while pralidoxime attacks the problem upstream, prising the phosphoryl group off the enzyme to restore acetylcholinesterase itself — the only way to fix the nicotinic weakness that atropine can't touch.

    "Aging" is why minutes matter. The phosphorylated enzyme undergoes a spontaneous chemical change — loss of an alkyl group — that makes the bond permanent. After aging, pralidoxime is useless and recovery depends on synthesizing new enzyme over days to weeks. Aging half-times vary enormously: soman ages in about two minutes, making oxime therapy nearly futile once symptoms are established, whereas many pesticide OPs age over many hours, leaving a real reactivation window. This single kinetic fact drives the doctrine of giving the oxime as early as possible and the military interest in pretreatment to shield a fraction of enzyme before exposure.

    The MG connection, both directions. Nerve-agent poisoning is mechanistically an acetylcholinesterase-inhibitor overdose — the same drug class (pyridostigmine) used to treat myasthenia gravis. Push a cholinesterase inhibitor too far and you convert therapy into a cholinergic crisis; that is exactly the "cholinergic crisis vs myasthenic crisis" distinction at the MG bedside, and it is the reason pyridostigmine pretreatment against nerve agents is dosed and monitored so carefully.
