    Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome (LEMS)¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: an antibody attack on presynaptic voltage-gated calcium channels that throttles acetylcholine release — proximal weakness, autonomic dysfunction, and hyporeflexia. Half the time it is the first sign of a small-cell lung cancer.

    Who this is for: anyone working up proximal weakness that looks like myasthenia but isn't behaving — and the reason the page exists is that finding LEMS means starting a cancer hunt.

    🚩 LEMS is a paraneoplastic red flag until proven otherwise

    ~50–60% of LEMS is paraneoplastic, and the tumor is almost always small-cell lung cancer (SCLC). The neurologic syndrome frequently precedes the cancer diagnosis by months to years — so a new LEMS diagnosis is a mandate to search, and to keep searching if the first scan is clean.

    • CT chest first; FDG-PET/CT if CT is negative and suspicion is high.
    • Repeat imaging every 3–6 months for ~2 years if the initial search is negative — the tumor may not yet be radiologically visible.
    • The strongest single risk factor is smoking. A smoker with LEMS has SCLC until proven otherwise.

    The triad that separates it from MG¶

    Feature LEMS Myasthenia gravis
    Weakness pattern Proximal, legs > arms; gait first Ocular/bulbar first; fatigable
    On brief exercise Transiently improves (post-exercise facilitation) Worsens
    Reflexes Reduced or absent — and may return after exercise Normal
    Autonomic features Prominent — dry mouth, erectile dysfunction, constipation, orthostasis Absent
    Ocular involvement Mild or late Common, early
    Antibody P/Q-type VGCC (~85–95%) AChR / MuSK
    Cancer link SCLC in ~50–60% Thymoma in 10–15%

    The counterintuitive sign: strength and reflexes that improve after a few seconds of exercise (facilitation) point to LEMS, not MG. Dry mouth is the most consistent autonomic clue and is easy to ask about.

    Diagnosis¶

    • Serology — P/Q-type voltage-gated calcium channel (VGCC) antibody, positive in ~85–95%.
    • Electrodiagnostics are characteristic and confirmatory:
      • Low baseline CMAP amplitude.
      • Decrement on low-frequency (2–3 Hz) RNS — like MG.
      • But marked facilitation — CMAP amplitude increase >60–100% after brief maximal exercise or high-frequency (20–50 Hz) stimulation. This post-exercise facilitation is the signature and distinguishes it from MG.
    • The DELTA-P score (bulbar, erectile dysfunction, weight loss, performance status, and others) stratifies SCLC risk and guides how aggressively to hunt.

    The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)¶

    1. Recognize the triad — proximal weakness + autonomic features + hyporeflexia, with facilitation.
    2. Confirm — VGCC antibody + EDx showing post-exercise facilitation.
    3. Hunt for cancer — this is the priority. CT chest → PET/CT → serial imaging for 2 years.
    4. Treat symptoms — amifampridine.
    5. Treat the cause — the tumor (paraneoplastic) or immunotherapy (autoimmune/non-tumor).
    6. Military disposition — and note that a paraneoplastic LEMS is a cancer diagnosis first.

    Treatment¶

    Step 1 — symptomatic: amifampridine

    • Amifampridine (3,4-diaminopyridine; Firdapse) — the FDA-approved first-line symptomatic therapy (approved 2018; label includes patients ≥6 years). A potassium-channel blocker that prolongs the presynaptic action potential, increasing calcium influx and ACh release.
    • Typical dosing titrated across the day; higher daily maxima (up to 80–100 mg/day for patients

      45 kg) are approved.

    • Lowers the seizure threshold — caution at higher doses and with any seizure history.
    • Pyridostigmine adds little in LEMS and is not a substitute.

    Step 2 — treat the underlying driver

    • Paraneoplastic LEMS: treat the SCLC. Cancer treatment frequently improves the neurologic syndrome — the tumor is both the cause and the therapeutic target.
    • Non-tumor (autoimmune) LEMS: immunotherapy — prednisone plus a steroid-sparing agent (azathioprine, mycophenolate); IVIG or PLEX for severe or rapidly progressive weakness.

    Same NMJ-drug cautions as MG

    LEMS shares MG's sensitivity to neuromuscular-blocking drugs — aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, IV magnesium, and neuromuscular blockers can all worsen it. If a paraneoplastic patient is heading for thoracic surgery or chemotherapy, flag the NMJ sensitivity to the anesthesia and oncology teams. See the MG precipitant list.

    Military medicine — LEMS

    Framework — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2, and DoDI 1332.18. See Profiles, MEB/MAR2 & deployability.

    • The disposition is usually driven by the cause, not the LEMS itself. A paraneoplastic LEMS is an SCLC diagnosis — malignancy governs retention (a separate, non-neurologic standard) and the member is non-deployable on that basis alone.
    • For non-tumor autoimmune LEMS, the retention analysis runs like other neuromuscular disease: AR 40-501 3-31 captures neurologic conditions with residual functional impairment, and the DoD floor is DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 5.26.i (peripheral neuropathy / paralytic disorders with permanent functional impairment) applied to the NMJ; document the proximal weakness, autonomic burden, and treatment dependence.
    • Deployability: proximal weakness affecting gait, autonomic instability (orthostasis, heat intolerance), and dependence on amifampridine ± immunotherapy make generalized LEMS non-deployable.
    • Profile: permanent for established disease; functional limits reflecting proximal strength, gait, and heat/orthostatic tolerance.
    • MEB/DES: refer. In a paraneoplastic case, coordinate with oncology — the cancer work-up and the neurologic documentation both belong in the packet.

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    Presynaptic, not postsynaptic — and that is the whole story. In LEMS, antibodies against P/Q-type voltage-gated calcium channels on the motor nerve terminal reduce the calcium influx that triggers acetylcholine vesicle release. Fewer quanta are released per impulse, so the first contraction is weak — but a burst of activity transiently loads the terminal with calcium, briefly increasing release and producing the facilitation that defines the syndrome on exam and on EMG. The same channels sit on autonomic terminals, which is why dry mouth and orthostasis travel with the weakness. It is the mirror image of myasthenia gravis, where the defect is postsynaptic receptor loss and repeated activity only worsens transmission.

    The tumor connection is mechanistic. Small-cell lung cancer expresses functional voltage-gated calcium channels, and the anti-tumor immune response cross-reacts with the identical channels at the neuromuscular junction. LEMS is, in a real sense, the immune system finding the cancer before the radiologist does — which is exactly why the neurologic diagnosis obligates the search.

    Key evidence:

    • Firdapse (amifampridine) FDA approval, 2018 — first approved LEMS therapy; symptomatic first-line.
    • P/Q-type VGCC antibody — the confirmatory serology (~85–95%).
    • DELTA-P score — stratifies SCLC risk at diagnosis.
