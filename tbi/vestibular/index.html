    TBI comorbidity — Vestibular / dizziness¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    Part of the TBI work-up & comorbidities hub.

    One-liner: dizziness is the second most common post-mTBI symptom after headache — 50–80% acutely, persisting in 25–30% beyond 3 months. The highest-yield moves are to catch the curable causes (BPPV), name the specific diagnosis (many have specific treatments), and refer for vestibular rehabilitation — not to reach for a vestibular suppressant. In blast mTBI, the otolith organs (utricle/saccule) are more vulnerable to overpressure than the semicircular canals, which shifts testing and rehab.

    Name the diagnosis — it's not all "post-concussive dizziness"¶

    Differential of post-traumatic dizziness (each treats differently)

    • BPPV — commonest and curable; post-traumatic BPPV is distinct (below).
    • Labyrinthine concussion — direct inner-ear injury → acute vestibular hypofunction without fracture; looks like vestibular neuritis but with a trauma history.
    • Vestibular migraine — a major driver of persistent post-traumatic dizziness, especially with a migraine-phenotype PTH; responds to migraine preventives, not suppressants.
    • PPPD (persistent postural-perceptual dizziness) — Bárány Society 2017 diagnosis: chronic non-spinning dizziness/unsteadiness ≥3 months, worse upright, with self-motion, and with complex visual stimuli; mTBI is a recognized trigger. Treat with SSRI/SNRI + CBT + vestibular rehab, not suppressants (below).
    • Superior semicircular canal dehiscence (SSCD) — trauma unmasks a thin superior canal → sound/pressure-induced vertigo, autophony, pulsatile tinnitus (~45% report a preceding traumatic event). Diagnose with high-res CT temporal bone + VEMP.
    • Perilymph fistula — oval/round-window disruption after barotrauma/impact → fluctuating hearing loss + positional vertigo.
    • Secondary endolymphatic hydrops — post-traumatic Ménière-like: episodic vertigo, hearing loss, tinnitus.
    • Central vestibular dysfunction — diffuse axonal injury of vestibular nuclei/cerebellum/ vestibulo-thalamic pathways; may coexist with peripheral disease.

    Acute — exclude a central cause first

    New acute vestibular syndrome after trauma: exclude posterior-circulation stroke or vertebral/carotid dissection — use HINTS (a normal head-impulse, direction-changing nystagmus, or skew deviation is central), plus red flags (headache/neck pain, focal signs). Don't call new dizziness "concussion" until the central cause is excluded.

    Assess¶

    Screening & bedside tools (with cutoffs)

    • VOMS (Vestibular/Ocular-Motor Screening) — smooth pursuit, saccades, VOR, visual-motion sensitivity, near-point convergence. Abnormal = symptom provocation ≥2 points on any item, or NPC >5 cm.
    • Dix-Hallpike (posterior canal) and supine roll test (horizontal canal) — positional BPPV.
    • Dynamic visual acuity (DVA) — VOR during head movement; ≥2-line drop from static acuity is abnormal.
    • DHI (Dizziness Handicap Inventory) — 25 items; >30 = moderate handicap; tracks response.
    • BESS (Balance Error Scoring System) — the SCAT6/MACE2 balance test.
    • FGA (Functional Gait Assessment) — 10-item dynamic gait; more sensitive than the Berg Balance Scale in mTBI (head turns, eyes-closed walking).

    Treat¶

    BPPV — post-traumatic BPPV is harder than idiopathic

    • Reposition, don't suppress. Dix-Hallpike → Epley for posterior canal; supine roll → Lempert (barbecue) roll for horizontal canal.
    • Check all canals: post-traumatic BPPV is multi-canal in ~50–67% (vs ~10% idiopathic) and more often bilateral — a Dix-Hallpike alone will miss horizontal-canal disease.
    • Expect higher recurrence and more maneuvers. Diagnose early — median time to first repositioning is ~13.5 days in polytrauma, and earlier treatment improves outcomes.

    Vestibular rehabilitation therapy (VRT) — first-line for persistent symptoms

    • Refer for VRT for persistent dizziness/imbalance/oculomotor symptoms. A 2024 meta-analysis (6 RCTs) showed improved DHI (SMD −0.33) and VOMS (SMD −0.40) at end of treatment; individually tailored multimodal (cervical + vestibular + oculomotor) therapy had a moderate effect (SMD 0.63) and made patients ~3× more likely to return to sport by 8 weeks.
    • Goal-based reassessment: VA/DoD recommends vestibular/balance therapy beyond the acute phase but discourages prolonged therapy without improvement — reassess against functional goals.
    • Provider matters — ideally a therapist with post-graduate vestibular-rehab training.

    Cervicogenic dizziness — identify and treat the neck

    Common after whiplash/blast (cervical proprioceptive dysfunction). Suspect when dizziness is provoked by neck movement, with neck pain and restricted cervical ROM. Identify with the cervical joint-position-error test and smooth-pursuit neck-torsion test. Treat with cervical manual therapy + proprioceptive retraining (Langevin 2022 RCT improved cervical and vestibular measures).

    PPPD — break the dizziness→anxiety→avoidance cycle

    Persistent dizziness often becomes maladaptive: dizziness → anxiety → movement avoidance → deconditioning → worse dizziness (the mechanism behind PPPD) — high-yield to catch in service members, where avoidance degrades duty performance. Treat all three arms: SSRI/SNRI (sertraline, venlafaxine), CBT targeting dizziness-related anxiety/avoidance (see mood/PTSD), and graded vestibular exposure via rehab. Suppressants make it worse.

    Vestibular suppressants — mostly a don't after concussion

    • Not recommended routinely (VA/DoD) — they delay central compensation and promote counterproductive adaptation.
    • If symptoms are severe enough to limit function, a brief (<1 week) trial of meclizine 25 mg is the preferred agent. Avoid benzodiazepines (clonazepam/diazepam/lorazepam — sedation, cognitive impairment, dependence).
    • Never treat BPPV with a suppressant — reposition instead.

    When to refer for formal vestibular lab testing

    Escalate beyond bedside (VNG, calorics, rotary chair, VEMP, posturography) for:

    • Persistent dizziness >4 weeks despite initial management.
    • Atypical nystagmus not consistent with BPPV.
    • Suspected SSCD (order high-res CT temporal bone + VEMP) or perilymph fistula (fluctuating hearing loss + positional vertigo).
    • Failure to respond to vestibular rehab.
    • Need for objective documentation (MEB/VA rating).

    Military note: persistent vestibular dysfunction is deployment- and (with the vision/oculomotor overlap) special-duty-limiting; blast exposure favors otolith injury (VEMP-testable). Document objective testing for the TBI hub retention/aeromedical framing.
