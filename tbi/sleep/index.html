    TBI comorbidity — Sleep¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    Part of the TBI work-up & comorbidities hub.

    One-liner: sleep disruption sustains every other TBI symptom (headache, mood, cognition, fatigue) — fix it first. Sleep–wake disturbance affects 30–70% of mTBI and ~46% of chronic TBI patients (insomnia the commonest), and a VA cohort of ~200,000 veterans confirmed TBI independently raises the risk of insomnia, hypersomnia, and sleep apnea years after injury.

    Sleep gates recovery — the relationship is bidirectional

    Poor sleep worsens post-traumatic headache (OR ~2.9 in the first 6 weeks), cognition, and mood/anxiety; conversely, treating sleep improves outcomes across every other domain. The 2025 AAPM&R guideline explicitly prioritizes sleep as a treatment target because it gates recovery. Improving sleep often unmasks how much of the "post-concussive" picture was sleep-driven.

    Screen and measure — validated instruments

    • ISI (Insomnia Severity Index) — 7 items, validated in TBI; ≥10 = clinical insomnia.
    • PSQI (Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index) — global sleep quality; >5 = poor sleep.
    • ESS (Epworth Sleepiness Scale) — daytime sleepiness; ≥10 = excessive.
    • STOP-Bang — OSA risk (below).
    • Actigraphy — objective sleep–wake/circadian patterns when PSG isn't indicated.
    • PSG ± MSLT — for suspected OSA, narcolepsy, or hypersomnia (MSLT confirms objective sleepiness and separates it from fatigue).

    Name the disorder — it's not all insomnia¶

    The post-TBI sleep differential (with rough prevalence)

    • Insomnia — most common; onset and/or maintenance difficulty.
    • Obstructive sleep apnea — 20–77% across studies; drives "refractory" fatigue/cognitive complaints (work-up below).
    • Post-traumatic hypersomnia (~11–22%) — sleep need ≥2 h/day above pre-injury, from hypothalamic injury / reduced hypocretin (orexin) signaling.
    • Narcolepsy / narcolepsy-like (~6%) — loss of hypocretin neurons → excessive daytime sleepiness, occasionally cataplexy. Exclude before starting a DORA (contraindicated).
    • Periodic limb movement disorder (~7%).
    • Parasomnias — nightmares, REM sleep behavior disorder — especially with comorbid PTSD.
    • Fatigue ≠ sleepiness. Fatigue is daytime tiredness without objective sleepiness on MSLT and needs different management (activity pacing, mood/pain treatment) — do not reach for a wake-promoting agent for pure fatigue.

    Insomnia¶

    Step 1 — CBT-I is first-line (Strong for)

    CBT-I before drugs. VA/DoD 2019 Chronic Insomnia CPG gives it a Strong for recommendation; a 2026 network meta-analysis (22 RCTs, n=1,299 TBI patients) found CBT the most consistently effective intervention across ISI/PSQI/ESS — outperforming every drug tested. 70–80% get enduring benefit and ~50% reach remission.

    • No in-person CBT-I? Use internet-delivered CBT-I (eCBT-I / SHUTi) — a 2024 JAMA Netw Open RCT in military TBI patients showed feasibility and efficacy, and it fixes the access barrier (military CBT-I referrals rose 372% over 2005–2014).
    • Pair with sleep-hygiene / circadian measures: fixed wake time, morning light, evening screen/caffeine limits — relevant to shift work and deployment.

    Step 2 — pharmacotherapy if CBT-I is insufficient (dosed)

    Adjunct to CBT-I, not a substitute. Match the drug to onset vs maintenance; reassess and taper.

    • Melatonin (prolonged-release) 2–5 mg, ~2 h before bedtime — best TBI-specific evidence: an RCT (n=33) improved PSQI (−1.79 vs placebo, p≤0.0001), sleep efficiency, anxiety, and fatigue; a 2023 scoping review found benefit in 8/9 studies, well tolerated. Sleep-onset benefit mainly — less for maintenance.
    • Dual orexin receptor antagonists (DORAs) — suvorexant 10–20 mg, lemborexant 5–10 mg, or daridorexant 25–50 mg qhs. Work for both onset and maintenance, less cognitive impairment than benzodiazepines, low abuse potential; a 2025 network meta-analysis shows all three beat placebo. Mechanistically apt (TBI disrupts the orexin system) — but contraindicated in narcolepsy (exclude first). Likely PA-gated on TRICARE.
    • Low-dose doxepin 3–6 mg qhs — FDA-approved for sleep-maintenance insomnia; the only sedating antidepressant with consistent insomnia efficacy (2024 NEJM review).
    • Trazodone 25–100 mg qhs — widely used off-label (helpful with comorbid mood), but no TBI-specific RCT and "inconsistent efficacy evidence" for general insomnia; a head-to-head vs melatonin vs combination trial is underway (2026).
    • Avoid: chronic benzodiazepines / Z-drugs (cognition, dependence, fall/duty risk) and antihistamines (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine — anticholinergic/cognitive load, weak evidence).

    Excessive daytime sleepiness / hypersomnia¶

    Wake-promoting agents — for objective sleepiness, not fatigue (dosed)

    First exclude and treat OSA and confirm objective sleepiness (ESS/MSLT). Then, for post-traumatic hypersomnia (AASM 2021 Conditional recommendation):

    • Modafinil 100–200 mg each morning, or armodafinil 150–250 mg each morning. A 2025 meta-analysis (3 RCTs, n=158) showed reduced ESS (MD −1.65, p=0.04); armodafinil 250 mg gave the clearest objective sleep-latency benefit (+7.2 vs +2.4 min). Watch for treatment- emergent insomnia (RR ~3.7).
    • Schedule IV controlled substances — relevant for military prescribing and deployability.

    Obstructive sleep apnea¶

    OSA — screen, test in tiers, treat (Strong for PAP)

    • Screen with STOP-Bang (Snoring, Tiredness, Observed apnea, Pressure, BMI, Age, Neck, Gender): ≥3 intermediate, ≥5 high risk. Have a low threshold — OSA prevalence in TBI is 20–77%.
    • Phased, cost-effective work-up: STOP-Bang → home sleep apnea (portable) testing → lab PSG if portable testing is negative/discordant but suspicion remains.
    • PAP therapy is first-line (VA/DoD 2019 OSA CPG, Strong for). Set expectations: CPAP reliably improves AHI but may not fully resolve daytime sleepiness in TBI — reassess for a coexisting central hypersomnia if sleepiness persists on adherent PAP.

    Light therapy — non-drug, no interactions, deployable

    Useful for circadian misalignment, sleepiness, and comorbid low mood — attractive in the military setting. Morning blue-wavelength light (~469 nm, 30 min) phase-advanced sleep, cut daytime sleepiness, and improved executive function in an mTBI RCT (n=32); a 2022 meta-analysis (6 RCTs, n=278) found reduced sleep disturbance (SMD −0.63) and depression (SMD −1.00). Morning bright light ~10,000 lux, 60 min improved insomnia, mood, and pain in a veteran cohort.

    TRICARE — TBI sleep prescribing

    Melatonin, trazodone, doxepin, modafinil/armodafinil are generic/formulary (modafinil-class are Schedule IV; generic modafinil is preferred over branded). DORAs (suvorexant, lemborexant, daridorexant) are PA-gated — document CBT-I ± prior-agent failure. CBT-I, eCBT-I, PSG, and PAP go through normal sleep-medicine/behavioral-health referral. Verify at the TRICARE Formulary Search.

    Military note: untreated OSA, chronic hypnotics, and Schedule IV wake-promoting agents are duty/deployability considerations — document the work-up, diagnosis, and PAP adherence. See the TBI hub for the retention/aeromedical framing.
