    TBI comorbidity — Autonomic¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    Part of the TBI work-up & comorbidities hub.

    One-liner: two very different problems live under "autonomic." (A) mTBI autonomic dysfunction — orthostatic and exercise intolerance, palpitations, temperature dysregulation — is an outpatient issue, very treatable with graded reconditioning. (B) Paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity (PSH) is a severe-TBI ICU emergency — different population, setting, and drugs. Keep them separate.

    Why it happens — and why exercise is both test and treatment

    mTBI causes diffuse axonal injury of brainstem autonomic centers and impairs cerebrovascular autoregulation, so the brain can't hold steady perfusion when blood pressure or metabolic demand changes. During exertion this creates a demand–perfusion mismatch → headache, dizziness, fog. Standardized testing shows blunted sympathetic baroreflex and reduced HR/BP variability in the first two weeks. Graded exercise restores autonomic function and autoregulation — which is exactly why exercise is both diagnostic (BCTT) and therapeutic.

    (A) mTBI autonomic dysfunction / exercise intolerance / POTS¶

    Assess — orthostatic vitals + the Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test

    • Symptoms: orthostatic intolerance/lightheadedness, exertional symptom exacerbation, palpitations/tachycardia, sweating and temperature dysregulation.
    • Orthostatic vitals / POTS: sustained HR rise ≥30 bpm (≥40 in adolescents) within 10 min of standing/tilt, without orthostatic hypotension. Concussion is a recognized POTS trigger, and POTS resolution tends to parallel return to activity — reinforcing exercise as treatment.
    • Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test (BCTT) — the validated exercise-tolerance test (~94% sensitivity, ~95% specificity for concussion): walk at 3.3 mph / 0% incline, increase incline 1° per minute, rate symptoms every minute; stop at a ≥3-point symptom rise (0–10 scale) or volitional exhaustion. In community (non-athlete) adults, ~56% show exercise intolerance at 1 week and ~48% at 1 month.

    Sub-symptom-threshold aerobic exercise (SSTAE) — the prescription

    The HR at the BCTT symptom threshold is the target (if no treadmill, use ~50% of age-predicted max HR, max = 220 − age):

    1. Exercise at 80% of the symptom-threshold HR, 20 min/day, 5–6 days/week.
    2. Retest every 2–3 weeks and advance the target HR upward as tolerance improves.
    3. Stay sub-threshold — a >2–3-point symptom rise means back off.

    Adult RCT evidence: the 2026 Valaas trial (n=81, persistent PCS 3–24 mo) improved exercise tolerance at 12 weeks (sustained at 6 mo); the ACTBI trial (n=52) improved quality of life vs stretching (p=0.047), no adverse events. Nuance: SSTAE reliably improves exercise tolerance, but its effect on overall symptom burden in adults is less robust than in adolescent athletes — both treatment and control groups tend to improve over time.

    Orthostatic intolerance / POTS pharmacotherapy — non-drug first

    Drugs here are borrowed from the general OH/POTS literature, not TBI-specific. Exhaust non-pharmacological measures first (they are the cornerstone):

    • First-line (non-drug): fluids 2–3 L/day, salt 10–12 g/day, compression garments, and the graded exercise program above.
    • If insufficient, symptom-directed: beta-blocker or ivabradine (palpitation-dominant); midodrine (hypotension-dominant); atomoxetine (an NRI — for brain fog with low catecholamines); pyridostigmine as an adjunct. Fludrocortisone for refractory volume support.

    Temperature dysregulation — practical, and military-relevant

    Post-TBI thermoregulatory dysfunction can present as heat intolerance, excessive sweating, or impaired sweating — a real hazard for troops exercising in the heat. Use graded heat acclimatization and modified PT standards during recovery; document heat intolerance as a duty/deployability consideration.

    (B) Paroxysmal sympathetic hyperactivity (PSH) — severe-TBI ICU¶

    PSH — recognize, standardize, and treat the storm

    Who: 10–30% of moderate–severe TBI; ~80% of all PSH follows TBI. Definition: simultaneous paroxysmal, transient surges in sympathetic activity (↑HR, ↑BP, ↑RR, hyperthermia, sweating) and motor activity (posturing) in survivors of severe acquired brain injury.

    Diagnose with the PSH Assessment Measure (PSH-AM, 2014 consensus): a Clinical Feature Scale (grading HR, BP, RR, temperature, sweating, posturing) + an 11-item Diagnosis Likelihood Tool — standardizes diagnosis and tracks response.

    PSH management ladder (2017 Lancet Neurology / 2024 ACS)

    • Find and treat triggers first — ~80% of paroxysms are allodynic (pain, urinary retention, movement, suctioning). Minimize noxious stimuli.
    • Abort/suppress: opioids (morphine) are the most-used first-line agent for allodynic responses.
    • Prevent/blunt: propranolol has the most evidence (low-quality data suggest lower mortality in severe TBI with PSH); alpha-2 agonists (clonidine, dexmedetomidine). A 2026 RCT found propranolol + gabapentin cut PSH incidence to 10% (vs 33% propranolol alone, 60% standard care) — though gabapentin prolonged recovery time.
    • Adjuncts/refractory: benzodiazepines, bromocriptine, baclofen (intrathecal for refractory cases), dantrolene.
    • Feed the storm: resting energy expenditure can rise up to 3× baseline during paroxysms — weight loss of 25–29% is reported. Prioritize nutritional support.

    Military note: exercise intolerance directly limits return-to-duty fitness — the BCTT + SSTAE progression doubles as a documentable recovery metric; heat intolerance is a deployability consideration. See the TBI hub and the exercise prescription on the hub Return-to-activity section.
