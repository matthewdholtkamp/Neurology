    Traumatic brain injury (TBI)¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: brain injury from an external force — the signature injury of the post-9/11 military, and mostly a mild TBI (concussion) problem. The acute event is usually the easy part; the value is in managing the long tail of comorbidities — headache, sleep, mood/PTSD, cognition, dizziness, vision, and the ones people forget (neuroendocrine, autonomic, suicide risk). Treat them by domain (each has its own page in the sidebar under TBI), and the patient gets better.

    🚩 Acute TBI — exclude a structural emergency first

    • Image (non-contrast CT) for red flags: worsening/severe headache, vomiting, ↓ or fluctuating consciousness, focal deficit, seizure, anticoagulation, or a high-risk mechanism (use a validated head-CT rule). Suspect cervical-artery dissection with neck trauma/pain.
    • Second-impact syndrome: a second head impact before the first concussion has recovered can be catastrophic (diffuse cerebral edema) — remove from activity and do not return to play/duty while symptomatic.
    • Moderate/severe TBI → trauma/neurosurgery, ICP management, and the ICH/increased-ICP pathways; this page focuses on the post-acute and chronic course.

    Classify the severity¶

    Severity GCS LOC Post-traumatic amnesia Imaging
    Mild (concussion) 13–15 ≤30 min <24 h Normal (or complicated-mild if abnormal)
    Moderate 9–12 30 min–24 h 1–7 days Often abnormal
    Severe ≤8 >24 h >7 days Abnormal

    Confirming an mTBI diagnosis — 2023 ACRM criteria

    The table above grades severity; to confirm the diagnosis of mild TBI, the 2025 AAPM&R guideline endorses (Strong for) the 2023 ACRM diagnostic criteria as the preferred standard — useful in Work-ups 2–3 to separate true mTBI from mimics. Require all four: (1) a plausible mechanism; (2) ≥1 acute clinical sign (LOC, post-traumatic amnesia, disorientation, or an acute seizure); (3) ≥2 acute symptoms (headache, dizziness, nausea, fogginess, etc.); and (4) ≥1 objective finding (cognitive impairment, balance deficit, or oculomotor dysfunction). A 2026 structured interview standardizes their application.

    Which patient is in front of you? — pick the pathway¶

    The work-up is completely different by phase. Sort the patient into one of three before anything else:

    Presentation Setting Pathway
    Acute head injury, just happened ER / aid station / field Work-up 1 — Acute TBI (ER)
    Recent concussion, still symptomatic (days–weeks) clinic / concussion cell Work-up 2 — Acute-phase mTBI (outpatient)
    Symptoms persisting >~4 wks, or moderate–severe TBI in the chronic phase, or repeated concussions TBI/neuro clinic Work-up 3 — Long-term TBI follow-up

    Work-up 1 — Acute TBI in the ER¶

    The acute head-injury evaluation

    1. ABCs + C-spine precautions; GCS, pupils, focal exam; glucose; note anticoagulation/ antiplatelet use, LOC, and post-traumatic amnesia.
    2. Decide on CT with a validated rule (e.g., Canadian CT Head Rule). Get a non-contrast head CT for any high-risk feature: GCS <15 at 2 h, suspected open/depressed skull fracture, any basilar-skull-fracture sign (hemotympanum, raccoon eyes, Battle's sign, CSF oto/ rhinorrhea), ≥2 episodes of vomiting, age ≥65; or medium-risk: retrograde amnesia ≥30 min, dangerous mechanism. Anticoagulation/coagulopathy or post-traumatic seizure → image regardless. Add CTA neck if dissection is a concern. Blood biomarkers (GFAP + UCH-L1) can help rule out the CT in adult mTBI within 12 h — see box below.
    3. If there's a bleed: reverse anticoagulation (specifics in the box below, or the ICH reversal table), BP control, and neurosurgery for a surgical lesion (EDH/SDH/contusion) or raised ICP. Consider TXA within 3 h for mild–moderate TBI (GCS 9–15) with intracranial hemorrhage; avoid in isolated severe TBI (box below). Moderate/severe → trauma bay + ICP/PbtO₂ pathway (box below).
    4. Disposition: admit/observe for an abnormal CT, GCS <15, persistent vomiting, anticoagulation, or an unsafe home; a normal CT with a normal exam and improving symptoms can go home with a reliable observer, written return precautions, and no return to play/duty the same day.
    5. In uniform: after a potentially concussive event, MACE2 and the mandated recovery period apply (military box).

    Blood biomarkers — GFAP + UCH-L1 (rule out the CT)

    The FDA-cleared GFAP + UCH-L1 assay (Abbott i-STAT, cleared 2018) helps rule out intracranial injury in adult mTBI evaluated within 12 h of injury. A negative panel — GFAP <30 pg/mL and UCH-L1 <360 pg/mL (2024 ACS Best Practices thresholds) — carries an NPV approaching 100%. A 2025 meta-analysis found 100% pooled sensitivity (95% CI 99–100%) with ~31% specificity; a 2026 review estimated a 20–30% reduction in CT use. Highest value where CT is limited (austere/deployed) — a negative panel can safely omit the scan. It does not override a red flag that already mandates imaging.

    Anticoagulation reversal & TXA (acute bleed) — dosed

    Reversal (2024 ACS Best Practices / Neurocritical Care Society):

    • Warfarin → 4-factor PCC (Kcentra), INR-based, dosed on actual body weight capped at 100 kg: INR 2–3.9 → 25 units/kg (max 2500), INR 4–6 → 35 units/kg (max 3500), INR >6 → 50 units/kg (max 5000). (Many trauma centers use a fixed 1500–2000 units to save time — give it, then check INR.) Plus vitamin K 10 mg IV by slow infusion over 10–30 min (sustains reversal; PCC alone wears off). Recheck INR at 15–60 min, target <1.5.
    • Dabigatran → idarucizumab 5 g IV (two 2.5 g / 50 mL vials, consecutive boluses). If unavailable: activated PCC (FEIBA) 50 units/kg and consider hemodialysis.
    • Factor Xa inhibitors (apixaban/rivaroxaban) → 4-factor PCC 50 units/kg (or a fixed 2000 units) — andexanet alfa has been withdrawn from the US market.
    • Antiplatelet (aspirin/clopidogrel): do not routinely transfuse platelets for spontaneous/traumatic ICH (PATCH-signal harm) unless a neurosurgical procedure is planned; consider desmopressin (DDAVP) 0.4 µg/kg IV ×1.
    • Repeat CT at 6–24 h to check for hematoma progression before starting VTE prophylaxis.

    Tranexamic acid — mixed and evolving. Consider TXA within 3 h (1 g IV bolus + 1 g infusion, or a 2 g bolus) for mild–moderate TBI (GCS 9–15) with intracranial hemorrhage — CRASH-3 reduced head-injury–related death in this group, and a prehospital-trial subgroup found a 2 g bolus cut 28-day mortality in confirmed ICH (17% vs 27%). Avoid in isolated severe TBI (GCS ≤8) — no benefit (CRASH-3), with a signal of possible harm. A 2024 meta-analysis (9 RCTs, n=14,747) found no overall mortality/disability benefit, though TXA may limit hematoma expansion.

    VTE prophylaxis timing (2024 ACS Best Practices) — dosed

    Balance re-bleeding against clot risk. Agent — LMWH preferred over UFH: enoxaparin 30 mg SC q12h or 40 mg SC daily (reduce to 30 mg daily if CrCl <30), or UFH 5000 units SC q8–12h when LMWH is contraindicated. Start mechanical prophylaxis (SCDs) on admission in the meantime.

    • Low-risk nonoperative TBI: start within 24 h if follow-up CT shows no ICH progression.
    • Moderate/high-risk nonoperative TBI: within 24–48 h if follow-up CT is stable.
    • Post-craniotomy/craniectomy: within 24–48 h if the postoperative CT is stable.

    A 2025 Military TBI Initiative meta-analysis found no increase in ICH progression with prophylaxis started within 72 h.

    Moderate–severe TBI — ICP / PbtO₂ / CPP targets (with doses)

    For GCS <9 with an abnormal CT, place ICP monitoring (Brain Trauma Foundation, 4th ed.); treat ICP >22 mmHg. Target CPP 60–70 mmHg — avoid driving CPP >70 (increased ARDS risk). PbtO₂ monitoring target >20 mmHg is an emerging complement: OXY-TC (Lancet Neurol 2023) cut brain-hypoxia time with dual ICP + PbtO₂ monitoring, though 6-month extended-GOS was not significantly improved. Where invasive monitoring is unavailable (austere/deployed), the 2025 Brussels (B-ICONIC) consensus guides non-invasive ICP.

    Tiered ICP control — head of bed 30°, normocarbia (PaCO₂ 35–40), sedation/analgesia first; then for a surge:

    • Hyperosmolar bolus (either): hypertonic saline 3% 250 mL (or 23.4% 30 mL via central line) or mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV — mannitol needs a normal BP (osmotic diuresis) and serum osm <320; hypertonic saline is preferred if hypotensive.
    • Brief hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) only as a temporizing bridge to the OR/definitive care.
    • Refractory: neurosurgery for decompressive craniectomy / CSF drainage; not routine steroids (harmful in TBI — CRASH).

    Work-up 2 — Acute-phase outpatient mTBI (days–weeks out, still symptomatic)¶

    The concussion-clinic / aid-station work-up

    1. History: mechanism, LOC/PTA, and a symptom inventory across domains (headache, dizziness, cognitive, sleep, mood), prior concussions, and persistence risk factors (cognitive symptoms, pre-existing anxiety/depression or sleep disorder, female sex, high acute symptom burden — see box below).
    2. Targeted exam: neuro + cervical spine, a vestibular/ocular-motor screen (VOMS), and a balance check (BESS). Use the right tool for the phase — SCAT6 on the sideline/≤72 h, SCOAT6 in the office at 3–30 days (or MACE2 in uniform) — plus a symptom scale (PCSS / Neurobehavioral Symptom Inventory). Cutoffs in the box below.
    3. Imaging is usually NOT needed — a normal exam with improving symptoms doesn't warrant it (AHS/ACR); image only for red flags, focal deficits, or worsening/atypical course.
    4. Manage: brief relative rest (24–48 h) → early sub-threshold aerobic exercise, sleep hygiene, and symptom-targeted treatment (treat the headache phenotype; avoid opioids/ butalbital). Reassure — most recover in days–weeks.
    5. Graded return to activity/duty; step back a stage on symptom recurrence. Treat the high-persistence-risk patient aggressively and early.
    6. Follow up; if not recovering by ~4 weeks → escalate to Work-up 3 / a multidisciplinary concussion clinic.

    Concussion assessment tools — which, and when

    • SCAT6 — sideline/acute, within 72 h of injury.
    • SCOAT6 — office-based subacute assessment at 3–30 days; the 6th Amsterdam Consensus (2023) created it specifically for the clinic-follow-up gap.
    • VOMS — positive if symptom provocation ≥2 on any item or NPC ≥5 cm (AUC 0.89 for concussion identification).
    • mSIT Plus (2026 NCAA–DoD CARE, n=59,901) — a streamlined 6-question index + modified VOMS, AUC 0.94; built for time-limited field/military screening.
    • MACE2 — the mandated DoD sideline tool after a concussive event.

    Persistence risk factors — who won't recover fast (treat early, aggressively)

    2025 evidence (JAMA Netw Open meta-analysis, McIntosh et al.; NCAA–DoD CARE Consortium):

    • Cognitive symptoms (difficulty concentrating) — strongest predictor (OR >3).
    • Pre-existing anxiety/depression or a sleep disorder.
    • Acute clinical signs (LOC, amnesia).
    • Female sex — significant but a smaller effect.
    • High acute symptom burden (SCAT total >45), prior concussion history, and delayed reporting predict symptoms persisting >29 days.

    Work-up 3 — Long-term TBI follow-up (persistent — the common military clinic visit)¶

    Structured multi-domain review (persistent post-concussive / chronic / repeated TBI)

    1. Re-confirm the diagnosis and rule out a new/secondary process if the pattern changed.
    2. Systematically screen and treat every comorbidity domain — work the domain pages below (headache, sleep, mood/PTSD, cognition, vestibular, vision, neuroendocrine, autonomic, seizures, pain/substance). This is where persistent TBI is won or lost.
    3. Don't miss the quiet ones: neuroendocrine (hypopituitarism), OSA, and suicide risk — screen deliberately.
    4. Refer to a multidisciplinary TBI clinic (neuro + rehab + behavioral health + sleep + vestibular/PT + endocrine + neuropsych) for refractory/complex cases.
    5. Own the disposition: profile, MEB/DES, VA rating documentation, and — for a repeated- impact history — honest TES/CTE counseling (below). Track longitudinally with a symptom inventory and treat to target.

    Manage the comorbidities — by domain¶

    Persistent post-TBI symptoms are multidomain and overlapping — treat each explicitly (this is the engine of Work-up 3, and drives symptom care in Work-up 2); improving one (sleep, mood, headache) lifts the others. Each domain has its own page (nested under TBI in the sidebar):

    • Headache — the commonest symptom; phenotype-directed (full detail on the post-traumatic headache page).
    • Sleep — fix it first; CBT-I, screen for OSA.
    • Mood & PTSD — CBT + SSRI/SNRI, prazosin; screen suicide every visit.
    • Cognitive — treat the contributors first; cognitive rehab.
    • Vestibular / dizziness — BPPV/Epley; vestibular rehab.
    • Vision / oculomotor — convergence insufficiency; vision therapy.
    • Neuroendocrine — hypopituitarism screening — the quiet, treatable miss.
    • Autonomic — dysautonomia; exercise reconditioning.
    • Post-traumatic seizures — 7-day prophylaxis ≠ epilepsy prevention.
    • Pain & substance — opioid-sparing; screen substances.

    Return to activity / duty¶

    Brief relative rest → early sub-threshold aerobic exercise → graded, supervised return (school/ work/cognitive load before full contact/duty); step back a stage on symptom recurrence. In uniform, follow the Progressive Return to Activity stages (military box). The headache-specific detail is on the post-traumatic headache page.

    Sub-threshold aerobic exercise prescription

    Start early — within 24–72 h, not after a rest period. Prescribe activity kept below the symptom-exacerbation threshold (a >2-point rise on a 0–10 symptom scale = "more than mild" = went too hard):

    1. Find the threshold with the Buffalo Concussion Treadmill Test (BCTT) — the HR at which symptoms rise >2 points.
    2. No treadmill? Prescribe at ~50% of age-predicted max HR (max = 220 − age).
    3. Dose ~20 min/day below that threshold; advance as tolerated, backing off on a >2-point rise.

    RCT evidence: early aerobic exercise cut median recovery 17 → 13 days (p=0.009) and persistent symptoms 32% → 21% (p=0.04). Strong for in both the Amsterdam 2023 consensus and the 2025 AAPM&R guideline (2025 NEJM review, Leddy).

    Long-term: CTE / neurodegeneration & suicide risk¶

    Traumatic encephalopathy syndrome (TES) — counsel carefully, don't over-diagnose

    Chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) is a neuropathological diagnosis (perivascular p-tau at sulcal depths) — only confirmable at autopsy. In life, the 2021 NINDS consensus criteria define Traumatic Encephalopathy Syndrome (TES): substantial repetitive head-impact exposure (contact sport, military blast), progressive cognitive impairment (memory/executive) and/or neurobehavioral dysregulation (explosivity, impulsivity), not fully explained by another disorder. It is a clinical syndrome, not a confirmed CTE diagnosis — counsel honestly about uncertainty, treat the modifiable comorbidities, and avoid nihilism.

    The 2021 NINDS TES criteria are explicitly research criteria — use them cautiously in clinical and medicolegal settings. Do not disclose provisional "levels of certainty" for underlying CTE pathology to patients: it can produce a nocebo effect — worsening mental health and deterring pursuit of treatable conditions. Keep counseling focused on the modifiable comorbidities.

    Suicide risk is elevated after TBI — screen and act

    TBI (especially with mood/PTSD, chronic pain, and repeated injury) independently raises suicide risk. Screen for suicidal ideation at every visit, treat depression/PTSD/pain/sleep aggressively, restrict means, and connect to behavioral health / the Veterans Crisis Line.

    TRICARE — TBI prescribing

    Most symptom-directed drugs are generic/formulary, no PA — SSRIs/SNRIs, prazosin, trazodone, melatonin, the oral headache preventives, levetiracetam, and standard hormone replacements. CGRP agents, onabotulinumtoxinA (chronic-migraine phenotype), and stimulants carry the usual PA/specialist requirements. Multidisciplinary rehab, CBT-I, vestibular/vision therapy, and behavioral health go through normal specialty/rehab referral. Verify at the TRICARE Formulary Search.

    Military medicine — TBI / concussion (mTBI)

    Quoted from DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1/Vol 2, AR 40-501, the VA/DoD mTBI CPG (2021), and DoDI 6490.11; DES governed by DoDI 1332.18 / DoDM 1332.18. mTBI is protocolized in DoD — follow the mandated pathway, not just this page.

    • Event-driven protocol: after a potentially concussive event (blast, vehicle, fall, blunt), DoD policy mandates evaluation + a mandatory recovery period — screen with MACE2 and manage per the deployed-concussion protocol before return to duty.
    • Return to activity: use the Progressive Return to Activity stages; symptom recurrence → step back a stage.
    • Deployability: acute concussion is temporarily non-deployable pending clearance; persistent multi-domain sequelae are deployment-limiting; multiple concussions raise the return-to-duty bar.
    • Profile (DA 3349): temporary during recovery; permanent for persistent, functionally limiting post-concussive syndrome.
    • EPTS / LOD: combat/duty-incurred TBI is typically in the line of duty — document event, mechanism, timeline (drives LOD and VA rating).
    • Retention — DoD & Army: DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.h places TBI with persistent sequelae — severe headaches, cognitive/sensory/motor impairment, or seizures — below retention. Army: migraine-phenotype PTH ≥3 incapacitating days/mo under AR 40-501 3-31g, and residual persistent severe headache / other neuro sequelae under 3-31k → DES (MEB/PEB). Document objective frequency, failed trials, neuropsych findings, and duty impact.
    • Accession — DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1 para 6.26: persistent post-concussive symptoms >1 month, a history of head trauma with persistent focal deficit, or ≥3 mild brain injuries are disqualifying for entry.
    • Aeromedical (AR 40-501 ch. 4, table 4-2): head-injury standards are graded by how long the post-traumatic syndrome persists — e.g., PTH resolving ~<14 days → temporary aviation suspension; post-traumatic syndrome persisting >~6 weeks → permanent DQ / 2-yr termination of aviation service. Confirm the exact table-4-2 tier for the class.

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    • Sort by phase first — the work-up is different for acute TBI in the ER (CT-rule triage, reversal, neurosurgery/disposition), acute-phase outpatient mTBI (symptom inventory + VOMS/balance, usually no imaging, exercise + graded return), and long-term follow-up (systematic multi-domain comorbidity review — the common military clinic visit).
    • TBI is mostly a mild-TBI, chronic-comorbidity problem — the acute event resolves; the persistent symptoms (headache, sleep, mood/PTSD, cognition, dizziness, vision) are what disable, and they're multidomain and interlinked.
    • Treat by domain and fix sleep + mood first — they gate everything else; headache-specific CBT, CBT-I, and vestibular/vision rehab are the highest-value non-drug tools.
    • Don't miss the quiet ones: post-traumatic hypopituitarism (screen moderate–severe/blast TBI in the first year — treatable), OSA, and suicide risk (screen every visit).
    • Rest is not the treatment — brief relative rest then early sub-threshold aerobic exercise (within 24–72 h; BCTT threshold or ~50% max HR) and graded return; avoid opioids/butalbital.
    • Acute mTBI triage is getting a biomarker — a negative GFAP + UCH-L1 panel (within 12 h, NPV ~100%) can safely omit the CT and may cut CT use 20–30% — high value in austere/deployed care.
    • Acute bleed management has specifics — agent-specific reversal, TXA within 3 h for mild–moderate TBI with ICH (avoid in isolated severe), VTE prophylaxis within 24–48 h once the follow-up CT is stable (LMWH preferred), and for GCS <9 ICP >22 mmHg / CPP 60–70 / PbtO₂ >20 targets.
    • Confirm mTBI with the 2023 ACRM criteria (mechanism + acute sign + ≥2 symptoms + an objective finding) — the 2025 AAPM&R-preferred standard, beyond the GCS/LOC/PTA severity grade.
    • CTE is a post-mortem diagnosis; TES (2021 NINDS) is a research clinical syndrome — counsel with honesty about uncertainty, don't disclose provisional CTE-certainty levels (nocebo), treat the modifiable.
    • Post-traumatic seizure prophylaxis is 7 days for early seizures in moderate–severe TBI — it does not prevent epilepsy.
