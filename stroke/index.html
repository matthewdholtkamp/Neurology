    Acute ischemic stroke¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: sudden focal neurologic deficit from arterial occlusion — "time is brain." Every minute of an untreated large-vessel occlusion loses ~1.9 million neurons (Saver's oft-cited MCA-territory estimate). Your job on the code is to move fast, in order: last-known-well → glucose → CT → blood pressure → reperfusion decision.

    The ER consult usually sounds like: "Older patient, weak on one side, started a couple hours ago — they're in the scanner now, no NIHSS yet, no labs." Your value is imposing the checklist on the chaos. Be fast and a little abrupt; it saves brain.

    🚩 The clock is the whole game — establish these first

    • Last known well (LKW), not "time found." The entire reperfusion algorithm keys off this number. Nail it from the patient, family, EMS, or phone records.
    • Check a glucose immediately — hypo/hyperglycemia is the great stroke mimic. It is the only mandatory lab before thrombolysis.
    • Get the patient to non-contrast CT now (+ CTA head/neck if no contraindication) — you are excluding hemorrhage and finding a large-vessel occlusion.
    • Don't over-treat blood pressure. In a possible reperfusion candidate the target is <185/110; otherwise leave it alone (permissive hypertension) unless >220/120.
    • Mimics: hypoglycemia, seizure/Todd's, complex migraine, Bell's palsy, functional, toxic-metabolic. But a focal deficit is a stroke until proven otherwise — don't stall the code chasing a mimic.

    Acute stroke checklist — what I want done, in order¶

    Code stroke — the sequence

    1. LKW + glucose — the two numbers that gate everything.
    2. Focused exam + NIHSS (compact scale below). Note anticoagulant/antiplatelet use.
    3. STAT non-contrast CT head; CTA head & neck (skip CTA only if true contrast contraindication — most kidneys tolerate it, don't wait on a creatinine in a code).
    4. Labs (don't wait on them except glucose): CBC/platelets, PT/INR/aPTT, BMP, troponin, type & screen, hCG if applicable; ECG + telemetry.
    5. Two large-bore IVs. Manage BP to <185/110 only if pursuing lytic (labetalol 10–20 mg IV or nicardipine gtt 5–15 mg/h).
    6. Reperfusion decision — thrombolysis? thrombectomy? both? (below). Consent in parallel, don't let it delay the bolus.
    7. Weigh the patient (dosing is weight-based; don't guess high).
    8. If LVO: activate neuro-interventional / arrange transfer immediately — in parallel with giving lytic ("drip and ship").

    Time goalposts (AHA/ASA Target: Stroke)

    Beat these clocks — every step is a race:

    Milestone Goal
    Door → stroke team / physician ≤10–15 min
    Door → CT initiated ≤25 min
    Door → CT read ≤45 min
    Door → needle (thrombolysis) ≤60 min (standard); ≤45 min aspirational
    Door → arterial puncture (EVT) ≤90 min (direct arrivals)
    Door-in–door-out (transfer for EVT) ≤60 min

    The published guideline standard is door-to-needle ≤60 min, with ≤45 min aspirational; some centers push a ≤30-min institutional goal, but that is not the guideline benchmark.

    Compact NIHSS (rapid field version)¶

    A fast, practical aid — not a substitute for the formal 15-item NIHSS you'll document.

    Domain Quick check
    1a–1c LOC Alert / drowsy / stupor; month + age; blink eyes + squeeze hand
    2 Gaze Eyes midline, partial gaze palsy, or forced deviation
    3 Visual fields Full / partial / complete / bilateral hemianopia
    4 Facial palsy Symmetric / minor / partial / complete
    5–6 Motor arm/leg Arm at 90° (10 s), leg at 30° (5 s): drift → falls → no movement, each limb
    7 Ataxia Finger-nose, heel-shin — out of proportion to weakness
    8 Sensory Pinprick: normal / mild / severe loss
    9 Language Aphasia: none / mild / severe / mute-global
    10 Dysarthria Normal / slurred / unintelligible
    11 Extinction/neglect None / one modality / profound

    NIHSS ≤3 = "minor"; ≥6 with cortical signs (gaze, aphasia, neglect) predicts a large-vessel occlusion — get the CTA and call for thrombectomy.

    Imaging & the reperfusion fork¶

    • Non-contrast CT: rules out hemorrhage; look for early ischemic change / ASPECTS.
    • CTA head & neck: finds the LVO (ICA, M1/M2, basilar) and any tandem/dissection.
    • CT perfusion (or MRI DWI/FLAIR): needed for extended-window decisions (late thrombolysis and 6–24 h thrombectomy) — quantifies core vs. salvageable penumbra.

    Two independent decisions, made in parallel — a patient can get both:

    Reperfusion — Step 1: IV thrombolysis¶

    IV thrombolysis — offer within 4.5 h of LKW (Class 1)

    Two agents, co-equal Class 1 within 4.5 h (2026 AHA/ASA); tenecteplase is the practical default:

    • Tenecteplase (TNK) 0.25 mg/kg IV, single bolus (max 25 mg) — one push, no infusion, no line to babysit; easier for transfers. Preferred where available.
    • Alteplase (tPA) 0.9 mg/kg IV (max 90 mg) — 10% as a bolus over 1 min, remainder over 60 min.

    Label vs. guideline — a 3-hour nuance for tenecteplase

    The FDA approved TNKase (tenecteplase) for AIS on 3 Mar 2025. The indication itself is broad — "acute ischemic stroke in adults," no time window stated — but the label's Recommended Dosage section says to "initiate treatment as soon as possible and within 3 hours after onset." So using TNK in the 3–4.5 h window is guideline-supported (2026 AHA/ASA, Class 1) but beyond the labeled dosing timing — fine to do, just know the distinction and document it. (Alteplase is similar: ≤3 h labeled, 3–4.5 h by guideline/ evidence.) The agents are clinically co-equal across 4.5 h; the labeling nuance is a timing footnote, not a reason to prefer one drug.

    Before you push, confirm the four: "Do I have the CT, the glucose, the blood pressure (<185/110), and the ECG?" Weight-based dose — weigh, don't eyeball.

    Absolute contraindications (do NOT give lytic)

    • Hemorrhage on CT, or extensive early infarct >⅓ MCA territory.
    • BP that won't come below 185/110 despite treatment.
    • Glucose <50 and deficit resolves with correction.
    • Platelets <100k, INR >1.7, elevated aPTT / therapeutic heparin ≤48 h, or a DOAC ≤48 h (unless specific reversal/normal assay).
    • Ischemic stroke, intracranial/spinal surgery, or serious head trauma within 3 months; known intracranial neoplasm (intra-axial), AVM, or aneurysm at risk; active internal bleeding; infective endocarditis; suspected aortic dissection.

    Historically 'absolute,' now RELATIVE (weigh case-by-case — the 2026 guideline liberalized these)

    Rapidly-improving or mild-but-disabling deficits, seizure at onset (if deficit is from the stroke, not the postictal state), major surgery <14 d, GI/GU bleed <21 d, recent MI, pregnancy/early postpartum, recent arterial puncture. Don't reflexively exclude — discuss with stroke/neuro.

    The "million-dollar presentation" (say this to consent/activate): "__-year-old with [risk factors] presents with acute [deficit/syndrome], LKW [time]. BP __, NIHSS __, glucose __, INR __, platelets __. CT shows [no bleed], CTA shows [occlusion/patent]. No lytic contraindication."

    Consent, in plain language (benefit is at 90 days, not at the bedside)

    Thrombolysis is the best chance of returning to independence after stroke — roughly a ~10% absolute increase in good outcome at 3 months. The risk is bleeding: symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage — historically ~6% with alteplase (NINDS definition) vs ~1% without, though contemporary series (and the stricter ECASS-III definition) run ~2–3%, including with tenecteplase. Part of that "6% → 2–3%" gap is definitional, not purely the drug. Overall mortality is not increased. The benefit is not seen immediately — it shows up over the following weeks.

    Extended-window thrombolysis (perfusion-guided) — the window now stretches to 24 h

    When >4.5 h or wake-up/unknown onset with a favorable perfusion or DWI–FLAIR mismatch. The evidence is now tenecteplase-based and reaches 24 h, including non-LVO patients:

    • Wake-up / unknown onset, ≤~9 h: DWI–FLAIR mismatch (WAKE-UP) or perfusion mismatch (EXTEND, 4.5–9 h from LKW) → lytic is reasonable.
    • 4.5–24 h, LVO without access to thrombectomy — TNK: TRACE-III (NEJM 2024) showed benefit for tenecteplase with perfusion mismatch (mRS 0–1: 33% vs 24%). This is the "thrombectomy isn't available/transfer is too far" scenario.
    • 4.5–24 h, non-LVO with perfusion mismatch — TNK: OPTION (JAMA 2026) showed benefit (mRS 0–1: 44% vs 34%, NNT 11) but with more sICH (2.8% vs 0%).
    • TNK-PLUS (JAMA 2026): tenecteplase bridging before EVT in the 4.5–24 h window.
    • Caveat: extended-window lysis raises sICH risk — reserve it for genuine mismatch and counsel accordingly. And if thrombectomy is immediately available for an LVO, do that — TIMELESS was negative for TNK when EVT was on hand (lysis didn't add benefit).

    Reperfusion — Step 2: endovascular thrombectomy (LVO)¶

    Mechanical thrombectomy — for large-vessel occlusion; give lytic too if eligible

    Thrombolysis and thrombectomy are not either/or — a lytic-eligible LVO patient gets both (don't wait to see if the lytic works). Activate neuro-IR / transfer the moment CTA shows an LVO.

    • Anterior LVO (ICA, M1 ± proximal M2), 0–6 h: thrombectomy, Class I (standard-window trials enrolled ASPECTS ≥6).
    • 6–16 h: Class I if DAWN or DEFUSE-3 clinical–imaging mismatch met (perfusion/DWI).
    • 16–24 h: Class IIa by DAWN criteria.
    • Large-core infarct — thrombectomy still helps, down to ASPECTS 3: the large-core trials (SELECT2, ANGEL-ASPECT, TENSION [to 12 h], TESLA) specifically enrolled ASPECTS 3–5 (some 0–5), and benefit held down to ASPECTS 3. The ATLAS individual-patient meta-analysis (Lancet 2026, 6 RCTs) is the definitive pooled evidence: NNT 4.2 for ≥1-point mRS shift, NNT 8.6 for functional independence, and reduced mortality (NNT 14.6). Don't write off a low-ASPECTS patient — get neuro-IR involved.
    • Basilar-artery occlusion: thrombectomy up to ~12 h (ATTENTION) and ~24 h (BAOCHE) (both 2023) — posterior strokes are devastating and under-treated; don't anchor on a low NIHSS.

    After reperfusion — the post-lytic order set¶

    Post-thrombolysis orders (first 24 h)

    • ICU/stroke-unit monitoring ≥24 h. Neuro checks + BP q15 min ×2 h → q30 min ×6 h → q1 h to complete 24 h.
    • Keep BP <180/105 ×24 h (labetalol pushes ± nicardipine gtt).
    • Hold all antithrombotics ×24 h — no aspirin/clopidogrel/heparin/prophylactic anticoagulation.
    • Avoid for 24 h: central lines, arterial sticks, NG tube, urinary catheter, LP (or any non-compressible puncture).
    • A statin is fine to start (high-intensity — see secondary prevention).
    • Repeat non-contrast CT at 24 h before starting antithrombotics. Any acute decline → STAT CT (think symptomatic hemorrhage).

    Symptomatic ICH after thrombolysis — stop and reverse

    Sudden decline, new headache, vomiting, or BP spike within 24 h:

    1. STOP the lytic infusion. STAT non-contrast CT.
    2. Labs: CBC/platelets, PT/INR/aPTT, fibrinogen, type & cross; repeat q2–4 h until stable.
    3. Cryoprecipitate 10 units IV (replaces fibrinogen — first-line); recheck fibrinogen, **goal

      150 mg/dL**, repeat as needed.

    4. Tranexamic acid 1 g IV (or aminocaproic acid) as antifibrinolytic.
    5. Platelets if thrombocytopenic; correct any coagulopathy.
    6. Neurosurgery for possible decompression/EVD; treat elevated ICP; frequent neuro checks.

    Blood pressure — the rules change by pathway¶

    Situation Target How
    Before thrombolysis <185/110 Labetalol 10–20 mg IV, or nicardipine 5–15 mg/h gtt
    After thrombolysis (24 h) <180/105 Same; nicardipine/clevidipine gtt if labile
    Post-thrombectomy, successful reperfusion Individualized, usually ≤180; do NOT drive SBP <140 Over-lowering a reperfused brain harms — see warning
    No reperfusion (permissive HTN) Treat only if >220/120, then lower ~15% Restart home meds ~24–48 h once stable

    Don't over-lower after a successful thrombectomy

    The 2025 ACC/AHA hypertension guideline carries a Class 3: Harm recommendation against lowering SBP <140 mmHg in the first 24–72 h after successful EVT reperfusion — intensive lowering worsened outcomes. Keep it controlled (≤180) but don't chase a low number. For an incompletely reperfused vessel, avoid over-lowering to preserve collateral flow.

    Not a reperfusion candidate? Start antiplatelet therapy¶

    Antiplatelet — after 24 h if lysed, or now if not lysed (and CT shows no bleed)

    • Aspirin 160–325 mg within 24–48 h (rectal/NG if dysphagic) — the one proven early drug for everyone not getting a lytic.
    • Minor stroke (NIHSS ≤3) or high-risk TIA (ABCD² ≥4): short-course DAPT. Start aspirin + clopidogrel (clopidogrel 300–600 mg load → 75 mg/day; ASA 81 mg) for 21 days, then single-agent — the benefit is concentrated in the first 21 days (CHANCE/POINT); longer DAPT just adds bleeding.
      • Ticagrelor + aspirin (THALES) is an alternative, useful with CYP2C19 loss-of-function (clopidogrel non-responders).

    Secondary prevention — start it before discharge¶

    Secondary prevention is owned on this admission and continued in clinic. The full mechanism-by-mechanism playbook lives on the Stroke prevention page — the essentials to start in-hospital:

    Start before discharge (every patient)

    • High-intensity statin — atorvastatin 80 mg (or rosuvastatin 20–40); target LDL <70 (add ezetimibe ± PCSK9). Start in hospital.
    • Antithrombotic matched to mechanism — single antiplatelet for most; 21-day DAPT for minor stroke/high-risk TIA (see above); DOAC for AF, started early (≤4 days, CATALYST); never reflexively combine antiplatelet + anticoagulant.
    • BP toward <130/80 once stable; diabetes (GLP-1/SGLT2i), smoking cessation, diet/ exercise, weight.
    • Determine the mechanism (cardioembolic / large-artery / small-vessel / ESUS) — it drives carotid revascularization, PFO closure <60, and prolonged rhythm monitoring. → full detail on the prevention page.
    • Inpatient basics: dysphagia screen before anything PO, DVT prophylaxis, early mobilization.

    TIA — a treated emergency, not "a pass"¶

    A TIA is a transient deficit without infarction but with a high, front-loaded early stroke risk — same-day work-up and immediately-started prevention. Full detail (ABCD², the work-up order set, 21-day DAPT, mechanism-directed therapy) is on the dedicated TIA page. In brief: ABCD²-stratify → MRI + vessel imaging + rhythm monitoring today → start antiplatelet/DAPT + statin now → treat the mechanism. Don't send a real TIA home without a plan.

    Supportive care & admission¶

    Stroke-unit admission bundle

    • Dysphagia screen before any PO (meds, food, water) — aspiration is a top early killer.
    • Glucose: avoid hypoglycemia and treat hyperglycemia — the 2026 guideline doesn't set a stroke-specific number; the general inpatient 140–180 target is reasonable (and SHINE showed no benefit from tight <110 control). Treat fever (worsens outcome); normoxia (O₂ only if <94%).
    • DVT prophylaxis — intermittent pneumatic compression immediately; pharmacologic prophylaxis once bleeding risk allows (and ≥24 h post-lytic).
    • Early mobilization + therapy (PT/OT/SLP); swallow eval; secondary-prevention teaching.
    • Telemetry ≥24–48 h hunting for AF.

    Disposition¶

    Stroke-unit or ICU (all post-lytic/post-thrombectomy). Transfer any LVO to a thrombectomy-capable center emergently. Admit TIAs or observe with an expedited same-day work-up — the early recurrence risk is real.

    Military medicine — stroke / cerebrovascular disease

    Framework — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 6130.03, DoDI 1332.18, the JTS Clinical Practice Guideline "Management of Stroke and Cerebrovascular Emergencies in the Deployed Setting" (03 Jul 2024), and service aeromedical policy before counseling.

    • Deployed setting: stroke in theater is a telemedicine + evacuation problem — thrombolysis and thrombectomy are rarely available forward. Follow the JTS deployed-stroke CPG: rapid recognition, glucose, CT if available, BP management, and urgent MEDEVAC to a capability.
    • Young-stroke work-up matters more here: service members skew young — pursue dissection (blast/blunt/neck trauma), PFO, hypercoagulable states, endocarditis, and drug use; the etiology drives both treatment and the board.
    • Deployability: an acute stroke/TIA is non-deployable during work-up and recovery; residual deficit or a high-risk unfixed source is deployment-limiting.
    • Profile (DA Form 3349): temporary during recovery; permanent for residual neuro/cognitive deficit or seizure.
    • Retention / MEB: DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2, para 5.26.b lists cerebrovascular conditions — (3) stroke and (6) recurrent TIA "unless the underlying etiology is identified and definitively treated" — as below retention standards when they persist despite treatment and impair duty (gating clause 5.26.a). The Army implements this at AR 40-501 para 3-31f (stroke, when residuals affect performance). So: define the etiology, treat it, and document NIHSS/mRS, deficits, and the secondary-prevention plan → DES (MEB/PEB) if residuals impair duty. A TIA with a fully identified and definitively treated cause can retain — the etiology work-up is what the standard turns on.
    • Aeromedical / special duty: a history of stroke/TIA is disqualifying for flying duty, and most secondary-prevention regimens (anticoagulation especially) are independently waiver-dependent. Confirm the exact standard for the class involved.

    TRICARE — secondary-prevention prescribing

    The acute drugs (tenecteplase, alteplase, thrombectomy) are inpatient — not formulary-gated. For outpatient secondary prevention, high-intensity statins, aspirin, and clopidogrel are generic/formulary; DOACs are covered (a preferred DOAC exists on the Uniform Formulary — verify the current preferred agent and any PA at the TRICARE Formulary Search, as DoD P&T revises tiers).

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    • The whole field is time + vessel. Two parallel decisions: thrombolysis (drug, ≤4.5 h or perfusion-selected later) and thrombectomy (large-vessel occlusion, out to 24 h). Eligible patients get both.
    • Tenecteplase has arrived (with a label caveat). The 2026 AHA/ASA guideline makes TNK 0.25 mg/kg and alteplase 0.9 mg/kg co-equal Class 1 within 4.5 h; TNK's single bolus is operationally simpler. Note the FDA label approves TNK only to 3 h — 3–4.5 h is guideline-based but off-label. Reteplase may be next: RAISE (2025) found reteplase (two 18 mg boluses) superior to alteplase for excellent 90-day outcome (79.5% vs 70.4%) without more sICH — not yet in guidelines, but a signal that the thrombolytic field is still moving.
    • Extended-window lysis now reaches 24 h — and it's tenecteplase. Beyond WAKE-UP/EXTEND (~9 h), TRACE-III (LVO without EVT access) and OPTION (non-LVO with mismatch) show TNK benefit out to 24 h with perfusion selection — at the cost of more sICH (OPTION 2.8% vs 0%). TNK-PLUS studies pre-EVT bridging. But if EVT is immediately available, do EVT — TIMELESS was negative for adding TNK.
    • Late thrombectomy windows opened up. DAWN and DEFUSE-3 established 6–24 h thrombectomy with mismatch; SELECT2, ANGEL-ASPECT, TENSION (≤12 h), TESLA extended it to large-core infarcts (ASPECTS 3–5, benefit down to 3); the ATLAS meta-analysis (Lancet 2026) pooled it (NNT 4.2 mRS shift, 8.6 independence, 14.6 mortality). ATTENTION/BAOCHE (2023) cover basilar occlusion.
    • Short DAPT, not long. For minor stroke / high-risk TIA, 21 days of aspirin+clopidogrel (CHANCE/POINT) beats aspirin alone; beyond 21 days you only add bleeding. THALES offers ticagrelor+ASA.
    • Anticoagulate AF early — it reduces recurrence. CATALYST (Lancet 2025; pooling ELAN/OPTIMAS/ TIMING) shows early DOAC ≤4 days lowers recurrent ischemic stroke/sICH at 30 days with no excess sICH — early is now preferred, not just tolerated. The 1-3-6-12 wait is out.
    • Post-EVT, don't over-lower BP. The 2025 ACC/AHA hypertension guideline flags SBP <140 in the first 24–72 h after successful reperfusion as Class 3: Harm.
    • Chase LDL <70 with a high-intensity statin (± ezetimibe/PCSK9); treat the mechanism, not just "a stroke."
