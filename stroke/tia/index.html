    Transient ischemic attack (TIA)¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: a transient focal neurologic deficit from brain/retinal ischemia without infarction — a warning shot, not a benign event. The stroke that follows is preventable, and the risk is front-loaded to the first 48 hours, so a TIA is a same-day emergency work-up, not a "come back to clinic next week."

    🚩 A TIA is a treated emergency — act in hours, not days

    • Definition is tissue-based: transient deficit with no infarction on imaging. If MRI shows a matching DWI lesion, it's a stroke (treat/secondary-prevent as such), not a TIA.
    • Highest recurrence is immediate — a real TIA carries a substantial 90-day stroke risk, concentrated in the first 2 days. Rapid work-up + prevention cuts that risk by up to ~80%.
    • Exclude mimics: seizure/Todd's, migraine aura (spreading, positive symptoms), hypoglycemia, syncope/pre-syncope, functional. TIA is sudden, maximal, focal, negative (loss of function).

    Rapid approach¶

    1. Confirm it's vascular and transient (sudden, focal, negative symptoms; fully resolved).
    2. Risk-stratify (ABCD² — below) to decide admit vs. expedited-outpatient.
    3. Same-day work-up — MRI, vessel imaging, ECG/telemetry, echo (order set).
    4. Start secondary prevention immediately — don't wait for the work-up to finish.
    5. Find the mechanism and treat it (AF → anticoagulate; carotid → revascularize).

    Risk-stratify — ABCD² (and beyond)¶

    ABCD² factor Points
    Age ≥60 1
    BP ≥140/90 at presentation 1
    Clinical: unilateral weakness 2 / speech disturbance without weakness 1 1–2
    Duration ≥60 min 2 / 10–59 min 1 1–2
    Diabetes 1

    2-day stroke risk rises with score (~1% at 0–3, ~4% at 4–5, ~8% at 6–7). ABCD²-I adds carotid stenosis ≥50% and a positive DWI (2 points each) — a positive DWI or a high score means higher risk → admit / urgent work-up.**

    Image everyone — don't let ABCD² gatekeep (the 2023 AHA/ASA ED statement retired it as a triage tool)

    Clinical scores lack the sensitivity/specificity to safely identify who can skip urgent evaluation — ~20% of patients with ABCD² <4 still had a serious finding (significant carotid stenosis, intracranial stenosis, or AF) on work-up. So the 2023 AHA/ASA ED-TIA statement says give every TIA patient urgent brain + neurovascular imaging regardless of score, and let imaging + mechanism drive disposition, not the number.

    • Never send home a crescendo/recurrent TIA, a carotid/AF source, or a positive DWI — high-risk regardless of score.
    • The Canadian TIA Score (incorporates AF, carotid stenosis, DWI) out-discriminates ABCD² (AUC ~0.70 vs ~0.60; validated in >7,600 patients) — a better tool if you use one.

    The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)¶

    1. Confirm TIA vs stroke vs mimic; get a glucose and a focused exam.
    2. Risk-stratify (ABCD², dual/crescendo pattern, known AF/carotid disease).
    3. Complete the same-day work-up (order set).
    4. Start antithrombotic + statin now (below) — before results are all back.
    5. Mechanism-directed prevention: AF → early DOAC; symptomatic carotid → revascularize within 2 weeks; ESUS → monitoring.
    6. Disposition: admit high-risk; otherwise a rapid-access TIA pathway with prevention started.

    Same-day work-up¶

    Order it all today (a rapid TIA pathway cuts recurrent stroke ~80%)

    • MRI brain (DWI) — up to half of clinical "TIAs" show infarction (reclassifies to stroke and changes prognosis). CT if MRI unavailable. A negative early DWI doesn't fully reassure — false-negative rates run ~22–32% when MRI is done within ~3–4.5 h; if suspicion is high, image after ~6 h or repeat.
    • Vessel imaging — CTA or MRA of head and neck — find symptomatic carotid stenosis and dissection/intracranial disease.
    • ECG + telemetry / prolonged monitoring — hunt for atrial fibrillation (extend monitoring if cryptogenic).
    • Echocardiography for a cardioembolic source; labs (glucose, lipids, CBC, coags, A1c).

    Start secondary prevention immediately¶

    Antithrombotic + statin — start today (don't wait for the work-up)

    • High-risk TIA (ABCD² ≥4) or minor stroke: 21-day DAPT. Aspirin + clopidogrel (clopidogrel 300–600 mg load → 75 mg/day; ASA 81 mg) for 21 days, then single agent — benefit is concentrated in the first 21 days (CHANCE/POINT); longer DAPT only adds bleeding.
      • DAPT is for the non-reperfusion, non-revascularization patient — the trials excluded those for whom thrombolysis, thrombectomy, or carotid revascularization was indicated. Sort out the reperfusion/carotid question first.
      • The window is wider than you think — up to 72 h. INSPIRES (NEJM 2023, China, n≈6,100) showed clopidogrel+aspirin started within 72 h still cut 90-day stroke (7.3% vs 9.2%) at the cost of more (still low) bleeding — a patient seen 1–3 days out can still benefit (Asian-population data; generalizability less certain, as with CHANCE).
      • Ticagrelor + aspirin (THALES) is an alternative — but flag the bleeding signal: it cut stroke/death (5.5% vs 6.6%) at the cost of more fatal bleeding/ICH (0.4% vs 0.1%). It's preferred in CYP2C19 loss-of-function carriers (CHANCE-2; LOF present in ~25% of White and ~60% of East Asian patients; benefit concentrated in the first week and in lower-risk carriers), if genotyping is available.
    • Lower-risk TIA: single antiplatelet — aspirin or clopidogrel (ASA-dipyridamole is no longer preferred/widely available).
    • High-intensity statin — atorvastatin 80 mg, target LDL <70 (TST/2021 AHA) — escalate ezetimibe → PCSK9 inhibitor to get there. (European ESC/EAS guidance targets <55 for very-high-risk ASCVD, which includes most atherosclerotic stroke/TIA — a stricter goal worth knowing.)
    • Blood pressure control toward <130/80 once stable.
    • Full detail + the mechanism-by-mechanism playbook: Stroke prevention page.

    Anticoagulate — a DOAC (over warfarin for non-valvular AF), and start early — after a TIA (no infarct) begin within a day or two. CATALYST (Lancet 2025, IPD meta-analysis, n=5,441) confirms early DOAC (≤4 days) even after ischemic stroke lowers the 30-day composite (2.1% vs 3.0%, OR 0.70) with no excess sICH — so don't sit on it. LAAO if anticoagulation is contraindicated.

    70–99% (or 50–69% in selected patients): revascularize within 2 weeks (CEA first-line; CAS/ TCAR in selected anatomy) + medical therapy — see the carotid disease page. (In low-to-intermediate-risk symptomatic or asymptomatic ≥50% stenosis, ECST-2 found no benefit of revascularization over optimized medical therapy — the benefit tracks predicted risk.)

    • Antiplatelet, NOT empiric anticoagulation — NAVIGATE ESUS and RE-SPECT ESUS were negative; don't anticoagulate (or use ticagrelor) empirically for ESUS.
    • Prolonged rhythm monitoring — an insertable cardiac monitor finds AF in ~12–15% at 12 months (vs ~2–5% with external monitors); worth it even in non-cryptogenic stroke (STROKE-AF: ~12%). Anticoagulate by device-AF duration thresholds (≥24 h + CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 → anticoagulate; <5 min → don't) — per the 2024 ACC arrhythmia-monitoring pathway; full detail on the prevention page.
    • PFO closure for age <60 with an otherwise-cryptogenic embolic event and high-risk PFO features (use the PASCAL classification).

    Disposition¶

    • Admit / observe with an expedited work-up for high-risk features: ABCD² ≥4, crescendo or dual TIA, known AF or symptomatic carotid stenosis, or a positive DWI.
    • Lower-risk patients can go through a rapid-access TIA clinic/pathway — only if same-day imaging and prevention are actually available; otherwise observe.
    • Either way, secondary prevention starts at first contact.
    • Structured follow-up pays off — a disease-management program (scheduled visits, risk-factor targets, support) cut major cardiovascular events at 12 months (STROKE-CARD: 5.4% vs 8.3%, HR 0.63). Book the follow-up and own the risk factors, don't just discharge on pills.

    Military medicine — TIA

    Framework — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 6130.03, and aeromedical policy.

    • Retention hinges on the work-up. DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.b(6) places recurrent TIA below retention standards unless the underlying etiology is identified and definitively treated (gated by 5.26.a); Army implements at AR 40-501 para 3-31f. So a single TIA with a fully worked-up, definitively-treated cause (e.g., carotid revascularized, AF anticoagulated) can retain — an unexplained or recurrent TIA trends toward the DES (MEB/PEB). Document the etiology work-up, the treatment, and duty impact.
    • Deployability: non-deployable during work-up; a treated single-cause TIA may return to duty per specialty review; anticoagulation for AF is itself deployment/special-duty-limiting.
    • Aeromedical: a history of TIA is disqualifying/waiver-dependent for flying duty.

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    • TIA is defined by tissue, not time — transient deficit without infarction; a DWI lesion makes it a stroke. ~Half of clinical TIAs are actually infarcts on MRI.
    • The danger is immediate — highest stroke risk in the first 48 h, so same-day imaging + started prevention; rapid TIA pathways cut recurrent stroke ~80%.
    • 21-day DAPT for high-risk TIA/minor stroke (CHANCE/POINT), then single agent — and the window extends to 72 h (INSPIRES), so a patient seen days out still benefits; ticagrelor+ASA / CHANCE-2 for CYP2C19 LOF. Add high-intensity statin to LDL <70; BP <130/80.
    • ABCD² is effectively retired as a triage gate (2023 AHA/ASA ED statement) — ~20% of ABCD²<4 had serious findings; image everyone, let imaging + mechanism decide. The Canadian TIA Score (AUC ~0.70 vs ~0.60) is the better tool. A negative early DWI (<3–4.5 h) can be falsely reassuring (~22–32% false-negative) — repeat/delay if suspicious.
    • Sort out reperfusion/carotid before DAPT — DAPT eligibility assumes thrombolysis/thrombectomy/ carotid revascularization is not indicated. Ticagrelor+ASA carries a fatal-bleeding/ICH signal (0.4% vs 0.1%) — not a free swap.
    • Treat the mechanism — early DOAC for AF (CATALYST), carotid revascularization within 2 weeks for high-risk symptomatic stenosis (benefit tracks predicted risk — ECST-2), no empiric anticoagulation for ESUS, ICM for occult AF, PFO closure <60. Full detail on the prevention page.
    • Structured follow-up works (STROKE-CARD) — book it; own the risk factors. Factor XIa inhibitors are the emerging lower-bleeding antithrombotic to watch.
