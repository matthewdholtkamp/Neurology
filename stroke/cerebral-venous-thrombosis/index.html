    Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (CVST)¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: thrombosis of the dural venous sinuses or cortical veins — the great mimic and the great miss. It hits young people (often women on estrogen or peripartum), presents most often as a new, worsening headache, and the counter-intuitive rule that saves brains is: you anticoagulate even when there's hemorrhage.

    🚩 Think CVST when the headache doesn't fit an artery

    Suspect it with:

    • New/progressive or thunderclap headache — especially with papilledema, seizures, encephalopathy, or focal deficits.
    • A hemorrhage or infarct that crosses arterial territories (bilateral, parasagittal, or temporal — venous distribution).
    • Isolated raised intracranial pressure (headache + papilledema + diplopia mimicking IIH).
    • Risk factors: pregnancy/postpartum, estrogen (OCP), thrombophilia, malignancy, head/neck infection (mastoiditis, sinusitis), dehydration, IBD.

    Get dedicated venous imaging — MRV or CTV. A "normal" non-contrast CT does not exclude it (look for a dense cord/sinus sign, or a bleed that doesn't fit an artery). For isolated cortical vein thrombosis (easily missed on standard MRV), use SWI or GRE sequences.

    D-dimer helps only in low-probability patients

    D-dimer sensitivity is ~90–97%, but ~10% of confirmed CVT has a normal D-dimer — especially isolated headache, subacute/chronic onset, or low thrombus burden. A normal D-dimer with a low clinical probability can help exclude CVT; in moderate/high-probability patients, image the veins regardless. Don't let a negative D-dimer stop the workup.

    Rapid approach¶

    1. Image the veins: MRV or CTV (the diagnostic test — don't rely on non-contrast CT or a negative D-dimer).
    2. Anticoagulate — even if there's venous hemorrhage (the bleed is from the clot; treating the clot helps).
    3. Treat complications: seizures, raised ICP / threatened vision, large venous infarct.
    4. Find the cause: pregnancy/estrogen, thrombophilia, infection, malignancy.
    5. Plan duration of anticoagulation and counsel on estrogen avoidance / future pregnancy.

    The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)¶

    1. Confirm on MRV/CTV (± MRI showing venous infarct/hemorrhage).
    2. Start therapeutic anticoagulation now — LMWH preferred (even with hemorrhage present).
    3. Admit; treat seizures and raised ICP; neurosurgery on standby for a large infarct.
    4. Transition to oral anticoagulation and set the duration by provoked vs unprovoked/thrombophilia.
    5. Work up etiology; stop estrogen; counsel future-pregnancy prophylaxis.

    Acute treatment¶

    Anticoagulate — yes, even with hemorrhage

    • Therapeutic LMWH (e.g., enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SC q12h) is first-line acutely — preferred over unfractionated heparin (better outcomes) unless renal failure or likely need for rapid reversal/procedures favors UFH infusion.
    • Intracranial hemorrhage from the CVST is NOT a contraindication — the venous congestion that caused the bleed improves with anticoagulation. This is the point everyone finds counter-intuitive; do it anyway.
    • Endovascular thrombectomy/thrombolysis is NOT routine — TO-ACT was negative. Reserve it for clinical deterioration despite adequate anticoagulation.

    Manage the complications

    • Seizures: treat clinical seizures; give an ASM if there's a supratentorial lesion + seizure (high recurrence). No routine prophylaxis without a lesion/seizure.
    • Raised ICP / papilledema threatening vision: acetazolamide, therapeutic LP, monitor visual fields/acuity; shunt or optic-nerve-sheath fenestration if vision is failing.
    • Large venous infarct with mass effect / herniation: decompressive craniectomy is life-saving — operate EARLY (within ~48 h); delay worsens outcomes. DECOMPRESS2 (n=118): ~36% independent (mRS 0–2) and ~34% mortality at 12 months, and ~79% of survivors judged surgery worthwhile — so offer it even to severe presentations. Coma and fixed dilated pupils predict poor outcome.

    VITT-CVT — a different disease; do NOT give heparin

    If CVST follows an adenoviral COVID vaccine with thrombocytopenia (and a positive anti-PF4 ELISA) — vaccine-induced immune thrombotic thrombocytopenia. Now rare, but the management is opposite to reflex:

    • Avoid all heparin — use a non-heparin anticoagulant (argatroban or fondaparinux).
    • IVIG 1 g/kg × 2 days (± corticosteroids) — immunomodulation lowers mortality (≈29% vs 70%).
    • Do NOT transfuse platelets (fuels thrombosis).
    • Involve hematology urgently.

    Transition & duration¶

    Oral anticoagulation and how long

    • Transition to an oral anticoagulant once stable. DOACs are now a reasonable alternative to warfarin for non-VITT, non-antiphospholipid CVST (2024 AHA statement) — the evidence base has grown well beyond dabigatran: DOAC-CVT (Lancet Neurol 2025, prospective, n=619) and ACTION-CVT (Stroke 2022, n=845) show similar recurrence with equal or less bleeding vs VKA. Apixaban is now the most commonly used agent in practice; dabigatran (RE-SPECT CVT) and rivaroxaban (SECRET) are also studied. Use warfarin for antiphospholipid syndrome (DOACs underperform there).
    • Duration:
      • Provoked (transient factor, e.g., estrogen): 3–6 months.
      • Unprovoked: 6–12 months.
      • Recurrent VTE or severe thrombophilia / antiphospholipid: indefinite.
    • Stratify by recurrence risk when choosing duration. Overall recurrent VTE is ~5 per 100 patient-years (cumulative ~8% at 2 y, ~12% at 5 y). Highest-risk → favor longer/indefinite anticoagulation: antiphospholipid antibodies (aHR ~3.9), prior VTE (aHR ~3.4), active cancer, and idiopathic CVT. (EXCOA-CVT, ongoing, is the first RCT of short vs long duration — until then, duration is expert-consensus.)
    • Stop estrogen-containing contraception permanently; in future pregnancies, give LMWH prophylaxis through pregnancy and postpartum (highest-risk window).

    TRICARE — CVST prescribing

    Enoxaparin (LMWH), warfarin, and acetazolamide are generic/formulary. DOACs are covered — apixaban is the most-used agent in practice (DOAC-CVT/ACTION-CVT), and a preferred DOAC exists on the Uniform Formulary. Verify the current preferred agent and any PA at the TRICARE Formulary Search.

    Long-term outcomes & follow-up¶

    Most patients do well functionally — ~80–90% reach mRS 0–2 — but the mRS hides a real chronic burden, so counsel and follow accordingly:

    • Common residual symptoms: chronic headache, fatigue, cognitive impairment, and mood disturbance. ~42% had not returned to work/school at 6 months despite most being "independent" — ask about function, not just the mRS.
    • Epilepsy develops in >10% — higher with seizures at onset, hemorrhagic lesions, superior sagittal sinus involvement, or craniectomy.
    • Repeat venous imaging (MRV/CTV) to document recanalization and guide duration.
    • Dural arteriovenous fistula is a rare late complication (~2%) — consider it for new pulsatile tinnitus or worsening headache.
    • Heavy menstrual bleeding on anticoagulation is a common, adherence-limiting problem in young women — address it proactively (gynecology; it is not a reason to silently stop the DOAC).

    Disposition¶

    Admit (stroke unit / ICU if depressed consciousness, large infarct, or refractory seizures/ICP). Neurosurgery available for decompression. Outpatient hematology/thrombophilia work-up and anticoagulation follow-up; repeat venous imaging to document recanalization.

    Military medicine — cerebral venous thrombosis

    Framework — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 6130.03, DoDI 1332.18, and aeromedical policy before counseling.

    • Demographics fit the force: young service members, estrogen contraception, dehydration (field/heat), and peripartum are all in play — keep CVST on the differential for a service member with a new persistent headache and papilledema.
    • Deployability: acute CVST and the anticoagulation that follows are deployment-limiting; estrogen contraception should be changed after CVST.
    • Retention / MEB: CVST isn't named verbatim, but its consequences are — a venous infarct/hemorrhage and any persistent sequelae (severe headaches, seizures, sensory/motor or cognitive deficit) fall under DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.b/5.26.c (cerebrovascular / CNS sequelae, gated by 5.26.a; Army AR 40-501 3-31f/k). Residual deficit, seizures, or a thrombophilia requiring indefinite anticoagulation generally triggers the DES (MEB/PEB). Document etiology, imaging, anticoagulation plan, and duration.
    • Aeromedical: CVST history, seizures, and chronic anticoagulation are disqualifying/ waiver-dependent for flying duty.

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    • Anticoagulate through the hemorrhage. The venous bleed is a consequence of the clot; therapeutic LMWH (preferred over UFH) is first-line even when ICH is present — the single most counter-intuitive, most important teaching point.
    • Diagnose with venous imaging (MRV/CTV). Non-contrast CT and D-dimer miss it (D-dimer normal in ~10%, esp. isolated headache/subacute) — image low- or high-probability patients. SWI/GRE for isolated cortical vein thrombosis.
    • DOACs are now a reasonable alternative for non-VITT/non-antiphospholipid CVST — the evidence tripled with DOAC-CVT (2025) and ACTION-CVT; apixaban is the most-used agent, with dabigatran (RE-SPECT CVT) and rivaroxaban (SECRET) also studied. Warfarin for antiphospholipid.
    • Recurrence ~5/100 patient-years — longer/indefinite anticoagulation for antiphospholipid, prior VTE, cancer, or idiopathic; EXCOA-CVT (ongoing) will finally test duration.
    • VITT-CVT is a different disease — no heparin, IVIG + argatroban/fondaparinux, no platelets.
    • Endovascular therapy isn't routine — TO-ACT negative. Decompressive craniectomy is life-saving for a herniating venous infarct — operate early (DECOMPRESS2: ~36% independent, most survivors judged it worthwhile).
    • The chronic burden is under-appreciated — headache, fatigue, cognitive/mood symptoms, epilepsy (>10%), and a large return-to-work gap despite good mRS. Follow with repeat imaging and QoL attention.
