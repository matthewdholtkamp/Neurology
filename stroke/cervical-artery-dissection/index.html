    Cervical artery dissection¶

    Last reviewed: July 2026

    One-liner: a tear in the wall of the carotid or vertebral artery in the neck — the leading cause of stroke in the young and middle-aged. The tell is pain first, ischemia later: neck/head pain (± Horner's or pulsatile tinnitus) that precedes a TIA or stroke by hours to days — a window where recognition prevents the stroke.

    🚩 Young patient + neck/head pain + a local sign → image the vessels

    Suspect dissection with:

    • New neck or head/face pain (often unilateral) after trauma — even trivial (MVC, sports, neck manipulation), heavy lifting, or spontaneously.
    • Local signs before the stroke: partial Horner's (ptosis + miosis, carotid), pulsatile tinnitus, a lower-cranial-nerve palsy, or posterior neck pain (vertebral).
    • Then ischemia — TIA/stroke in the corresponding territory, classically hours to days later.
    • Vascular risk clues: fibromuscular dysplasia, connective-tissue disease (vascular Ehlers-Danlos, Marfan, Loeys-Dietz), family history — and hypertension (genetically higher BP, especially diastolic, is a likely causal risk factor, with a larger effect than for non-dissection stroke).

    Get dedicated vessel imaging now — you're trying to catch it in the pain-before-stroke window.

    Diagnose¶

    Imaging — find the intramural hematoma / flap

    • CTA neck+head or MRI/MRA with fat-saturation — look for the crescent sign (intramural hematoma), tapered ("flame") occlusion, or a pseudoaneurysm. Fat-sat MRI is especially good for the mural hematoma.
    • DSA is the historical gold standard — reserve for ambiguous cases or planned intervention.
    • Look for fibromuscular dysplasia — present in ~6–14% of cervical dissections (up to ~40% have FMD in ≥1 arterial bed on whole-body CTA), and FMD raises recurrent-dissection risk — so image the other vessels, not just the culprit.

    Connective-tissue / genetics screening — how far to go

    A monogenic connective-tissue disorder (vascular EDS, Marfan, Loeys-Dietz, osteogenesis imperfecta) is found in <1% of dissections, but >50% have subtle ultrastructural connective-tissue changes on skin biopsy. Refer to genetics for a suspected syndrome, multiple/recurrent dissections, or familial cases. (The PHACTR1 locus links FMD, migraine, and dissection — a shared arterial-wall biology.)

    The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)¶

    1. Suspect it in any young/middle-aged stroke or unexplained neck pain + local sign.
    2. Confirm on CTA or fat-sat MRI/MRA.
    3. Antithrombotic — risk-stratify (radiographic risk vs. bleeding risk), start early, for 3–6 months (below).
    4. Acute stroke from dissection? Standard reperfusion still applies (thrombolysis/thrombectomy).
    5. Re-image at ~3–6 months; stop or step down based on healing and residual risk.
    6. Counsel on trauma/manipulation avoidance; control blood pressure; screen for connective-tissue disease / FMD.

    Treatment¶

    Antithrombotic — RISK-STRATIFY (start early); it's no longer a simple coin-flip

    The RCTs (CADISS, TREAT-CAD) were underpowered and showed no clear winner, but larger data now favor tailoring (2024 AHA Scientific Statement). A meta-analysis including STOP-CAD (n≈5,000) found anticoagulation lowered ischemic stroke (RR ~0.63) but roughly doubled major bleeding (RR ~2.25) — so match the drug to the patient:

    • Favor anticoagulation (heparin/LMWH → DOAC or VKA) when high-risk radiographic features + low bleeding risk: occlusive dissection, intraluminal/free-floating thrombus, or severe stenosis. (STOP-CAD: only occlusive dissections had a stroke benefit from anticoagulation, aHR ~0.36; the CADISS+TREAT-CAD IPD meta-analysis: benefit only in those without a dissecting aneurysm.)
    • Favor an antiplatelet when lower-risk features or elevated bleeding risk: a large infarct, hemorrhagic transformation, or intradural (intracranial) extension. If antiplatelet: a short course of DAPT (21–90 days) → single antiplatelet is reasonable (analogous to minor-stroke/TIA data; ~44% of CADISS patients got dual).
    • If you anticoagulate, prefer a DOAC over warfarin (more convenient, likely better benefit–risk per the TREAT-CAD investigators — though direct data are small and no RCT exists), and consider switching to an antiplatelet before ~180 days to cut late bleeding risk.
    • Start early — recurrent ischemic events cluster in the first weeks (~87% within 30 days).
    • Intracranial dissection (higher SAH risk) is managed differently — individualize with neurology/neurosurgery.

    Recurrence is uncommon and usually benign (STOP-CAD 2026)

    2-year recurrent-dissection rate ~3.2%, median time ~53 days (~half within 2 months); predictors = younger age, migraine, FMD (presenting with stroke actually predicted lower recurrence). Reassuringly, only ~5 of 88 recurrences caused a new ischemic event. Predictors of a subsequent ischemic stroke by day 180: infarct on baseline imaging (aHR ~9.9), prior stroke (~7.3), presentation <7 days (~3.0), occlusive dissection (~2.3).

    If it presents as an acute stroke — reperfuse normally

    • Extracranial dissection is NOT a contraindication to IV thrombolysis — treat an acute ischemic stroke by the usual reperfusion algorithm.
    • Thrombectomy for a large-vessel occlusion from the dissection.
    • Acute carotid stenting for a dissection-related tandem occlusion is not routine: pooled TITAN/ETIS registry data showed higher reperfusion (89% vs 68%) but no better 90-day outcome (and no excess sICH) — the acute-stenting benefit was seen in atherosclerotic, not dissection, tandem lesions. (The randomized TITAN trial is ongoing.) Reserve elective stenting for refractory ischemia despite medical therapy or an enlarging/symptomatic pseudoaneurysm.

    Disposition¶

    Admit acute presentations (stroke unit; monitor for extension/ischemia). Prognosis is generally good — recurrence is low and most heal. Outpatient neurology follow-up with repeat vessel imaging and connective-tissue evaluation where indicated.

    Military medicine — cervical artery dissection

    Framework — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 6130.03, and aeromedical policy.

    • Fits the force: young service members, trauma/blast, load-bearing, combatives, and neck manipulation are all mechanisms — keep dissection high on the differential for a service member with neck/head pain and any focal sign.
    • Retention: a dissection that causes stroke/TIA is a cerebrovascular condition under DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.b (stroke b(3); recurrent TIA b(6) unless etiology identified and definitively treated), Army AR 40-501 3-31f — a healed dissection with a treated cause and no residual deficit may retain per specialty review; residual deficit or recurrence trends to the DES.
    • Deployability / aeromedical: non-deployable during work-up/treatment; anticoagulation and a stroke history are deployment- and flight-status-limiting/waiver-dependent.

    📚 Discover — evidence brief¶

    • The #1 cause of stroke in the young — and the pain precedes the stroke, so recognition in the neck-pain window is prevention.
    • Diagnose with fat-sat MRI/MRA or CTA (crescent sign / intramural hematoma); DSA only if ambiguous.
    • Antithrombotic choice is now risk-stratified, not a coin-flip (2024 AHA Scientific Statement). The RCTs (CADISS/TREAT-CAD) were underpowered; larger data (STOP-CAD, IPD meta-analysis) show anticoagulation lowers ischemic stroke (~RR 0.63) but ~doubles major bleeding — so anticoagulate high-risk radiographic features (occlusive dissection, intraluminal thrombus, no dissecting aneurysm) with low bleeding risk, and use an antiplatelet (± short DAPT) for lower-risk/high-bleed patients. Prefer a DOAC if anticoagulating; consider stepping to an antiplatelet by ~180 days.
    • Start early — recurrent ischemic events cluster in the first 30 days.
    • Don't withhold thrombolysis/thrombectomy for an acute stroke from an extracranial dissection; acute stenting of a dissection tandem occlusion isn't routine (TITAN/ETIS: more reperfusion, no better outcome).
    • Recurrence is uncommon (~3% at 2 y) and usually benign (STOP-CAD 2026); predictors = young age, migraine, FMD. Image all vessels for FMD (~6–14%); refer genetics for syndromic/recurrent/familial.
    • Control blood pressure — likely a causal risk factor, not just connective tissue.

    Key sources: 2024 AHA Scientific Statement on cervical artery dissection (Yaghi et al.); CADISS (2015) & TREAT-CAD (2021); STOP-CAD (Stroke 2024) + secondary analyses (2025/2026); CADISS+TREAT-CAD IPD meta-analysis (JAMA Neurol 2024); FMD (JAMA Netw Open 2025); TITAN/ETIS (tandem occlusion); JACC Mendelian-randomization (BP causal).
