# Botulism

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** a **presynaptic toxin** that cleaves the machinery of acetylcholine release,
producing **symmetric, descending, flaccid paralysis** with prominent bulbar and autonomic features
— and a normal sensorium throughout. The treatment is **antitoxin, given on suspicion**, plus
ventilatory support.

**Who this is for:** the ED or ICU provider facing acute descending weakness. Botulism is rare,
time-critical, and a **reportable public-health and potential-bioterrorism event** — recognizing it
fast is the entire game.

!!! danger "🚩 Recognize it and treat before you confirm"
    **The classic picture:** an afebrile, alert patient with **acute, symmetric, descending
    paralysis** that starts with the **bulbar "4 D's" — diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia,
    dysphonia** — plus **dilated, poorly reactive pupils**, dry mouth, and progressive limb
    weakness, **with normal sensation and normal mentation.**

    - **No fever** (unless from a wound or aspiration), **no sensory loss**, **preserved
      consciousness** — these separate it from stroke, GBS, and encephalitis.
    - **Descending** (cranial nerves → arms → trunk → legs) and **symmetric** — the opposite of
      Guillain-Barré's ascending pattern.
    - **Antitoxin does not reverse established paralysis — it halts progression.** Every hour of
      delay is more toxin bound. **Do not wait for laboratory confirmation**, which takes days.
    - **Call your state health department / CDC immediately** — antitoxin is released only through
      public health, and reporting is mandatory.

## Forms — the route changes the antitoxin

| Form | Source | Key points |
|---|---|---|
| **Foodborne** | Preformed toxin — home-canned/fermented foods | Outbreak potential; **investigate co-exposed contacts** |
| **Wound** | *C. botulinum* in a wound producing toxin in vivo | **Injection drug use, esp. black-tar heroin.** No GI prodrome; **needs antibiotics + debridement** in addition to antitoxin (avoid aminoglycosides) |
| **Infant** | Spore colonization of the gut (honey, soil/dust) | **The "floppy baby"** — constipation, poor feeding, weak cry, hypotonia. **Treat with BabyBIG, not equine antitoxin** |
| **Iatrogenic** | Cosmetic/therapeutic botulinum toxin overdose | Usually mild/localized, occasionally systemic |
| **Inhalational** | Aerosolized toxin | **Not natural — implies bioterrorism.** Botulinum toxin is a **CDC Category A select agent** |

## Diagnosis

- **Clinical and immediate** — the syndrome above drives treatment; confirmation is retrospective.
- **Confirmatory:** toxin assay (mouse bioassay) and culture from **serum, stool, gastric contents,
  wound, or food** — coordinated through public health.
- **Electrodiagnostics** can support (facilitation on high-frequency RNS, like a presynaptic
  disorder) and help exclude mimics.
- **Rule out the mimics actively:** Guillain-Barré / Miller Fisher (ascending, areflexia,
  albuminocytologic dissociation), myasthenic crisis, brainstem stroke, tick paralysis,
  organophosphate poisoning (which is the **opposite** autonomic picture — wet, not dry).

## The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Recognize** acute, symmetric, descending, afebrile flaccid paralysis with bulbar onset and
   clear sensorium.
2. **Protect the airway** — serial FVC and bulbar assessment; **intubate early** for respiratory or
   bulbar failure. This is the intervention that saves lives.
3. **Call public health NOW** for antitoxin release and reporting — in parallel with airway care.
4. **Give antitoxin on clinical suspicion** — do not wait for confirmation.
5. **Address the source** — wound debridement + antibiotics for wound botulism; supportive care.
6. **Report and investigate** — outbreak and bioterrorism implications.

## Treatment

!!! orderset "Step 1 — airway and supportive care"
    - **Serial FVC, negative inspiratory force, and bulbar assessment.** **Intubate early** — many
      patients need prolonged mechanical ventilation (weeks to months); recovery requires
      regeneration of nerve terminals.
    - Meticulous **ICU supportive care** — the paralysis is survivable with good support and is
      largely reversible over time.

!!! orderset "Step 2 — antitoxin, as fast as possible"
    - **Adults and children >1 year: heptavalent botulinum antitoxin (HBAT/BAT, equine)** — covers
      toxin types A–G. Released through the **state health department / CDC.**
    - **Infants: human-derived botulism immune globulin (BabyBIG)** — obtained from the **Infant
      Botulism Treatment and Prevention Program (California Dept of Public Health)**. **Do not use
      equine antitoxin in infants.**
    - **Antitoxin binds circulating toxin and stops further progression; it does not reverse
      paralysis already established** — which is exactly why speed matters.

!!! warning "Wound botulism specifics"
    Add **antibiotics and surgical debridement** — but **avoid aminoglycosides and clindamycin**,
    which impair neuromuscular transmission and can worsen the weakness. Penicillin/metronidazole
    are typical choices. Give **antitoxin first**, then source control.

      disposition.** Botulinum toxin is a **Category A biological threat agent**; an
      **inhalational or clustered presentation in a deployed or garrison setting must trigger the
      CBRN and force-health-protection chain immediately**, not just individual treatment.
    - **Reporting is mandatory** — to the servicing MTF, public health emergency officer, and
      through medical intelligence channels. A single case can be a sentinel event.
    - **Recovery and retention:** most survivors recover fully over weeks to months, so the usual
      disposition is a **temporary profile through convalescence and rehabilitation**, with
      **retention determined by residual deficit** under AR 40-501 3-31 if recovery is incomplete —
      not by the acute episode itself.
    - **Deployability:** non-deployable during the acute illness and prolonged recovery; reassess
      once ventilator-independent and strength has returned.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

**One of the most potent toxins known, with a precise molecular target.** Botulinum neurotoxin is
taken up by cholinergic nerve terminals, where its light chain is a **protease that cleaves SNARE
proteins** (SNAP-25, syntaxin, synaptobrevin) — the fusion machinery that docks acetylcholine
vesicles to the presynaptic membrane. With SNARE cleaved, **no acetylcholine is released**, and the
muscle is chemically denervated. Recovery is not a matter of clearing the toxin; it requires the
terminal to **sprout and rebuild new release sites**, which is why paralysis lasts weeks to months
even after antitoxin. The same mechanism, dosed and localized, is what makes botulinum toxin a
therapeutic — from [chronic migraine](../headache/migraine.md) to [cervical dystonia](index.md) —
and what makes MG a **contraindication** to receiving it.

**Why "treat on suspicion" is not just caution.** Antitoxin can only neutralize toxin that is still
**circulating and unbound.** Once toxin has entered the nerve terminal, no antibody can retrieve it.
Every hour of diagnostic delay converts reversible circulating toxin into fixed, bound toxin — so
the therapeutic window is measured against the clock, and the decision to treat is made clinically,
before any test returns.
