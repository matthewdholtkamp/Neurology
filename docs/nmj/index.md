# Myasthenia gravis

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** an antibody-mediated disorder of the **postsynaptic** neuromuscular junction —
**fatigable, fluctuating weakness** that worsens with use and through the day and recovers with
rest. Ocular, bulbar, limb, and — the one that kills — respiratory muscles.

**Who this is for:** the provider seeing fluctuating diplopia/ptosis or dysphagia in clinic, and
the one facing a patient in **myasthenic crisis** in the ED. MG is a real active-duty diagnosis and
a named referral condition under AR 40-501 3-31c — but the ocular-only carve-out means the
disposition is not automatic.

!!! danger "🚩 Myasthenic crisis — respiratory failure is the emergency"
    **The threat is the diaphragm, and pulse oximetry will reassure you right up until the patient
    crashes.** Hypoxia is a late sign; hypercapnic fatigue comes first.

    **Assess ventilation directly, not with the pulse ox:**

    - **Single-breath count** — ask the patient to count out loud on one breath. Normal reaches
      ~30; **cannot reach 20 = significant respiratory muscle weakness.**
    - **Forced vital capacity (FVC)** — **<15–20 mL/kg** signals impending failure and probable
      intubation. Trend it.
    - **Negative inspiratory force** and rising **PaCO₂**.
    - **Bulbar failure** — can't handle secretions, weak cough, nasal voice — is an independent
      reason to secure the airway.

    **BiPAP can buy time early but fails fast** in true respiratory-muscle fatigue — do not let it
    delay intubation in a tiring patient. **If you are asking whether to intubate, intubate.**

    !!! warning "Two intubation traps specific to MG"
        - **Succinylcholine behaves unpredictably** (altered receptor pharmacology) and
          **non-depolarizing blockers have prolonged, exaggerated effect** — MG patients are
          exquisitely sensitive. Warn anesthesia; expect a reduced-dose, carefully titrated
          approach.
        - **Do not give IV magnesium** to a crashing MG patient (e.g. for a coincident indication)
          — it blocks neuromuscular transmission and can precipitate or deepen crisis.

## Rapid approach

1. **Recognize the pattern** — fatigable, fluctuating, fatigues with use, worse later in the day,
   spares sensation and reflexes.
2. **Protect the airway first** if bulbar or respiratory weakness is present — everything else waits.
3. **Send serology** — AChR, then MuSK if negative (see below).
4. **Confirm** with the pattern + serology; use EDx (RNS, SFEMG) when serology is negative.
5. **Image the mediastinum** (CT/MRI chest) — **every MG patient needs a thymoma screen.**
6. **Treat** — symptomatic (pyridostigmine) + immunotherapy; crisis gets IVIG or PLEX.
7. **Screen for precipitants** — infection, and the long list of drugs that worsen MG.
8. **Start the military disposition** — profile, and the ocular-vs-generalized determination.

## Diagnosis

**The clinical signature is fatigability.** Sustained upgaze reproduces ptosis; repeated
abduction fatigues an eye; proximal limbs weaken on repetition. **Ptosis and diplopia** are the
commonest presenting features. **Sensation, reflexes, and pupils are normal** — a fixed pupil or
sensory loss argues against MG and for a mimic.

### Serology — send in this order

| Antibody | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **AChR (acetylcholine receptor)** | ~**85%** of generalized MG | First test. Highly specific. Titre does not track severity |
| **MuSK (muscle-specific kinase)** | ~**5–8%** | Send if AChR-negative. **Distinct phenotype** — prominent bulbar/facial/neck/respiratory weakness, and **different treatment** (see below) |
| **LRP4** | ~1–3% | Send in double-seronegative patients |
| **Seronegative** | remainder | Especially **ocular-only** MG. A negative panel does not exclude MG — lean on EDx |

!!! tip "When serology is negative — electrodiagnostics"
    - **Repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS)** — a **decremental response** (>10% CMAP decrement at
      low-frequency stim) supports MG. Widely available; lower sensitivity in purely ocular disease.
    - **Single-fiber EMG (SFEMG)** — **increased jitter**, the most sensitive test for MG (>95%),
      but non-specific and operator-dependent. The test to get when RNS and serology are negative
      but suspicion is high.
    - The **ice-pack test** (ptosis improves ≥2 mm after 2 min of ice) is a useful, cheap bedside
      adjunct for ptosis — not a substitute for confirmation.

!!! warning "Every MG patient needs a chest CT/MRI"
    **10–15% of MG patients have a thymoma**, and its presence changes management independently of
    the MG — a thymoma is a surgical indication **regardless of MG severity or serostatus.**
    Conversely, thymoma essentially never accompanies **MuSK** disease. Image the mediastinum in
    everyone at diagnosis.

### Ocular vs generalized — the distinction that drives everything

- **Ocular MG** — weakness confined to lids and extraocular muscles. Often seronegative.
- **~50% of ocular MG generalizes**, usually **within the first 1–2 years.** After 2 years of
  purely ocular disease, the risk of generalizing falls substantially.
- This split is the hinge of both **treatment intensity** and the **military disposition** — the
  regulations disqualify generalized MG but retain disease *confined to the extraocular muscles.*

## The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Is the airway safe?** Bulbar or respiratory weakness → assess ventilation directly, admit,
   treat as crisis. Nothing below matters until this is answered.
2. **Confirm the diagnosis** — pattern + serology, EDx if seronegative.
3. **Screen the mediastinum** — chest CT/MRI for thymoma.
4. **Start symptomatic control** — pyridostigmine (less useful in MuSK disease).
5. **Start immunotherapy** — most generalized patients need it; steroids + a steroid-sparing agent.
6. **Decide on thymectomy** — yes for thymoma (any), and for non-thymomatous **AChR-positive
   generalized** MG (MGTX). **Not** for MuSK disease.
7. **Escalate to targeted biologics** when refractory or steroid-dependent — FcRn, complement, or
   anti-CD19.
8. **Hand the patient a precipitant-drug list** and address the military disposition.

## Myasthenic crisis — acute management

!!! orderset "Step 1 — airway and monitoring"
    - **Admit to an ICU/step-down** anyone with a crisis or impending crisis.
    - **Serial FVC and single-breath count** — every few hours; trend beats snapshot.
    - **Intubate** for FVC <15 mL/kg, rising CO₂, bulbar failure with secretions, or clinical
      fatigue. **Do not wait for hypoxia.**
    - **Hold pyridostigmine** in intubated crisis patients — it increases secretions and adds
      little on the ventilator; resume as they recover.
    - **Find and treat the precipitant** — infection is the commonest; also review every new drug
      (see the avoid list).

!!! orderset "Step 2 — rapid immunotherapy: IVIG or PLEX"
    Both work; there is no clear efficacy winner, so choose on access and the patient.

    - **IVIG 2 g/kg** over **2–5 days.** Easier logistically, no vascular access issue. Response in
      **3–5 days.**
    - **Plasma exchange — 5 exchanges over 7–10 days** (1–1.5 plasma volumes each). Often felt to
      work slightly faster; **preferred in severe/fulminant crisis** and in **MuSK disease**, where
      PLEX is particularly effective.
    - Either controls **~60–70%** of severe cases.
    - **Start or intensify chronic immunotherapy in parallel** — IVIG/PLEX is a bridge, not a
      maintenance plan. Steroids are usually begun or increased now (watch the early-worsening
      window, below).

!!! warning "Steroid early-worsening in crisis"
    High-dose corticosteroids can cause **transient worsening in the first 1–2 weeks** — a real
    hazard in a patient already near respiratory failure. In severe disease, **cover with IVIG or
    PLEX first (or concurrently)** and let the rapid therapy take hold before, or alongside,
    pushing steroids up.

## Chronic treatment

### Symptomatic

!!! orderset "Pyridostigmine — symptom control, not disease modification"
    - **Pyridostigmine 30–60 mg PO every 4–6 h**, titrated to symptoms (typical ceiling ~360–480
      mg/day; cholinergic side effects limit higher).
    - Muscarinic side effects — cramping, diarrhea, secretions — often need **glycopyrrolate** or
      hyoscyamine cover.
    - **Less effective, and often poorly tolerated, in MuSK disease** — do not lean on it there.
    - It treats symptoms only. Most generalized patients also need immunotherapy.

### Immunotherapy tiers

=== "First-line immunotherapy"

    - **Corticosteroids (prednisone)** — the workhorse. Start low and titrate up, or cover a
      faster start with IVIG/PLEX to avoid early worsening. Effective, but the long-term toxicity
      is why steroid-sparing agents go on early.
    - **Steroid-sparing oral agents** — started early to get patients off steroids:
        - **Azathioprine** — best-established; slow onset (months). Check **TPMT** before starting.
        - **Mycophenolate mofetil** — widely used, better tolerated; also slow.
        - Others: **tacrolimus, cyclosporine, methotrexate** in selected patients.

=== "Refractory / steroid-dependent — targeted biologics"

    The class that reorganized MG care. Reserve for inadequate control on, or intolerance of,
    conventional immunotherapy — and note the **serostatus restrictions**, which are the practical
    decision driver.

    **FcRn inhibitors** (lower total IgG, including pathogenic antibody — cyclical dosing):

    - **Efgartigimod (Vyvgart IV; Vyvgart Hytrulo SC** with hyaluronidase) — AChR+; given in cycles.
    - **Rozanolixizumab (Rystiggo, SC)** — **AChR+ or MuSK+.**
    - **Nipocalimab (Imaavy)** — **approved 30 Apr 2025**, AChR+ or MuSK+, adults and **adolescents
      ≥12**, IV or SC.
    - **Batoclimab** — phase 3, **not yet approved.**

    **Complement C5 inhibitors** (block terminal complement — **AChR+ only**, since complement
    damage at the endplate is the AChR mechanism; **ineffective in MuSK** disease):

    - **Eculizumab (Soliris)** — IV **every 2 weeks.**
    - **Ravulizumab (Ultomiris)** — IV **every 8 weeks** (longer half-life).
    - **Zilucoplan (Zilbrysq)** — **subcutaneous, self-administered daily.**
    - **All require meningococcal vaccination** ≥2 weeks before starting (boxed warning) — same
      rules as in [NMOSD](../ms/nmosd.md).

    **B-cell / anti-CD19:**

    - **Inebilizumab (Uplizna)** — anti-CD19, IV every 6 months; **FDA-approved for gMG 11 Dec
      2025** (AChR+ **and** MuSK+, MINT trial). Its third indication after NMOSD and IgG4-RD.
    - **Rituximab** (anti-CD20, off-label) — **particularly effective in MuSK disease**, where it
      is often used early rather than as last resort.

### Serostatus is the practical routing tool

| Situation | Favoured approach |
|---|---|
| **AChR+ generalized** | Pyridostigmine + steroids/steroid-sparing; **thymectomy** if non-thymomatous; escalate to **FcRn, complement, or inebilizumab** |
| **MuSK+** | Pyridostigmine often poorly tolerated; **rituximab early**; **FcRn (rozanolizumab/nipocalimab)** or inebilizumab; **complement inhibitors do not work**; **thymectomy does not help** |
| **Any thymoma** | **Thymectomy regardless** of severity or serostatus |
| **Ocular-only** | Pyridostigmine ± low-dose steroids; treat-to-prevent-generalization is debated |
| **Crisis / refractory** | IVIG or PLEX now; escalate chronic therapy |

## Thymectomy

- **Thymoma present → thymectomy**, always, as oncologic surgery.
- **No thymoma → thymectomy for AChR-positive generalized MG**, based on **MGTX** (the randomized
  trial): thymectomy plus prednisone was **superior to prednisone alone** — better QMG scores,
  **lower steroid requirement, fewer hospitalizations for exacerbation** — and the **benefit was
  sustained through 5 years.** Best evidence in patients **age ~18–65** within a few years of onset.
- **MuSK, LRP4, and purely ocular disease** — thymectomy is **not** supported.

!!! warning "Do not use in MG — the precipitant list"
    Give every patient this list. Many are drugs a *covering* provider reaches for without knowing
    the patient has MG.

    **Never / black-box:**

    - **Telithromycin** — severe, sometimes fatal MG exacerbation. **Contraindicated.**
    - **Fluoroquinolones** (cipro/levo/moxifloxacin) — FDA boxed warning in MG.
    - **D-penicillamine** — *causes* an MG-like syndrome. Avoid.
    - **Botulinum toxin** — avoid.

    **Use only if unavoidable, and watch closely:**

    - **Aminoglycosides** and **macrolides** (azithro/clarithro/erythromycin).
    - **IV magnesium** — blocks transmission; dangerous in active disease.
    - **Beta-blockers**, **procainamide**, **quinine/quinidine**.
    - **Statins** — can worsen or unmask MG.
    - **Hydroxychloroquine/chloroquine.**
    - **Immune checkpoint inhibitors** (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, ipilimumab) — can trigger or
      severely worsen MG, often within 6 weeks; a recognized immune-related adverse event.

!!! tip "TRICARE — MG prescribing"
    Pyridostigmine, prednisone, azathioprine, and mycophenolate are **generic/formulary.** The
    **targeted biologics** (all FcRn inhibitors, all complement inhibitors, inebilizumab) are
    **high-cost specialty drugs, PA-gated**, with a neurologist requirement and documentation of
    serostatus and failed conventional therapy.

    - **Serostatus gates coverage as well as efficacy:** complement inhibitors are AChR+ only, so a
      PA for a MuSK patient will (correctly) be denied — route MuSK disease to rituximab, an FcRn
      agent, or inebilizumab.
    - Infused agents (eculizumab, ravulizumab, inebilizumab, IV efgartigimod) typically run through
      the **medical benefit**; self-administered SC agents (zilucoplan, SC efgartigimod,
      rozanolixizumab) through **specialty pharmacy**.
    - **IVIG** for crisis/bridging is a medical-benefit authorization — start it early.
    - **Verify every agent at the TRICARE Formulary Search** before counseling.

!!! military "Military medicine — myasthenia gravis"
    *Framework — verify against current **AR 40-501**, **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2**, **DoDI 1332.18**, and
    service aeromedical policy. See [Profiles, MEB/MAR2 & deployability](../military/deployability.md).*

    - **Retention — and the carve-out that decides the case.** **AR 40-501 para 3-31c** lists
      **myasthenia gravis "unless clinically restricted to the extraocular muscles."** The DoD floor
      is identical — **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.l**, *"myasthenia gravis, unless only involving
      extraocular muscles."* Both regulations say the same thing: **generalized MG fails retention;
      purely ocular MG can retain.** Cite both, and make the ocular-vs-generalized determination the
      center of your documentation.
    - **Deployability: generalized MG is non-deployable**, on multiple independent grounds:
        - **Crisis risk** — a respiratory emergency that needs IVIG/PLEX and an ICU is
          incompatible with an austere or afloat environment.
        - **Infusion/monitoring-dependent therapy** and **immunosuppression.**
        - **Heat, exertion, and sleep deprivation all worsen fatigable weakness** — the operational
          environment is a direct provocation.
        - A long list of **common field/formulary drugs are contraindicated** (fluoroquinolones and
          aminoglycosides especially) — a real constraint on deployed medical support.
    - **Profile:** **permanent** for generalized disease, with functional limits reflecting bulbar,
      respiratory, and fatigable-strength limitations and heat intolerance. Ocular-only disease may
      warrant a lesser profile — but document the *generalization surveillance* explicitly, since
      ~50% convert.
    - **EPTS / LOD:** onset often predates diagnosis; document the first attributable symptom
      (fluctuating diplopia/ptosis) and review pre-accession records.
    - **MEB/DES:** refer generalized MG. Document serostatus, crisis history, respiratory function
      (FVC), medication trials, and duty impact. **A first crisis is a sentinel event** — it
      establishes the disease as generalized and, usually, as boardable.
    - **Aeromedical:** MG is **disqualifying for flying duty** — fatigable diplopia alone is
      incompatible with the standard.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

**The lesion is the endplate.** In AChR-positive MG, antibodies against the acetylcholine receptor
drive **complement-mediated destruction of the postsynaptic membrane** — the folds flatten, receptor
density falls, and the safety factor of neuromuscular transmission collapses, so repeated firing
fails progressively. That complement mechanism is exactly why **C5 inhibitors work in AChR disease.**
**MuSK** antibodies are IgG4 and act differently — they disrupt the MuSK–LRP4 clustering pathway
that maintains the receptor array **without prominent complement activation**, which is precisely
why **complement inhibitors fail in MuSK disease** while **B-cell depletion (rituximab) works so
well.** The serostatus is not a label; it is the mechanism, and it predicts which drug will work.

**A decade that transformed the refractory patient.** For most of MG's history, the patient who
failed steroids and azathioprine had few options. Since 2017, three mechanistic classes have
arrived: **complement inhibition** (eculizumab, then ravulizumab and zilucoplan), **FcRn blockade**
(efgartigimod, rozanolixizumab, and nipocalimab, which recycles less pathogenic IgG), and now
**anti-CD19 depletion** (inebilizumab, approved for gMG in December 2025). Refractory MG went from
a management dead-end to a menu — the harder question now is sequencing and cost, not whether an
option exists.

**Thymectomy earned its place by trial.** MGTX was the randomized evidence that settled a
decades-old practice: in non-thymomatous AChR-positive generalized MG, **thymectomy plus prednisone
beat prednisone alone**, and the benefit — better disease control, less steroid, fewer
exacerbations — **held through 5 years.**

**Key evidence:**

- **MGTX (NEJM 2016; Lancet Neurology 2019 extension)** — thymectomy + prednisone superior to
  prednisone alone in non-thymomatous AChR+ generalized MG; benefit sustained to 5 years.
- **REGAIN** — eculizumab in refractory AChR+ gMG.
- **CHAMPION-MG** — ravulizumab; **RAISE** — zilucoplan.
- **ADAPT** — efgartigimod; **MycarinG** — rozanolixizumab; **Vivacity-MG3** — nipocalimab.
- **MINT** — inebilizumab in AChR+ and MuSK+ gMG (basis of the Dec 2025 approval).
- **International Consensus Guidance for Management of MG, 2020 update** (Narayanaswami et al.,
  *Neurology* 2021).

## Related neuromuscular-junction disorders

MG is the postsynaptic, autoimmune prototype — but fatigable or acute weakness at the NMJ has
other causes that are treated completely differently:

- **[Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome (LEMS)](lems.md)** — *presynaptic* antibody disease,
  often paraneoplastic. The MG mimic that is really a lung-cancer search.
- **[Botulism](botulism.md)** — *presynaptic toxin*; descending paralysis, an antitoxin emergency.
- **[Nerve agent / organophosphate toxicity](nerve-agent.md)** — poisoning of the synaptic enzyme;
  a CBRN and pesticide emergency treated with atropine and pralidoxime.
