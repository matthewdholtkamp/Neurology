# Diplopia

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** the first fork is **monocular vs binocular.** Binocular diplopia (resolves when
either eye is covered) means the eyes are **misaligned** — a neurologic, neuromuscular-junction,
or muscle/orbit problem — and is what you work up. Monocular diplopia (persists with one eye
covered) is almost always **ocular/refractive** → ophthalmology, not a neurology emergency.

!!! danger "🚩 Red flags"
    - **CN III palsy with a fixed/dilated pupil** (± pain) → **posterior communicating artery
      aneurysm until proven otherwise** — emergent vascular imaging (CTA/MRA) and neurosurgery.
    - **Multiple ocular motor nerves together** → cavernous sinus / orbital apex / superior orbital
      fissure lesion (add proptosis, V1/V2 sensory loss, Horner's) → urgent imaging.
    - **Fatigable, variable diplopia with ptosis, pupil-sparing** → **myasthenia gravis** — it
      mimics any pupil-sparing motility pattern.
    - **CN VI palsy + papilledema/headache** → raised ICP (CN VI is **false-localizing**).
    - **Vertical misalignment/skew with other brainstem signs** → central (stroke, MS, Wernicke).
    - **Older patient with ischemic palsy** → screen for **giant cell arteritis** (ESR/CRP).
    - **Proptosis, lid lag, injection** → thyroid eye disease or orbital process.

## Rapid approach

1. **Monocular or binocular?** Cover each eye. Monocular → ophthalmology (refractive/lens/macula).
2. **Binocular:** characterize — worse at near vs distance, worse in a specific gaze, horizontal
   vs vertical, comitant vs incomitant.
3. **Localize:** single nerve (III/IV/VI), NMJ (variable, fatigable, + ptosis/orbicularis),
   muscle/orbit (restrictive), or supranuclear/internuclear (INO, skew).
4. **Check the pupil and lids** — the pupil is the pivotal sign in a CN III palsy.
5. **Image and test** per localization; treat the cause and relieve symptoms (prism/patch).

## Localize the lesion

=== "The three ocular motor nerves"
    - **CN III (oculomotor):** ptosis, "down-and-out" eye, and — critically — **pupil**
      (parasympathetic fibers run superficially). **Pupil-involving → compressive/aneurysm**;
      **pupil-sparing** isolated CN III in a vasculopath is often microvascular/ischemic — but
      modern practice is to **image most cases**, especially with any atypia or incomplete sparing.
      Course: between SCA and PCA, near PComm, into the cavernous sinus and superior orbital fissure.
    - **CN IV (trochlear):** vertical/torsional diplopia, worse on **down-and-in** gaze and **head
      tilt** to the affected side; patients adopt a compensatory head tilt **away**. Longest, most
      slender nerve; **decussates** and exits dorsally → vulnerable to head trauma.
    - **CN VI (abducens):** horizontal diplopia worse at distance and toward the palsy; **false-
      localizing** with raised ICP (long intracranial course over the petrous ridge/Dorello canal).

=== "Not a single nerve?"
    - **NMJ (myasthenia):** variable, fatigable, pupil-**sparing**, ptosis, orbicularis oculi
      weakness. Bedside: **ice-pack test**, fatigue on sustained upgaze. Test AChR/MuSK antibodies.
      *(A dedicated neuromuscular-junction page is planned.)*
    - **Restrictive/orbital:** thyroid eye disease, orbital myositis, trauma (blowout with entrapment)
      → forced ductions positive, proptosis; get orbital imaging and TFTs.
    - **Supranuclear / brainstem:** **INO** (adduction lag + abducting nystagmus → MS or stroke),
      skew deviation, gaze palsies → MRI.

## Bedside testing

!!! note "Exam checklist"
    - Diplopia **monocular vs binocular**; worse near/far; worse in which gaze direction
    - Acuity (Snellen), **EOM** versions/ductions, pupils, lids (ptosis, fatigability)
    - **Cover–uncover** (detects tropias — misalignment present all the time)
    - **Alternate (cross) cover** (detects tropias **and** phorias)
    - **Comitant** (equal in all directions → often longstanding/decompensated phoria) vs
      **incomitant** (worse in one direction → paretic/restrictive)
    - **Maddox rod** if the misalignment is unclear on cover testing

### Parks–Bielschowsky 3-step test (for an isolated vertical/CN IV palsy)

*Only valid for a single cyclovertical muscle — not with multiple muscles or restriction.*

1. **Which eye is higher in primary gaze?** Hyper eye → suspect its **SO/IR**; other eye → **SR/IO**.
2. **Worse in right or left gaze?** Circle the **SR/IR** on the side where that gaze is worst;
   **IO/SO** on the other.
3. **Worse on right or left head tilt?** Circle **SR/SO** on the worse-tilt side; **IO/IR** on the
   other.

The paretic muscle is the one **circled all three times** (classically the contralateral SO in a
CN IV palsy).

## Workup

- **Pupil-involving CN III:** emergent **CTA/MRA** (aneurysm) — do not wait.
- **Isolated pupil-sparing palsy:** MRI in most (especially <50, atypical, or not resolving in
  ~3 months); microvascular palsies should improve over weeks.
- **Suspected MG:** AChR/MuSK antibodies, ice-pack test, consider repetitive nerve stimulation;
  screen for thymoma.
- **Older/ischemic:** ESR/CRP for **GCA**.
- **Restrictive:** TFTs/TSI, orbital imaging, forced ductions.

## Management

Treat the underlying cause. For symptomatic relief while recovering: **monocular occlusion**
(patch) or **Fresnel/ground-in prisms**; strabismus surgery for stable, persistent deviations.

## Disposition

Emergent for pupil-involving CN III, multiple cranial nerves, or brainstem signs. Otherwise
outpatient neuro-ophthalmology work-up with symptomatic prisms/patching and interval follow-up to
confirm a presumed microvascular palsy actually resolves.

!!! military "Military medicine — diplopia"
    *Retention standard below is quoted from **AR 40-501 (27 June 2019), para 3-7b**; confirm
    against the current edition and verify DoDI 1332.18 and aeromedical policy.*

    - **Diplopia is duty- and safety-limiting** — persistent double vision is generally
      disqualifying for flight, dive, and weapons/vehicle-dependent duty, and non-deployable while
      active.
    - **Chase the cause, because the cause drives the board:** a microvascular palsy that fully
      resolves is very different from **myasthenia gravis** (typically → MEB; see the neuromuscular
      section) or a demyelinating **INO** (→ the MS pathway).
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary during recovery/work-up; permanent for persistent
      misalignment or an underlying disqualifying diagnosis.
    - **EPTS / LOD:** document mechanism — **head trauma** (classic for CN IV) ties into the
      [post-traumatic](../headache/post-traumatic.md) picture and LOD determination.
    - **Retention — AR 40-501 (27 June 2019) para 3-7b:** **binocular diplopia not correctable by
      surgery, that is severe, constant, and in a zone less than 20 degrees from the primary
      position** falls below retention standards → **referral to the DES** (MEB/PEB). A disqualifying
      underlying condition (e.g., myasthenia) drives the board on its own terms; document the
      localization and imaging/antibody work-up.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** binocular diplopia and impaired stereopsis are
      **disqualifying/waiver-dependent** for flying and special duty — consult the governing policy.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **Monocular vs binocular is the whole triage** — monocular is ophthalmologic (refractive/lens),
  binocular is a misalignment you localize and image.
- **The pupil decides urgency in CN III palsy:** pupil involvement points to a **compressive
  aneurysm** and mandates emergent vascular imaging; the old "rule of the pupil" for benign
  microvascular sparing has enough exceptions that **imaging most CN III palsies** is the safer
  modern default.
- **Myasthenia is the great mimic** — variable, fatigable, pupil-sparing diplopia with ptosis;
  always on the differential for an isolated ocular motility problem.
- **CN VI is false-localizing** — pair it with headache/papilledema and think raised ICP.
- **Don't forget GCA** in an older patient with an ischemic ocular motor palsy.
