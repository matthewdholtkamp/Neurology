# Epilepsy syndromes

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** the **syndrome** — not just the seizure type — tells you which drug to use, which drug
will make it *worse*, the prognosis, and the counseling. The single highest-yield distinction for an
adult on call: **is this a genetic *generalized* epilepsy (use broad-spectrum, avoid
sodium-channel blockers) or a *focal* epilepsy?** Get that right and most decisions follow.

!!! danger "🚩 The one mistake to avoid — sodium-channel blockers in generalized epilepsy"
    **Carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, eslicarbazepine, phenytoin, and gabapentin/pregabalin can
    *aggravate* absence and myoclonic seizures** (and precipitate absence/myoclonic status). So if you
    can't tell focal from generalized, **start a broad-spectrum agent** (levetiracetam, lamotrigine, or
    valproate) — never a narrow sodium-channel blocker into an undifferentiated or generalized picture.
    *(Lacosamide is sometimes used for generalized tonic-clonic seizures, but its role in IGE is
    debated — don't reach for it as a default there.)*

## How syndromes are organized (ILAE 2022)

Three diagnostic levels — **seizure type → epilepsy type (focal / generalized / combined / unknown) →
epilepsy syndrome.** The 2022 ILAE grouping: **neonatal/infantile**, **childhood-onset**, **variable
age of onset**, and the **idiopathic (genetic) generalized epilepsies.** For adult practice, the two
buckets that matter most are the **genetic generalized epilepsies** and the **focal epilepsies**.

## Genetic (idiopathic) generalized epilepsies — the big adult-relevant group

!!! orderset "The four IGEs — recognize them, and treat them broad-spectrum"
    Normal development/exam, generalized spike-wave on EEG, often photosensitive, triggered by **sleep
    deprivation and alcohol.** **First-line = valproate (most effective) or levetiracetam/lamotrigine**
    — with **valproate avoided in childbearing potential** (→ lamotrigine/levetiracetam).

    - **Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy (JME)** — the classic adult IGE: **early-morning myoclonic jerks**
      (dropping the coffee cup), **generalized tonic-clonic seizures**, and often absence; onset in the
      teens; **usually lifelong** (needs long-term therapy). Counsel hard on **sleep and alcohol.**
      **In childbearing potential, levetiracetam is the preferred non-valproate first-line** — head-to-
      head it beat lamotrigine specifically in JME (treatment-failure HR ~0.47), and **lamotrigine
      worsened myoclonus** in a subset. **Valproate is the most effective overall** but off the table
      in anyone who could become pregnant.
    - **Juvenile absence epilepsy (JAE)** — absence + GTC, onset around puberty; lifelong.
    - **Childhood absence epilepsy (CAE)** — frequent brief absences, onset 4–10 y; often remits.
      **Ethosuximide** is first-line for absence-only (valproate/lamotrigine if GTC coexists).
    - **Epilepsy with generalized tonic-clonic seizures alone (GTCA).**
    - **Eyelid myoclonia with absences (Jeavons syndrome)** — a distinct childhood-onset generalized
      epilepsy: **eyelid myoclonia, eye-closure and photic sensitivity**; often drug-resistant and
      lifelong (valproate/levetiracetam; avoid Na-channel blockers).

    !!! tip "When first-line monotherapy fails in a woman with IGE"
        Both **substituting** another monotherapy and **adding on** are reasonable — but the best-
        supported **non-valproate dual therapy is levetiracetam + lamotrigine** (most effective
        combination in a large series of women aged 10–50). Reach for that pairing before considering
        valproate under a pregnancy-prevention framework.

## Focal epilepsies

!!! orderset "Mesial temporal lobe epilepsy with hippocampal sclerosis (MTLE-HS) — the surgically-remediable adult syndrome"
    A **formal ILAE 2022 syndrome** and the **most common focal epilepsy in adults** (often a history
    of childhood febrile seizures).

    - **Semiology:** an **aura** (rising epigastric sensation, déjà vu, fear, or an olfactory/gustatory
      hallucination) → **impaired awareness with automatisms** (lip-smacking, hand-fumbling) →
      postictal confusion.
    - **Frequently drug-resistant** — and when it is, **surgery is highly effective** (often curative);
      **refer early** (2022 ILAE surgical consensus — see the [seizure page](index.md)); MRI
      (hippocampal atrophy/T2 signal) + video-EEG.
        - **Anterior temporal lobectomy (ATL)** — the reference operation (~⅔ seizure-free at 2 y).
        - **Laser interstitial thermal therapy (LITT)** — minimally invasive alternative (2-yr
          seizure-freedom ~53–62%, somewhat below ATL but **far lower complication rates and shorter
          stay**) — favor it for **dominant-hemisphere** MTLE (better naming/memory preservation) or
          when morbidity is the priority. First-line for hypothalamic hamartoma.
    - **Medically:** treat as focal — **lamotrigine, levetiracetam, lacosamide, or
      oxcarbazepine/carbamazepine.**

!!! orderset "Variable-age-of-onset focal syndromes (ILAE 2022) — recognize these in adults"
    - **Sleep-related hypermotor epilepsy (SHE)** — formerly *nocturnal frontal lobe epilepsy*:
      **clusters of brief, stereotyped hyperkinetic/dystonic seizures out of sleep**, often
      **misdiagnosed as a parasomnia/night terrors.** Genetic (**CHRNA4, DEPDC5**) or structural
      (focal cortical dysplasia). **Carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine are often effective**; video-EEG (and
      DEPDC5 raises a surgical-lesion question).
    - **Epilepsy with auditory features (ADLTE / *LGI1*)** — focal seizures with **auditory auras**
      (buzzing, ringing); autosomal-dominant *LGI1*; generally drug-responsive. *(Don't confuse the
      genetic LGI1 epilepsy with acute anti-LGI1 autoimmune encephalitis.)*
    - **Reading epilepsy** — reflex seizures (jaw/perioral myoclonus) triggered by reading.

!!! orderset "Self-limited focal epilepsies of childhood (e.g., centrotemporal spikes / "rolandic")"
    Normal children, **nocturnal focal seizures** (hemifacial twitching, drooling, speech arrest) with
    **centrotemporal spikes**; **usually outgrown by adolescence** — often needs **no or minimal
    treatment.** ILAE dropped **"benign" → "self-limited"** for a reason: **~1–8% evolve into
    epileptic encephalopathy with spike-wave activation in sleep (EE-SWAS)** with real cognitive
    cost — so **watch for cognitive/behavioral regression and get a sleep EEG** if it appears.

## Developmental & epileptic encephalopathies (DEE)

Mostly pediatric, but these patients **transition into adult care**, so recognize them:

- **Lennox-Gastaut syndrome** — multiple seizure types (esp. **tonic/atonic → drop attacks**),
  cognitive impairment, slow spike-wave; drug-resistant. Broad-spectrum + valproate, lamotrigine,
  rufinamide, clobazam, cannabidiol, **fenfluramine** (now FDA-approved for LGS as well as Dravet);
  VNS/corpus callosotomy for drops.
- **Dravet syndrome** (**SCN1A**) — infantile onset, fever/vaccination-triggered prolonged seizures.
  **AVOID sodium-channel blockers — carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, phenytoin, AND lamotrigine (it *is*
  a sodium-channel blocker) — they worsen it.** Base: valproate + clobazam; the **three evidence-based
  add-ons are fenfluramine, stiripentol, and cannabidiol** (network meta-analyses favor **fenfluramine
  and stiripentol over cannabidiol** for seizure response). **Precision therapies** (antisense
  oligonucleotides, gene therapy) are in trials.
- **West syndrome / infantile spasms** — spasms + hypsarrhythmia + developmental regression; treat
  urgently (**ACTH/steroids; vigabatrin**, esp. tuberous sclerosis).
- **NORSE / FIRES** — new-onset refractory status epilepticus (FIRES = its febrile-triggered form, a
  formal ILAE 2022 DEE): a **previously healthy person → febrile illness → explosive refractory status
  → drug-resistant epilepsy.** Follow the 2022 international consensus: aggressive anesthetic control +
  an **early autoimmune/cytokine work-up** and **empiric immunotherapy** — **anakinra (IL-1) and
  tocilizumab (IL-6)** achieve seizure control in a majority in series (guided by cytokine profiling);
  ketogenic diet. Outcomes remain poor — get epileptology/neuro-ICU involved early.

## Two syndrome-specific rules that catch people

!!! warning "Get the syndrome right or the drug backfires"
    - **Generalized (IGE) or myoclonic/absence → broad-spectrum only** (levetiracetam/valproate/
      lamotrigine); **never carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine/eslicarbazepine/phenytoin/gabapentin**.
    - **Dravet (SCN1A) → avoid sodium-channel blockers**, including lamotrigine.
    - **Childbearing potential with IGE → levetiracetam or lamotrigine, not valproate** — and valproate
      in anyone who could become pregnant requires a **formal pregnancy-prevention programme /
      documented counseling** (EMA/FDA).
    - **Drug-resistant** = **failure of 2 appropriately chosen, tolerated ASMs** (ILAE/Kwan 2010) →
      **refer for surgical evaluation early** (MTLE is often curable; ATL or LITT).

!!! tip "Two things that are easy to skip"
    - **Genetic testing** is now part of syndromic diagnosis — send it for **DEEs, early-onset
      epilepsy, drug-resistant epilepsy, or a suggestive family history** (a specific gene, e.g.,
      **SCN1A/KCNQ2/DEPDC5**, increasingly steers treatment — and precision therapies are emerging).
    - **Screen for depression and anxiety** — they affect **~20–30% of adults with epilepsy** and drive
      quality of life and adherence more than seizure count alone.

    free) is the scenario most likely to support accession waiver / retention — document the remission.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **Syndrome drives the drug.** The critical adult split is **genetic generalized epilepsy** (JME,
  JAE, CAE, GTCA, Jeavons — broad-spectrum; avoid Na-channel blockers incl. eslicarbazepine) vs
  **focal epilepsy.**
- **JME is lifelong** — in childbearing potential, **levetiracetam is the preferred non-valproate
  first-line** (beat lamotrigine head-to-head; lamotrigine can worsen myoclonus); **levetiracetam +
  lamotrigine** is the best non-valproate combination when monotherapy fails.
- **Know the variable-age focal syndromes** — **sleep-related hypermotor epilepsy** (misdiagnosed as
  parasomnia) and **LGI1 auditory epilepsy**; **MTLE-HS** is now a formal syndrome and the
  surgically-remediable one (**ATL or LITT** — refer early).
- **Dravet (SCN1A): sodium-channel blockers — including lamotrigine — make it worse;** the
  evidence-based add-ons are **fenfluramine, stiripentol, cannabidiol** (fenfluramine now covers LGS
  too). **NORSE/FIRES** needs early immunotherapy (anakinra/tocilizumab).
- **Self-limited childhood focal epilepsy** is usually outgrown — but **watch for evolution to
  EE-SWAS.** **Send genetic testing** in DEE/early-onset/drug-resistant epilepsy, and **screen for
  depression/anxiety.**
