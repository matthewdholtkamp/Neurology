# Seizure & status epilepticus

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** a seizure is sudden, hypersynchronous cortical firing; **status epilepticus is the
neurologic code** — treat it on a **clock**, escalate on schedule, and **don't under-dose the
benzo** (the single most common mistake). This page covers the code, the first-seizure work-up, and
picking an antiseizure medication (ASM).

> *The ER consult usually sounds like:* "Known-or-unknown epileptic, two witnessed seizures, got 4 of
> Ativan, now not responding — should I load them with something, and you're admitting, right?" The
> answer to "load them?" is **yes, now** — and check whether that "not responding" is **postictal or
> ongoing *nonconvulsive* status.**

!!! danger "🚩 Status epilepticus — recognize it and start the clock"
    - **Definition (operational):** **≥5 minutes** of continuous seizure, **or ≥2 seizures without
      recovery** of consciousness between them. Don't wait 30 minutes — **5 minutes = treat.**
    - **"Stopped but won't wake up" = nonconvulsive status until proven otherwise** — get **urgent
      cEEG.** Persistent altered consciousness after visible convulsions stop is NCSE in up to ~20%.
    - **Under-dosing benzodiazepines** (and then blaming the "refractory" seizure) is the classic
      error — give the **full weight-based dose.**
    - **Find and fix the cause in parallel** — a seizure from **hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, a toxin,
      or eclampsia** won't stop on ASMs until the cause is corrected.

## Status epilepticus — the timed escalation ladder (the code)

!!! orderset "Step 0 — Stabilize (0–5 min)"
    - **ABCs**, O₂, position, suction; **IV access ×2**; **continuous ECG, BP, SpO₂.**
    - **Point-of-care glucose** — if low, **D50W 50 mL IV + thiamine 100 mg IV** (thiamine first/with
      glucose in the malnourished/alcohol-use patient).
    - **STAT labs:** CBC, BMP, Ca, Mg, glucose, **ASM levels**, ± LFTs, tox screen (blood + urine),
      ABG, β-hCG, PO₄; **ECG.**

!!! orderset "Step 1 — First-line benzodiazepine (5–20 min) — give the FULL dose"
    Pick by access; **these are frequently under-dosed** — use the real weight-based dose and **repeat
    once** if still seizing.

    - **IV lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg/dose), may repeat ×1** — first choice if IV is in.
    - **No IV? IM midazolam 10 mg** (>40 kg) — **RAMPART** showed IM midazolam is **at least as good as
      IV lorazepam** and faster to give; also **intranasal/buccal midazolam.**
    - **IV diazepam 0.15–0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg)**, or **rectal diazepam 0.2 mg/kg** if no IV.

!!! orderset "Step 2 — Second-line ASM (20–40 min) — the three are EQUIVALENT (ESETT)"
    **ESETT (NEJM 2019) found levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, and valproate stop ~45–47% of
    benzo-refractory status — no winner.** So **pick by the patient**, give the full load IV:

    - **Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg)** — fewest interactions/contraindications; a good
      default (esp. cardiac/hepatic disease, pregnancy).
    - **Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV (≤150 mg PE/min)** — **avoid** in hypotension/arrhythmia; watch
      the BP and QT.
    - **Valproate 40 mg/kg IV (may add 20 mg/kg)** — **avoid** in hepatic disease and
      pregnancy/childbearing potential.
    - *(Phenobarbital 15 mg/kg IV if none of the above is available.)*
    - **Continue the second-line agent** as you escalate; **arrange cEEG.**

!!! orderset "Step 3 — Refractory status (>40 min, or 2 lines failed) — anesthetize + cEEG"
    **Intubate**, admit to **ICU**, start **continuous cEEG**, and begin a **continuous anesthetic
    infusion** (titrate to seizure suppression, ± burst-suppression):

    - **Midazolam** — load 0.2 mg/kg, then 0.05–2.9 mg/kg/h.
    - **Propofol** — load ~1–2 mg/kg, then 1–15 mg/kg/h (watch propofol-infusion syndrome; limit
      duration/dose).
    - **Ketamine** — increasingly used (NMDA-mediated late status), usually **with** a
      GABAergic agent.
    - Keep the second-line ASM on board for maintenance.

!!! orderset "Step 4 — Super-refractory (>24 h, or recurs on weaning)"
    - **Pentobarbital/thiopental** infusion (load 5 mg/kg → titrate; **hypotension is expected** —
      pressors ready), titrated to seizure/burst suppression.
    - **Hunt hard for the cause** — **autoimmune/paraneoplastic (NORSE/FIRES)**, CNS infection,
      PRES/RCVS, toxic-metabolic; consider **immunotherapy** (steroids/IVIg/PLEX) empirically in NORSE.
    - Wean anesthetics slowly once seizure-free ~24–48 h; neurology + epileptology on board.

!!! orderset "Copy-paste — first 20 minutes"
    - **Glucose now**; D50 + thiamine if low.
    - **Lorazepam 4 mg IV** (or **midazolam 10 mg IM** if no IV) — **repeat ×1** if seizing at ~5 min.
    - **Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV** (or fosphenytoin/valproate by patient) as the seizure continues.
    - **cEEG, ICU, and treat the cause.** No sub-therapeutic benzos, no delay to second-line.

!!! warning "Toxin-driven seizures — the antidote matters"
    - **INH (isoniazid):** benzodiazepine **+ pyridoxine (vitamin B6) ~70 mg/kg, max 5 g.**
    - **Tricyclic antidepressants:** wide QRS → **sodium bicarbonate.**
    - **Organophosphates:** **atropine + pralidoxime** (+ benzodiazepine).
    - **Alcohol/benzo withdrawal:** escalating benzodiazepines. **Eclampsia: magnesium** (+ deliver).

## Is it actually a seizure? (mimics)

Before you commit to lifelong ASMs, exclude the imitators — **syncope** (convulsive syncope after a
faint; get an ECG/cardiac workup — cardiac syncope can kill), **psychogenic non-epileptic seizures
(PNES)**, TIA, transient global amnesia, migraine, and sleep disorders.

- **PNES tells (not diagnostic alone; confirm with video-EEG):** **side-to-side head shaking**, eye
  **closure** (epileptic seizures usually have eyes open), pelvic thrusting, **asynchronous** flailing,
  a **waxing–waning** course, prolonged duration, crying/stuttering, **preserved awareness despite
  bilateral movements**, and resistance to eye opening. Manage with **diagnosis delivery + behavioral
  health**, not more ASMs.

## First seizure — work-up & whether to treat

!!! orderset "First unprovoked seizure — the work-up (outpatient is OK if back to baseline, no deficits, low infection concern)"
    - **Labs:** CBC, BMP, Ca/Mg, glucose, LFTs, tox, ± ESR/CRP; **β-hCG.**
    - **Imaging:** **MRI brain (with & without contrast) is the study of choice**; CT in the ED (and
      before LP) to exclude a bleed/mass.
    - **EEG** — best **within 48 h** (raises yield); urgent if consciousness stays altered.
    - **LP only if infection/SAH suspected.**

!!! tip "Start an ASM after a *first* seizure? — individualize (AAN/AES 2015)"
    Immediate ASM **reduces recurrence over the first 2 years but does NOT improve long-term remission
    or quality of life.** Recurrence after a first unprovoked seizure is **~21–45% at 2 years.**

    - **Treat now if recurrence risk is high:** a **prior brain insult** (stroke/tumor/trauma), an
      **epileptiform EEG**, a **relevant lesion on MRI**, or a **nocturnal** seizure.
    - **Reasonable to wait** in a low-risk, normal-work-up first seizure — with a shared decision
      (weigh ASM adverse effects, ~7–31%).
    - An **acute *provoked* (symptomatic) seizure** — fix the provocation; long-term ASM usually isn't
      needed.

## Picking an ASM (by seizure type & the patient)

!!! orderset "Match the drug to the seizure *and* the person"
    In an adult ED with an unknown-etiology first seizure, most have a structural cause — a common
    default is **levetiracetam.** Then refine:

    | Situation | Reasonable first choices |
    |---|---|
    | **Focal (most adults)** | **Levetiracetam, lamotrigine, or lacosamide**; oxcarbazepine/carbamazepine |
    | **Generalized (GTC)** | **Levetiracetam, lamotrigine, valproate** *(valproate most effective — but see childbearing)* |
    | **Absence** | Ethosuximide, valproate, lamotrigine |
    | **Myoclonic (e.g., JME)** | **Levetiracetam, valproate** *(avoid carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine — worsen myoclonus/absence)* |
    | **Childbearing potential** | **Lamotrigine or levetiracetam** — **avoid valproate** (neural-tube defects, low IQ) |
    | **Older adults** | **Lamotrigine or levetiracetam** (fewer interactions, better tolerated) |
    | **Comorbid depression/mood** | Lamotrigine (avoid levetiracetam's irritability); **renal** → lamotrigine; **hepatic** → levetiracetam/gabapentin |

    - **Screen HLA-B\*15:02** before carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine in at-risk (Southeast Asian) ancestry
      (SJS/TEN).
    - **Refractory (failed 2 appropriate ASMs) = drug-resistant epilepsy** → refer to an
      **epilepsy center** for surgical/VNS/RNS/DBS evaluation and video-EEG.

## Classify it (ILAE 2017)

Three levels: **seizure type** (focal / generalized / unknown onset; **aware vs impaired awareness**;
motor vs non-motor) → **epilepsy type** (focal / generalized / combined / unknown) → **epilepsy
syndrome.** Getting the type right drives ASM choice (e.g., don't give a sodium-channel blocker in a
generalized/myoclonic syndrome).

## Counseling & driving

- **Driving is restricted after a seizure** — the seizure-free interval is **state/country-specific
  (commonly ~3–12 months; 6 months is typical)**; some states have **mandatory physician reporting.**
  Document the counseling.
- **Safety:** no swimming/bathing alone, heights, or operating dangerous machinery until controlled;
  sleep, alcohol moderation, adherence; **SUDEP** counseling for uncontrolled epilepsy.

## Disposition

Status epilepticus → **ICU with cEEG.** A first seizure back to baseline with a reassuring work-up can
be **discharged with urgent neurology follow-up, safety/driving counseling, and a decision on ASM.**
Admit for a prolonged postictal state, an unremitting/nonconvulsive picture, a concerning cause, or an
unsafe home situation.

!!! tip "TRICARE — seizure prescribing"
    **Levetiracetam, lamotrigine, valproate, phenytoin, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, phenobarbital,
    gabapentin** are **generic/formulary, no PA** — the whole core ladder is low-friction. **Newer
    agents (brivaracetam, lacosamide, cenobamate, perampanel)** are typically **PA-gated** (step
    therapy / specialist). Emergency IV agents are inpatient-stocked. *Verify at the TRICARE Formulary
    Search.*

!!! military "Military medicine — seizure / epilepsy"
    *Framework — verify against current **AR 40-501**, **DoDI 6130.03**, **DoDI 1332.18**, and
    aeromedical policy before counseling.*

    - **Retention (Army):** **AR 40-501 para 3-31i** addresses **seizure disorders/epilepsy** — a
      diagnosis of epilepsy (recurrent unprovoked seizures), or seizures not controlled off medication,
      generally falls below retention → **DES (MEB/PEB).** The joint floor is **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para
      5.26.c** (CNS conditions with persistent sequelae, incl. **seizures**). Document seizure type,
      frequency, provoking factors, work-up, ASM trials, and control.
    - **Accession:** a **history of epilepsy/recurrent seizures** is **disqualifying for entry** (DoDI
      6130.03 Vol 1) — waivers hinge on a documented seizure-free, medication-free interval.
    - **Deployability:** active epilepsy, ongoing ASMs, and the need for lab/level monitoring and
      reliable sleep are **deployment-limiting.** A **provoked** seizure with a corrected cause may
      return to duty per neurology review.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** a seizure history (and most ASMs) is **disqualifying for flying
      and many special-duty positions** — waiver-dependent; confirm the standard for the class.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **Treat status on a clock, not on a hunch.** The **AES 2016** algorithm: stabilize (0–5 min) →
  **full-dose benzo (5–20 min)** → **second-line ASM (20–40 min)** → **anesthetize + cEEG (>40 min).**
- **The second-line three are equivalent (ESETT 2019)** — levetiracetam ≈ fosphenytoin ≈ valproate
  (~45–47% each); **choose by comorbidity**, and give the **full** weight-based load. Levetiracetam is
  the cleanest default.
- **Benzos are under-dosed** — that's the commonest "refractory status" that wasn't. **RAMPART**:
  IM midazolam ≥ IV lorazepam when there's no IV.
- **"Won't wake up" → cEEG** for nonconvulsive status.
- **First seizure:** immediate ASM cuts early recurrence but not long-term remission (**AAN/AES
  2015**) — treat when recurrence risk is high (lesion, epileptiform EEG, nocturnal), otherwise a
  shared decision.
- **Match the ASM to the syndrome** (avoid sodium-channel blockers in generalized/myoclonic; avoid
  valproate in childbearing potential) and to the person; **two failed ASMs = refer to an epilepsy
  center.**

**Key sources:** AES 2016 status-epilepticus guideline (Glauser et al.); ESETT (Kapur et al., NEJM
2019); RAMPART (Silbergleit et al., NEJM 2012); AAN/AES 2015 first-unprovoked-seizure guideline
(Krumholz et al.); ILAE 2017 seizure/epilepsy classification.
