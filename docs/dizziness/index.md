# Dizziness & vertigo

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** the real question in a dizzy patient is not "is it vertigo or lightheadedness?" —
it's **"is this a posterior-circulation stroke or something benign?"** Modern practice sorts
dizziness by **timing and triggers (TiTrATE/ATTEST)**, not by the *quality* of the sensation
(which patients describe unreliably). Get the syndrome right and the exam you need falls out of it.

!!! danger "🚩 Red flags — think central / stroke"
    - **Any focal neurological finding** (dysarthria, dysmetria, crossed sensory loss, Horner's,
      diplopia, new deafness with vertigo → AICA).
    - **Central nystagmus:** direction-changing, purely vertical or torsional, or not suppressed
      by fixation.
    - **Severe truncal ataxia** — can't sit/stand unassisted.
    - **HINTS "INFARCT"** pattern in acute vestibular syndrome (below).
    - **New headache/neck pain**, especially with neck trauma → **do not do Dix-Hallpike or head
      impulse** until you've considered **arterial dissection**.
    - Multiple vascular risk factors + a first, sudden, sustained event.

## Step 1 — put the patient in one of four syndromes

| Syndrome | Pattern | Work the exam toward | Common causes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Acute Vestibular Syndrome (AVS)** | Acute onset, **continuous** for days, spontaneous nystagmus, nausea/gait trouble | **HINTS(+)** | Vestibular neuritis **vs stroke** |
| **Triggered episodic** | Brief spells **provoked** by position change | **Dix-Hallpike**, supine roll, orthostatic vitals | BPPV, orthostatic hypotension |
| **Spontaneous episodic** | Recurrent spells **without** a trigger | History, audiogram, vascular risk | Vestibular migraine, Ménière, TIA, panic |
| **Chronic** | Persistent > weeks–months | Gait, psych, meds | PPPD, bilateral vestibulopathy, drug effect |

!!! warning "HINTS is only valid in AVS"
    HINTS applies to a patient with **continuous, ongoing** vertigo **and spontaneous nystagmus**.
    Doing HINTS on a well-appearing patient **between** spells (no nystagmus) is a misuse and is
    misleading. If there's no continuous AVS, use the triggered/episodic pathway instead.

## The HINTS exam (for AVS)

Three parts. Remember **"INFARCT" = central** (any one is worrying):

- **Impulse Normal** — a **normal** horizontal head-impulse test (no corrective saccade) in a
  patient with AVS suggests the vestibular nerve is intact → **central**. *(A **positive/abnormal**
  head impulse — a catch-up saccade — is reassuringly **peripheral**.)*
- **Fast-phase Alternating** — direction-changing nystagmus on lateral gaze → central.
- **Refixation on Cover Test** — **skew deviation** (vertical corrective movement on alternate
  cover) → central/brainstem.

**HINTS performed correctly in AVS is more sensitive than early MRI-DWI for stroke** in the first
24–48 h (a negative HINTS out-performs an early negative DWI). **HINTS "plus"** adds new
unilateral hearing loss (points to stroke/AICA, not reassurance). *Caveat, straight from the
scutbook: if the patient is an older vasculopath, get the MRI anyway.*

## Central vs peripheral at the bedside

| Feature | Peripheral | Central |
|---|---|---|
| Nystagmus | Unidirectional, horizontal-torsional, **suppresses with fixation** | Direction-changing, vertical/pure torsional, **not suppressed** |
| Head impulse | **Abnormal** (corrective saccade) | Normal |
| Skew | Absent | May be present |
| Gait | Can usually walk | Often **severe** truncal ataxia |
| Hearing | Loss possible (Ménière, labyrinthitis) | Usually spared (except **AICA** → deafness + vertigo) |
| Other findings | None | Focal deficits (Wallenberg: crossed sensory, Horner's, dysphagia) |

## Disposition rules (from the scutbook, kept)

- **Focal findings, or symptomatic older patient with multiple stroke risk factors** → **admit +
  stroke work-up.**
- **Symptomatic older patient, mild risk, no focal findings, negative Dix-Hallpike** → **stroke-
  protocol MRI in the ER**, then discharge with **close** follow-up.
- **Very low risk with classic BPPV** → treat and discharge **without imaging** (Epley + written
  instructions).
- **Do NOT do Dix-Hallpike / head-impulse in a dizzy patient with neck pain** — think dissection.

## Cause-specific management

=== "BPPV (most common)"
    Positional vertigo lasting **< 1 min** (nausea can outlast it). **Dix-Hallpike** reproduces
    torsional up-beating nystagmus (worse affected-ear-down, fatigues on repetition). Treat with
    the **Epley** maneuver; horizontal-canal variants → supine roll test → **BBQ/Gufoni**. No
    routine imaging. Vestibular suppressants **hinder** recovery — avoid beyond the acute hours.

=== "Vestibular neuritis"
    AVS with peripheral HINTS, no other focal findings, often post-viral. **Image (stroke-protocol
    MRI) if any doubt.** Cornerstone is **early vestibular rehabilitation**; keep vestibular
    suppressants **short** (≤ 48–72 h). Corticosteroids may hasten recovery but the evidence is
    limited — reasonable, not mandatory.

=== "Ménière disease"
    Episodic vertigo **> 20 min** with **fluctuating hearing loss, aural fullness, tinnitus**.
    **ENT referral**, sodium restriction, diuretic; refractory → intratympanic steroids/gentamicin.
    Image to exclude central mimics if atypical.

=== "Vestibular migraine"
    Recurrent spontaneous vertigo with migrainous features (± headache) meeting Bárány/IHS criteria
    — now a defined entity, not a wastebasket. Manage like [migraine](../headache/migraine.md):
    trigger control and migraine preventives (nortriptyline, propranolol, topiramate); avoid
    reflexive triptan use in older/vasculopathic patients.

=== "Posterior-circulation stroke"
    Vertigo **plus** a focal finding. Territories: **PICA** (Wallenberg — crossed sensory,
    Horner's, dysphagia, ataxia), **AICA** (vertigo **+ ipsilateral deafness**), **SCA**.
    Consider **vertebrobasilar TIA** for episodic vertigo/diplopia/dysarthria/ataxia. Admit +
    stroke pathway.

## Order set

!!! orderset "ER — undifferentiated acute vertigo"
    - Orthostatic vitals; **finger-stick glucose**; ECG (rule out arrhythmia/pre-syncope)
    - Targeted exam: **HINTS if AVS**, otherwise **Dix-Hallpike**
    - **Stroke-protocol MRI (DWI)** if central features, risk factors, or any doubt
    - **Epley** for confirmed posterior-canal BPPV; **vestibular rehab** consult for neuritis
    - Antiemetic/vestibular suppressant **short-term only** (e.g., meclizine/ondansetron ≤ 48–72 h)

## Disposition

Most benign peripheral vertigo is an outpatient problem (treat + rehab + follow-up). When in doubt
in an older vasculopath, image before discharge. Admit anyone with focal findings or a positive
central screen.

!!! military "Military medicine — dizziness & vertigo"
    *Standards below are quoted from **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1 (accession)** and **AR 40-501**; verify
    current editions, DoDI 1332.18, and aeromedical/dive policy.*

    - **Accession — DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1, para 6.5 (Ears):** **any history of Ménière's syndrome,
      recurrent labyrinthitis, or other chronic vestibular disease**, or **recurrent/persistent
      vertigo in the last 12 months**, is **disqualifying for entry**. Vestibular disease is a
      hard stop for applicants, not just a duty limitation.
    - **Aeromedical & special duty are the crux for serving members.** Vertigo and **spatial
      disorientation** are flight-safety-critical: **recurrent vertigo, Ménière, and vestibular
      migraine are generally disqualifying** for flight, dive, airborne, and other special-duty
      status and require waiver. **BPPV** that fully resolves after repositioning is more favorable.
    - **Deployability:** acute vertigo is temporarily non-deployable; recurrent/unpredictable
      vertigo limits duty in operational, maritime, and aviation environments.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary during work-up/recovery; permanent for chronic or
      recurrent vestibular disease with functional limits.
    - **EPTS / LOD:** document onset and triggers (blast/noise exposure, head injury) — relevant to
      both LOD and any co-existing [post-traumatic](../headache/post-traumatic.md) picture.
    - **Retention / DES:** refractory Ménière, chronic vestibulopathy, or PPPD with duty impact may
      warrant **referral to the DES** (MEB/PEB); document objective testing (audiogram, VNG) and
      failed treatment.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **Timing-and-triggers beats symptom-quality.** The reliable data are *onset, duration, and
  triggers* — not whether the patient calls it "vertigo" or "lightheaded." Frameworks: **TiTrATE /
  ATTEST**.
- **In AVS, a correctly performed HINTS out-performs early MRI-DWI** for excluding stroke in the
  first 24–48 h — but it is **only valid in continuous AVS with nystagmus**, and is examiner-
  dependent. **HINTS-plus** adds new hearing loss (a *stroke* pointer via AICA).
- **BPPV → repositioning, not drugs;** vestibular suppressants slow recovery and should be brief.
- **Vestibular neuritis → early vestibular rehabilitation;** steroids are optional (weak evidence).
- **Vestibular migraine is now a defined diagnosis** (Bárány/IHS criteria), managed as migraine.
- **Don't provoke a neck-pain patient** (dissection risk) with positional testing.

**Key sources to cite (verify current versions):** TiTrATE/ATTEST diagnostic framework
(Newman-Toker et al.); HINTS vs early MRI-DWI studies in AVS; Bárány Society criteria for
vestibular migraine and Ménière disease; Cochrane reviews on vestibular rehabilitation and on
steroids for vestibular neuritis.
