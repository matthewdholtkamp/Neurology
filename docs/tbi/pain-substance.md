# TBI comorbidity — Pain & substance use

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

*Part of the [TBI work-up & comorbidities](index.md) hub.*

**One-liner:** chronic pain and substance use are common, **bidirectional** with TBI, and both are
worsened by the reflex reach for **opioids** — keep the plan **opioid-sparing, multimodal, and
mechanism-based**, and **screen actively.** Chronic pain affects **~40–51%** after complicated
mild-to-severe TBI (≈2× the general population), with a **median of 4 pain locations** and pain
**interference in ~55%.** Predictors: female sex, **military service**, pre-injury migraine, penetrating
injury, and poor sleep/mood/PTSD.

## Classify the pain by mechanism — it drives the drug

!!! note "Post-TBI pain phenotypes (IASP framework)"
    Mixed phenotypes are common (neuroinflammation + central sensitization) — which is *why* treatment
    must be multimodal.

    - **Nociceptive** (tissue injury/inflammation — musculoskeletal, cervicogenic, fracture) →
      **NSAIDs, physical therapy, targeted injections.**
    - **Neuropathic** (nerve lesion — occipital neuralgia, peripheral nerve injury, central pain) →
      **TCA/SNRI, gabapentinoid, nerve blocks.**
    - **Nociplastic** (altered processing without clear tissue/nerve damage — central sensitization,
      fibromyalgia-like) → **SNRI, exercise, CBT, neuromodulation** (drugs alone underperform).

## Treat — multimodal and opioid-sparing

!!! orderset "Pharmacologic ladder (dosed, with TBI-specific caveats)"
    - **TCA — nortriptyline (preferred) or amitriptyline 10–75 mg qHS** — first-line neuropathic
      (NNT ~4.6) and a **two-for-one** with sleep/[headache](headache.md) prevention. **Use
      nortriptyline** (less anticholinergic) and **start low** — anticholinergic load worsens
      [cognition](cognitive.md).
    - **SNRI — duloxetine 60–120 mg/day** (strongest evidence) or **venlafaxine XR 150–225 mg/day** —
      first-line neuropathic (NNT ~7.4); also treats comorbid [depression](mood-ptsd.md).
    - **Gabapentinoid — gabapentin 1200–3600 mg/day** or **pregabalin 150–600 mg/day** — effective for
      neuropathic pain, **but sedation/cognitive effects are a real problem in TBI** — weigh against the
      cognitive comorbidity, titrate slowly.
    - **NSAIDs** — for nociceptive/musculoskeletal pain; **limit duration** and **cap acute-medication
      days** when headache coexists (MOH, below).
    - **Topicals — lidocaine 5% patch, capsaicin** — for **localized** neuropathic pain, **minimal
      systemic/cognitive effect** — underutilized in TBI.

!!! warning "Every acute analgesic is a potential MOH contributor"
    Medication-overuse headache sits at the pain–headache intersection: **~27% of moderate–severe PTH
    patients have probable MOH at 2–4 months** (88% on simple analgesics, only 7% on a preventive).
    **Cap acute-medication days (<10–15/month by agent)** and **start preventive therapy early** in
    anyone with coexisting headache (see [headache](headache.md)).

!!! orderset "Non-drug — the disease-specific evidence"
    - **Collaborative care is the only RCT-proven chronic-pain treatment *in TBI*.** The **TBI Care RCT**
      (JAMA Netw Open 2024, n=158) — up to **12 sessions over 16 weeks** with a care manager delivering
      **CBT for pain** plus weekly expert-team review — cut **pain interference (3.46 vs 5.03)**,
      **sustained at 8 months**, and improved pain severity, depression, and anxiety.
    - **Graded aerobic exercise** — treats pain, autonomic dysfunction, and mood together (see
      [autonomic](autonomic.md)).
    - **Cervical PT** — for the very common cervicogenic component.
    - **Nerve blocks** (greater/third occipital, cervical medial branch) — high-yield for
      cervicogenic/occipital pain (also on the [headache page](../headache/post-traumatic.md)).
    - **Neuromodulation (rTMS, peripheral nerve stimulation)** — emerging; rTMS carries a weak
      recommendation for neuropathic pain.

!!! danger "Opioids — VA/DoD 2022 CPG framework, and a TBI mortality signal"
    - **Strong recommendation against initiating long-term opioid therapy** for chronic pain.
    - **Already on opioids?** Use a **collaborative, patient-centered taper** — **gradual (5–20% every
      4 weeks)** while **optimizing non-opioid therapy.** **Rapid tapering is harmful** (destabilizes
      patients, worsens depression, increases suicidal ideation, drives illicit use).
    - **Suspect OUD at any point → treat it** (**buprenorphine/naloxone**, **naltrexone**).
    - **TBI-specific:** strong opioids carry the **highest hazard for 30-day mortality after TBI
      (HR 1.41)**, and CNS-affecting drugs are far commoner in TBI patients than controls (37% vs 24%) —
      another reason to stay opioid-sparing.

## Screen the substances

!!! orderset "Substance-use screening — tools, timing, and the numbers"
    **New-onset SUD affects ~4.2% within 5 years** (global cohort, n≈1.89 M): **nicotine 2.4%, alcohol
    1.1%, cannabis 0.9%, opioids 0.4%** — and **~50% of eventual SUD is diagnosed within 3 years.** In
    active-duty airmen, mTBI raised the hazard for alcohol and nicotine dependence and drug abuse
    (highest early); among post-9/11 veterans with TBI, **26.9% received opioids** (of those, 30% also
    benzodiazepines, 36% sedative-hypnotics).

    - **Tools:** **AUDIT / USAUDIT-C** (alcohol — consider a **higher cutoff ≥11** after TBI vs the usual
      ≥8), **DAST-10** (drugs), or **TAPS** (tobacco, alcohol, prescription misuse, illicit — one
      instrument). All validated in TBI.
    - **When:** at **intake and follow-up**, with particular attention in the **first 3 years** (highest
      risk).
    - **Then:** treat and involve **behavioral health** (see [mood/PTSD](mood-ptsd.md)).

!!! note "Why TBI and substance use feed each other"
    **TBI → SUD:** reward-circuit neuroplasticity, chronic neuroinflammation / **microglial priming** (a
    "two-hit" vulnerability), impaired white-matter integrity in reward pathways, and **frontal
    disinhibition/impulsivity.** **SUD → TBI/worse recovery:** substances raise injury risk (falls,
    MVCs, assaults) and impair neuroplasticity, sleep, and medication adherence.

!!! tip "Cannabis — an explicit military caveat"
    Cannabis is the **3rd most common new-onset SUD after TBI (~0.9%)** and is increasingly used for
    chronic pain by veterans. For **active-duty service members it remains prohibited** — a
    fitness-for-duty / UCMJ issue **regardless of state law.** The **VA cannot prescribe or recommend**
    cannabis but should **discuss it openly** with transitioning/veteran patients.

*Military note:* opioid/substance issues carry fitness-for-duty, drug-testing, and readiness
implications — an added reason to stay opioid-sparing and screen early. See the [TBI hub](index.md).
