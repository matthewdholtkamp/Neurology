# TBI comorbidity — Neuroendocrine (hypopituitarism)

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

*Part of the [TBI work-up & comorbidities](index.md) hub.*

**One-liner:** **the quiet, high-yield miss.** Post-traumatic **hypopituitarism** is common, and its
symptoms — **fatigue, low mood, low libido, cognitive fog, weight change** — *mimic the rest of the TBI
picture*, so it gets blamed on "post-concussive syndrome" and never screened. It is **treatable, and it
changes disposition.** Pooled prevalence of **any** pituitary-axis dysfunction is **~33%** (higher
<3 months, much of it transient; **persistent ~15%** on strict criteria with repeat testing). By axis:
**GH ~18%, gonadal ~16%, ACTH ~10%, TSH ~6%.**

!!! note "Why the pituitary is vulnerable"
    Its blood supply runs through **long hypophyseal portal vessels** — **shearing, raised ICP, and
    hypotension** cause **ischemic necrosis**; **basal skull fracture** can directly injure the gland or
    **transect the stalk**; and **anti-pituitary/anti-hypothalamic antibodies** appear after TBI and
    predict **delayed-onset** deficiency. The **GH-secreting somatotrophs sit laterally**, fed by the
    most vulnerable portal vessels — which is why **GH is the axis most often lost.**

## Who to screen

!!! note "Screen the right patients — not every mTBI"
    - **Screen prospectively:** **complicated mild TBI** (clinically mild *but* an intracranial
      abnormality on imaging), **moderate**, and **severe** TBI.
    - **Uncomplicated mTBI:** screen **only if suggestive symptoms persist** (fatigue, low mood, low
      libido, cognitive fog, weight change, poor recovery) — testing *all* mTBI is neither realistic nor
      cost-effective.
    - **Lower the threshold** with: **basal skull fracture, diffuse axonal injury, raised ICP, prolonged
      ICU stay, older age, female sex, multiple acute-phase hormone deficiencies, radiological
      abnormalities.**

## When to screen — the timeline matters

!!! orderset "Screening timeline (what to test when)"
    1. **Acute phase (ICU) — test *only* two things:**
        - **Adrenal insufficiency** — random cortisol; **treat empirically if hemodynamically
          unstable** (below).
        - **Diabetes insipidus / SIADH** — follow **sodium and urine output.**
        - **Do NOT test GH, gonadal, or thyroid axes acutely** — critical illness confounds the results
          and acute replacement of these doesn't improve outcome.
    2. **3–6 months — first comprehensive screen (morning):** **cortisol** (± ACTH-stimulation if
       borderline), **IGF-1**, **testosterone/estradiol + LH/FSH**, **TSH + free T4**, **prolactin.**
       Watch for loss of secondary hair, new oligo/amenorrhea, sexual dysfunction, weight change,
       polydipsia, poor recovery.
    3. **12 months — repeat** if deficiencies were found at 3–6 months (some recover, some worsen) or if
       new symptoms appear.
    4. **Beyond 12 months — stay alert:** new deficiencies can emerge **even 5 years out** — re-test on
       new symptoms.

## Don't-miss emergencies

!!! danger "Acute adrenal insufficiency — treat before you confirm"
    Life-threatening in the ICU: **refractory hypotension, hyponatremia, hemodynamic instability.**
    A **random cortisol <10 µg/dL** in an acutely ill TBI patient is **highly suspicious**; **<3 µg/dL
    is diagnostic.** **If suspected and the patient is unstable, give stress-dose hydrocortisone
    50–100 mg IV q8h — do not wait for confirmatory testing.** Chronically it is subtle (fatigue, weight
    loss, orthostasis, hyponatremia) and easily mislabeled "post-concussive"; an undiagnosed patient can
    go into **adrenal crisis** with intercurrent illness — which is exactly why screening matters.

!!! warning "Sodium disorders — DI, SIADH, and cerebral salt wasting"
    - **Central DI** (~2.9% acutely, more in severe TBI): **polyuria >3 L/day, polydipsia,
      hypernatremia, dilute urine.** Usually **transient** (hours–days); **~7% permanent.**
    - **SIADH** (commoner than DI acutely): **hyponatremia, concentrated urine, euvolemia** — usually
      self-limited; **fluid restriction.**
    - **Triphasic response** (classic, rare): **DI → SIADH → permanent DI** over days as the posterior
      pituitary necroses and dumps stored AVP.
    - **Distinguish SIADH from cerebral salt wasting** — **euvolemic vs hypovolemic** hyponatremia;
      **treatment is opposite** (fluid restriction vs saline replacement). Get this right.

## Individual axes

!!! note "GH axis — most affected, hardest to diagnose"
    - **IGF-1 is a *screening* test, not diagnostic** — a **normal IGF-1 does not exclude** GH
      deficiency (sensitivity ~50%).
    - **Confirm with dynamic testing:** the **insulin tolerance test (ITT)** is gold standard;
      **glucagon stimulation** or **macimorelin** are alternatives. **GHRH-arginine is no longer
      recommended** (arginine supply).
    - **rhGH replacement** improved IGF-1, fatigue, mood, physical performance, and cognition across
      studies (with neuroimaging changes — increased cortical thickness/gray matter), and 6–12 months
      improved processing speed, memory, depression, and quality of life — but the **evidence base is
      still limited** (small, heterogeneous). The **BIAFAC** concept (benefit in patients *below normal
      but above* classic GH-deficiency cutoffs) is evolving.

!!! note "Gonadal axis — relevant to the military population"
    **Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism** is the **2nd commonest** deficiency (~16%): low libido, erectile
    dysfunction, fatigue, mood change, oligo/amenorrhea.

    - **Acute phase:** low testosterone is **expected** (critical illness) — **do not treat.**
    - **Chronic (>3–6 months):** persistent hypogonadism warrants replacement — **testosterone** in men,
      **estrogen/progesterone** in premenopausal women.
    - **Confirm it's *central*** (low/inappropriately normal **LH/FSH**) before attributing low
      testosterone to TBI rather than a primary gonadal cause — relevant given how often testosterone is
      prescribed in TBI veterans.

!!! tip "The symptom-overlap trap — the practical rule"
    Fatigue, disrupted sleep, altered cognition, weakness, exercise intolerance, and declining mental
    health can come from **either TBI or hypopituitarism** — you **cannot** tell them apart clinically.
    **The rule: if a TBI patient isn't improving despite appropriate multi-domain treatment
    ([sleep](sleep.md), [mood](mood-ptsd.md), [cognitive](cognitive.md)), check the pituitary.**

!!! note "Treat the confirmed deficiency (endocrinology)"
    Refer for **targeted hormone replacement** — replacing the deficient axis can **dramatically
    improve** fatigue, mood, and cognition that resisted everything else. Replace **glucocorticoid
    before thyroid** hormone (thyroxine can precipitate adrenal crisis in undiagnosed cortisol
    deficiency).

*Military note:* a **screenable, treatable** cause of "persistent post-concussive symptoms" that changes
the disposition — document the axis, the confirmatory test, and the replacement. See the
[TBI hub](index.md) for the retention/aeromedical framing.
