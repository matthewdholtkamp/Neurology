# TBI comorbidity — Neuroendocrine (hypopituitarism)

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

*Part of the [TBI work-up & comorbidities](index.md) hub.*

**One-liner:** **the quiet, high-yield miss.** Post-traumatic **hypopituitarism** is common after
**moderate–severe TBI (and blast mTBI)**, and its symptoms — **fatigue, low mood, low libido,
cognitive fog, weight change** — *mimic the rest of the TBI picture*, so it's blamed on "post-concussive
syndrome" and never screened. It's **treatable.**

!!! orderset "Screen the pituitary — don't blame everything on the concussion"
    - **Who:** **moderate–severe TBI**, **symptomatic blast mTBI**, and **any TBI patient not
      recovering as expected** — screen **within the first year** (and any time new hypopituitary
      symptoms appear).
    - **Send (morning):** **cortisol** (± ACTH-stimulation test if low → **adrenal insufficiency**),
      **IGF-1** (GH axis — the most commonly affected), **testosterone/estradiol + LH/FSH**, **TSH +
      free T4**, **prolactin**.
    - **Acute phase:** watch for **adrenal insufficiency** (hypotension/hyponatremia) and **diabetes
      insipidus / SIADH** — follow the **sodium**.
    - **Treat with targeted hormone replacement** (endocrinology) — replacing the deficient axis can
      **dramatically improve fatigue, mood, and cognition** that were resistant to everything else.

*Military note:* a screenable, treatable cause of "persistent post-concussive symptoms" that changes
the disposition — document it. See the [TBI hub](index.md).
