# Multiple sclerosis

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** immune-mediated demyelinating disease of the CNS, defined by lesions **disseminated
in space and time** — typically a young adult with relapsing neurologic events that recover
incompletely, accumulating disability over decades.

**Who this is for:** the provider seeing a first demyelinating event, and the one managing an
established MS patient's relapse, DMT, or board. **In a military population MS matters
disproportionately** — it presents in the 20–40 window, which is the active-duty demographic, and
it is a named referral condition under AR 40-501 3-31e.

!!! danger "🚩 Before you call it MS"
    **MS is over-diagnosed.** Misdiagnosis studies consistently find that a meaningful fraction of
    patients carrying an MS label never had it — most often because non-specific white-matter
    T2 hyperintensities (migraine, small-vessel disease) were read as demyelination, or because
    criteria were applied to a syndrome that was never a true demyelinating attack.

    **Do not diagnose MS on MRI alone.** The 2024 McDonald criteria require a compatible clinical
    syndrome or a rigorously defined radiologically isolated syndrome — not "white spots."

    **Red flags that argue against MS and for a mimic:**

    - **Normal brain MRI** in someone with suspected MS — reconsider hard.
    - **Longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis** (≥3 vertebral segments) → **[NMOSD](nmosd.md)**
      or **[MOGAD](mogad.md)**, not MS.
    - **Severe, bilateral, or steroid-dependent optic neuritis**, or optic neuritis with a swollen
      disc → MOGAD/NMOSD.
    - **Area postrema syndrome** (intractable hiccups, nausea, vomiting) → NMOSD until disproven.
    - **Meningeal signs, fever, encephalopathy, seizures** → infection, ADEM, autoimmune encephalitis.
    - **CSF pleocytosis >50 cells**, neutrophils, or very high protein → not typical MS.
    - **Absent oligoclonal bands** with an otherwise "classic" picture — still possible, but lower
      your confidence and widen the differential.
    - **Progressive myelopathy without relapses** → B12/copper deficiency, HTLV-1, dural AV fistula,
      spondylotic compression, hereditary spastic paraplegia, adrenomyeloneuropathy.
    - **Systemic features** (rash, arthritis, sicca, oral/genital ulcers, uveitis) → sarcoid, lupus,
      Sjögren, Behçet.

!!! warning "Send AQP4-IgG and MOG-IgG before you start a DMT"
    This is the single highest-consequence step on the page. **NMOSD and MOGAD are not MS**, and
    several MS drugs make NMOSD *worse* — interferon-beta, natalizumab, and fingolimod have all been
    associated with severe NMOSD attacks. Starting an MS DMT in an unrecognized NMOSD patient can
    precipitate blindness or a devastating myelitis.

    **Send cell-based assay serum AQP4-IgG and MOG-IgG in every case of optic neuritis, myelitis,
    or an atypical demyelinating presentation — before the first DMT dose.** Cell-based assay,
    not ELISA. Draw **before** or well after steroids/PLEX where possible, since treatment lowers
    titers and produces false negatives.

## Rapid approach

1. **Confirm it is a true demyelinating attack** — objective, CNS-localizing, >24 h, no fever/infection.
2. **MRI brain and full spine with and without gadolinium**, using an MS protocol.
3. **Send AQP4-IgG and MOG-IgG** (cell-based) — always, before treatment decisions.
4. **LP** for oligoclonal bands and kappa free light chain index when the diagnosis isn't already
   secure, and always when the presentation is atypical.
5. **Apply the 2024 McDonald criteria** — dissemination in space and in time.
6. **Treat the relapse** if disabling (steroids → PLEX for refractory).
7. **Start a DMT** — early, and in most cases high-efficacy.
8. **Address symptoms and the military disposition** — profile, and a DES referral under 3-31e.

## Diagnosis — the 2024 McDonald criteria

!!! note "Which version you are using"
    The current framework is the **2024 revisions of the McDonald criteria**, developed by the
    International Advisory Committee on Clinical Trials (ECTRIMS + National MS Society) and
    **published in *The Lancet Neurology* in September 2025**. The "2024" in the name refers to when
    the panel reported, not the publication date — expect to see both years cited.

    They are new enough that **published critique already exists** in *Lancet Neurology*
    correspondence, largely concerning the risk of over-diagnosis as the criteria become more
    permissive. Apply them with the mimic list above in hand.

**What changed that matters at the bedside:**

| Change | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Optic nerve is now a fifth topography for DIS** | The four classic regions (periventricular, cortical/juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord) are joined by the **optic nerve** — demonstrated by orbital MRI, OCT inter-eye asymmetry, or visual evoked potentials |
| **Kappa free light chain index** | Accepted as an **alternative to oligoclonal bands** for demonstrating dissemination in time. Automated, quantitative, and doesn't require electrophoresis |
| **Central vein sign (CVS)** | Supportive imaging biomarker; improves specificity by distinguishing demyelinating from vascular white-matter lesions |
| **Paramagnetic rim lesions (PRL)** | Supportive; marks chronic active lesions and increases diagnostic confidence |
| **Radiologically isolated syndrome can now become MS** | RIS may be diagnosed as MS when **DIS is met plus one of**: evidence of DIT, CSF positivity, or a threshold number of **CVS-positive lesions (e.g. ≥6)** |
| **Unified across the lifespan and course** | One framework for relapsing and progressive disease, paediatric through late-life presentations |

**Dissemination in space (DIS)** — lesions in **≥2 of 5** CNS regions:

1. Periventricular
2. Cortical or juxtacortical
3. Infratentorial
4. Spinal cord
5. **Optic nerve** *(new in 2024)*

**Dissemination in time (DIT)** — any one of:

- A new lesion on follow-up MRI compared with a baseline scan
- **Simultaneous enhancing and non-enhancing lesions** on a single scan
- **CSF-specific oligoclonal bands** *(substitutes for DIT)*
- **Elevated kappa free light chain index** *(new in 2024; substitutes for DIT)*
- A second clinical attack

!!! tip "Practical: how the MRI should be ordered"
    **Brain MRI with and without gadolinium**, 3 mm slices, including **3D FLAIR**, T2, T1
    pre/post, and **DWI**. **Full spine (cervical + thoracic) with and without contrast** — cord
    lesions carry diagnostic and prognostic weight and are frequently missed when only the cervical
    spine is imaged. Add **orbital MRI with fat suppression** if optic nerve involvement is the
    question. Ask the radiologist explicitly for **central vein sign** and **paramagnetic rim
    lesions** if your centre has susceptibility-weighted sequences — they now carry diagnostic weight.

## Disease course — name it, because it drives the DMT

| Phenotype | Pattern |
|---|---|
| **Clinically isolated syndrome (CIS)** | First demyelinating attack, criteria not yet fully met |
| **Radiologically isolated syndrome (RIS)** | Incidental MS-typical MRI lesions, **no clinical attack**. Under 2024 criteria a subset now qualify as MS |
| **Relapsing–remitting (RRMS)** | ~85% at onset. Discrete attacks with variable recovery |
| **Secondary progressive (SPMS)** | Gradual accumulation after an initial relapsing course; classified as **active/non-active** and **with/without progression** |
| **Primary progressive (PPMS)** | ~10–15%. Progression from onset, no early relapses; older, more male, more often myelopathic |

**PIRA — progression independent of relapse activity** — is the concept that reframed modern MS
care: disability accrues even when relapses and new lesions stop. It is why "no relapses" is not the
same as "well controlled," and why the field is chasing agents that touch smouldering inflammation.

## The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Is this a true relapse?** New or worsening objective neurologic deficit lasting **>24 hours**,
   in the absence of fever or infection. **Rule out pseudo-relapse** — a UTI, any infection, heat,
   or metabolic stress unmasking prior deficits. Check temperature and urinalysis **before** you
   reach for steroids. Treating a pseudo-relapse with steroids does harm and no good.
2. **Treat the relapse if it is disabling.** Mild sensory relapses often need nothing.
3. **Confirm the serology is back** — AQP4-IgG and MOG-IgG — before committing to an MS DMT.
4. **Start a DMT.** Do not "watch and wait" on active relapsing disease. Time to first effective
   therapy is the variable that most predicts long-term disability.
5. **Match the DMT to the patient** — disease activity, pregnancy plans, JCV status, comorbidity,
   monitoring burden, and, for a service member, **deployability of the monitoring regimen.**
6. **Treat symptoms explicitly** — spasticity, fatigue, bladder, neuropathic pain, mood, gait.
   Symptom burden drives function far more than EDSS does.
7. **Baseline the disability** — EDSS or timed 25-foot walk and 9-hole peg, documented. You will
   need it for the board and for judging DMT failure.
8. **Start the military disposition early.** MS is a named DES referral condition.

## Acute relapse management

!!! orderset "Step 1 — high-dose corticosteroids"
    - **Methylprednisolone 1000 mg IV daily × 3–5 days.** No taper required for most relapses.
    - **Oral is equivalent** at bioequivalent dose — **oral methylprednisolone 1000 mg daily × 3–5
      days** performs comparably to IV in randomised comparisons and is far easier logistically.
      Use it when IV access or infusion capacity is the constraint.
    - **Add gastroprotection** (PPI), monitor glucose (especially in diabetics), and warn about
      insomnia, mood lability, and metallic taste.
    - **Do not treat a pseudo-relapse.** Fever or infection first — treat that, reassess in days.

!!! orderset "Step 2 — plasma exchange, for steroid-refractory relapse"
    **Escalate if there is no meaningful improvement after the steroid course**, particularly for a
    severe attack (dense myelitis, severe optic neuritis with poor acuity, brainstem syndrome).

    - **PLEX — 5–7 exchanges over 10–14 days**, typically every other day.
    - **Start early.** Benefit is greatest when initiated within days to a few weeks of onset;
      waiting months forfeits most of the effect.
    - Response rates in steroid-refractory demyelinating attacks are substantial, and PLEX is
      **especially important in NMOSD**, where it should be considered **first-line alongside
      steroids** rather than as rescue.

!!! orderset "Step 3 — IVIG, when PLEX is unavailable or contraindicated"
    - **IVIG 0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days** (total 2 g/kg).
    - Evidence in acute MS relapse is **weaker than for PLEX** — treat it as the fallback, not the
      equal. It is a reasonable choice when vascular access, haemodynamic instability, or
      availability rules out exchange, and it is the preferred option in pregnancy.

!!! warning "Do not"
    - Do **not** give steroids for a pseudo-relapse.
    - Do **not** use chronic or pulsed steroids as a disease-modifying strategy — they do not alter
      the course.
    - Do **not** delay DMT initiation waiting for the relapse to fully resolve.

## Disease-modifying therapy

The central modern question is **strategy**, not just drug: start high-efficacy immediately, or
escalate from a platform agent? The field has moved decisively toward **early high-efficacy
treatment**, driven by observational data showing better long-term disability outcomes and by the
recognition that damage accrued early is not recoverable.

!!! note "The evidence status, stated honestly"
    The two randomised trials built to settle this — **DELIVER-MS** (36-month brain volume loss)
    and **TREAT-MS** (sustained EDSS-plus worsening) — are the definitive tests, and their
    **primary results were not yet published as of July 2026.** DELIVER-MS has completed
    enrolment (816 participants) and published its treatment-decision data in June 2026, so the
    primary analysis is imminent — notably, **67% of the observational cohort chose early
    high-efficacy treatment**, which tells you where practice already sits.

    Meanwhile the position has firmed. The July 2026 *NEJM* review by **Hauser** states that
    long-term prospective trials and real-world experience indicate the most effective control of
    relapsing disease is achieved by **initiating high-efficacy therapy at the earliest possible
    time.** Counsel accordingly — but say plainly that randomised confirmation is still pending.

### The tiers

=== "High-efficacy"

    **Anti-CD20 monoclonals** — the current workhorses of MS care.

    - **Ocrelizumab (Ocrevus)** — 300 mg IV × 2 doses two weeks apart, then **600 mg IV every 6
      months.** Also the **only agent approved for PPMS** (ORATORIO).
        - **Ocrevus Zunovo** is **ocrelizumab coformulated with hyaluronidase**
          (ocrelizumab/hyaluronidase-zhzo) for **subcutaneous** injection, approved for the **same
          adult indications** as IV. OCARINA II showed comparable pharmacokinetics and efficacy.
        - **Paediatric approval, 8 May 2026** — for **relapsing–remitting MS specifically**, ages
          **≥10 years and ≥25 kg (55 lb)**. Note this is **narrower than the adult label**, which
          covers *relapsing forms* including CIS and active SPMS. Based on **OPERETTA II** vs
          fingolimod (non-inferior ARR; 48% fewer new/enlarging T2 and 87% fewer enhancing lesions).
    - **Ofatumumab (Kesimpta)** — **20 mg subcutaneous monthly**, self-administered at home. The
      logistically simplest high-efficacy option and often the right answer for a mobile population.
    - **Ublituximab (Briumvi)** — IV, 1-hour infusions after the first. **Five-year ULTIMATE I/II
      open-label extension data** (*JAMA Neurology*, February 2026) confirm sustained low
      annualised relapse rate and a favourable long-term safety profile.
    - **Rituximab** — off-label, but **no longer merely "widely used."** **OVERLORD-MS**
      (*NEJM*, July 2026) is the first head-to-head randomised trial: in 216 newly diagnosed
      relapsing patients across 12 Norwegian and Swedish centres, **rituximab was non-inferior to
      ocrelizumab** for freedom from new or enlarging T2 lesions between months 6 and 24
      (**92.2% vs 94.8%**; risk difference **−2.6 percentage points**, 95% CI −9.4 to 4.3, against
      a 10-point non-inferiority margin). Clinical activity was minimal in both arms.
        - **The caveat is infection:** **82% vs 69%** of participants had an infection on rituximab
          versus ocrelizumab, though serious adverse events were similar (8% vs 7%).
        - Earlier **observational** data (*JAMA Neurology*, 2023) found **higher relapse rates**
          with rituximab in routine practice — likely reflecting dosing and interval heterogeneity
          rather than a true efficacy gap.
        - **Practical read:** rituximab is a defensible, far cheaper anti-CD20 choice with
          randomised support, provided the dosing is disciplined and you accept a higher infection
          burden. This matters a great deal for formulary-constrained care.

    !!! tip "Extended-interval dosing"
        Extending the interval between anti-CD20 doses — rather than fixed 6-monthly infusions — is
        increasingly used to **reduce infection risk, hypogammaglobulinaemia, and cost**, typically
        guided by B-cell repopulation or immunoglobulin levels. It is a proposed general strategy
        rather than a labelled regimen, so document the rationale. Relevant to the TRICARE cost
        conversation and to any patient accumulating infections on standard dosing.

    **Others:**

    - **Natalizumab (Tysabri)** — 300 mg IV every 4 weeks. Highly effective, but **PML risk** —
      stratify with **JCV antibody index**. High index plus prior immunosuppression plus long
      duration is the danger zone. A **biosimilar (Tyruko)** is available.
    - **Alemtuzumab (Lemtrada)** — induction; durable but carries **secondary autoimmunity**
      (thyroid, ITP, anti-GBM) requiring **monthly monitoring for 4 years** after the last course.
    - **Cladribine (Mavenclad)** — oral short-course induction over two years.
    - **Autologous haematopoietic stem cell transplantation (aHSCT)** — **off-label**, but it
      appears to provide **durable long-term benefit in relapsing MS**, and in selected patients
      with aggressive, highly active disease failing conventional therapy it is a legitimate
      consideration rather than a fringe option. Requires an experienced transplant centre;
      treatment-related morbidity is real. A late-stage randomised trial (**NCT04047628**) is
      ongoing. *Worth raising early in a young service member with aggressive disease, where the
      alternative is decades of accumulating disability.*

=== "Moderate — S1P modulators"

    - **Fingolimod, siponimod, ozanimod, ponesimod** — oral.
    - **First-dose bradycardia** monitoring (agent-dependent), **macular oedema** screening,
      lymphopenia, and **VZV serology + vaccination before starting.**
    - **Severe rebound disease on discontinuation** is a real hazard — never stop an S1P agent
      abruptly without a bridging plan.
    - **Siponimod** is labelled for **relapsing forms** (CIS, RRMS, and active SPMS) — not
      restricted to SPMS — but **EXPAND** is why it is the usual pick in **active SPMS**. Requires
      **CYP2C9 genotyping.**

=== "Platform / lower-efficacy"

    - **Interferon-beta** (Avonex, Rebif, Plegridy, Betaseron) — flu-like reactions, LFTs.
      **Best pregnancy safety record** of the DMTs.
    - **Glatiramer acetate** (Copaxone, Glatopa; a further generic approved January 2026) — injection
      site reactions, rare post-injection systemic reaction. Also pregnancy-friendly.
    - **Teriflunomide** — **teratogenic**, long half-life, requires **cholestyramine accelerated
      elimination**. Avoid in anyone who may become pregnant.
    - **Fumarates** — all three deliver the same active metabolite, **monomethyl fumarate**, and
      share flushing, GI upset, and **lymphopenia** (PML risk if prolonged and severe). Check
      absolute lymphocyte count before starting and periodically.
        - **Dimethyl fumarate (Tecfidera)** — the original; generic available. GI intolerance is
          the usual reason for discontinuation.
        - **Diroximel fumarate (Vumerity)** — second-generation, **bioequivalent monomethyl
          fumarate exposure** and therefore comparable efficacy, but **substantially better GI
          tolerability** — EVOLVE-MS-2 showed a **46% reduction in days with GI symptoms of at
          least moderate severity** versus dimethyl fumarate. The right switch for a patient
          failing dimethyl fumarate on tolerability rather than efficacy.
        - **Monomethyl fumarate (Bafiertam)** — the active metabolite given directly; approved for
          relapsing forms in adults.

=== "Progressive disease"

    - **Ocrelizumab** — currently the **only DMT approved for PPMS**. The effect is modest and
      concentrated in younger patients with inflammatory activity.
    - **Siponimod (Mayzent)** — approved for **relapsing forms of MS in adults**, which by label
      includes CIS, RRMS, **and active SPMS** — the same broad indication most relapsing-MS DMTs
      carry. It is **not label-restricted to active SPMS**, though **EXPAND** is the trial that
      makes it the natural choice there. Requires **CYP2C9 genotyping**.

    **BTK inhibitors — the actual state of play.** This class targets B cells *and* CNS-resident
    microglia, and because these agents are brain-penetrant they are the leading attempt to reach
    the **compartmentalised inflammation** behind a closed blood–brain barrier that drives
    progression. Recent trials have sorted the class sharply.

    - **Fenebrutinib** — a **highly selective, non-covalent** BTK inhibitor, and as of mid-2026
      the most convincing agent in the class. Three positive phase 3 trials:
        - **FENtrepid (PPMS)** — met its primary endpoint of **non-inferiority to ocrelizumab** for
          12-week composite confirmed disability progression, with a **12% risk reduction**
          (HR 0.88; 95% CI 0.75–1.03). Presented ACTRIMS, February 2026. Genentech/Roche describe
          it as the first investigational agent in over a decade to reduce disability progression
          in PPMS.
        - **FENhance 1 and 2 (relapsing MS)** — both met their primary endpoint versus
          teriflunomide, with ARR reductions of **51.1%** and **58.5%** (n≈1,497 combined, 96
          weeks), plus reduced risk of disability worsening (26% and 20%). Presented AAN, 2026.
          Liver enzyme elevations >3× ULN were comparable to teriflunomide.
        - **Not yet FDA-approved** — filings were to be submitted together with FENtrepid.
          If approved it would be the **first high-efficacy oral** covering both relapsing MS
          and PPMS.
    - **Tolebrutinib** — brain-penetrant but **relatively non-selective**, and its programme is
      mixed at best. Present it accurately:
        - **HERCULES (non-relapsing SPMS)** — **positive**: a **31% reduction in the risk** of
          6-month confirmed disability progression (**HR 0.69; 95% CI 0.55–0.88; P=0.003**).
          *Note this is a hazard-ratio risk reduction, not a "delay" — the sponsor's press
          language differs from the NEJM primary analysis.*
        - **GEMINI 1 and 2 (relapsing MS)** — **failed.** Not superior to teriflunomide on
          annualised relapse rate (0.13 vs 0.12 and 0.11 vs 0.11; n=1,873). A pooled 6-month
          confirmed-disability-worsening signal favoured tolebrutinib (**29% risk reduction,
          nominal P=0.023**) but **could not be formally tested** because the hierarchical
          testing plan had already failed.
        - **PERSEUS (PPMS)** — **failed** its primary endpoint (6-month composite confirmed
          disability progression); MRI measures improved only. **Sanofi will not pursue PPMS
          registration.**
        - **Regulatory:** **FDA Complete Response Letter, December 2025** — **not approved in the
          US.** EMA granted conditional marketing authorisation, and the UAE approved it for
          non-relapsing SPMS in August 2025. Given the GEMINI and PERSEUS results, treat near-term
          US approval as genuinely uncertain rather than imminent.
    - **Remibrutinib** — in phase 3 (**REMODEL-1** and **REMODEL-2**); no primary results yet.

    **The honest bottom line for non-active progressive MS:** outside ocrelizumab in PPMS and
    siponimod in active SPMS, **there is still no DMT that clearly helps**, and fenebrutinib —
    though the most promising candidate the field has had — is not yet approved. Say this plainly
    rather than implying a treatment exists. **Rehabilitation, exercise, symptom management, and
    vascular risk control** remain the interventions that change these patients' lives.

### Choosing — the practical decision table

| Situation | Reasonable choice |
|---|---|
| Newly diagnosed, active RRMS | **Anti-CD20** (ofatumumab if self-injection suits; ocrelizumab if infusion is easy) |
| Highly active / aggressive at onset | Anti-CD20, natalizumab (if **JCV negative**), or alemtuzumab/cladribine induction |
| Wants pregnancy soon | **Glatiramer** or **interferon**; or anti-CD20 timed so infusion precedes conception. **Avoid teriflunomide entirely** |
| JCV antibody positive, high index | **Avoid natalizumab**; anti-CD20 instead |
| Needs oral, low monitoring | S1P modulator or fumarate — accept lower efficacy |
| Established PPMS | **Ocrelizumab** if inflammatory activity; otherwise rehabilitation-led care |
| **Active SPMS** | **Siponimod** (EXPAND) or an **anti-CD20** — "active" means relapses or new/enhancing lesions, and that activity is what predicts benefit. For **non-relapsing** SPMS there is no approved option in the US (tolebrutinib received a CRL) |
| Cost or formulary constrained | **Rituximab** — now supported by randomised non-inferiority data (OVERLORD-MS), accepting a higher infection rate |
| Aggressive disease failing high-efficacy therapy | Consider **aHSCT** referral to an experienced centre |
| Breakthrough on a platform agent | **Escalate to high-efficacy** — do not switch laterally |

!!! warning "Before the first dose of any DMT"
    **Screen:** CBC with differential, CMP, **hepatitis B (HBsAg + anti-HBc)**, hepatitis C, HIV,
    **TB (quantiferon)**, **VZV IgG**, and pregnancy test. **Vaccinate before starting** — live
    vaccines are contraindicated once immunosuppressed, and **anti-CD20 blunts vaccine response**,
    so complete the schedule **≥4 weeks before** the first infusion where possible.

    **Hepatitis B reactivation** with anti-CD20 is a named, preventable catastrophe. Check it every
    time. **Monitor immunoglobulins** on long-term anti-CD20 — hypogammaglobulinaemia accumulates
    over years and drives infection risk.

!!! tip "TRICARE — MS prescribing"
    MS DMTs are **high-cost specialty drugs and are PA-gated across the board** — expect prior
    authorisation for essentially every agent, usually with a **neurologist prescriber
    requirement** and documentation of diagnosis, MRI activity, and prior therapy.

    - **Infused agents** (ocrelizumab, natalizumab, rituximab, alemtuzumab) are typically handled as
      a **medical benefit** rather than pharmacy — the authorisation pathway differs and often runs
      through the MTF or regional contractor rather than Express Scripts.
    - **Self-injected and oral agents** (ofatumumab, S1P modulators, fumarates, teriflunomide,
      glatiramer, interferons) run through the **specialty pharmacy** benefit.
    - **Generics** — glatiramer, dimethyl fumarate, fingolimod, and teriflunomide now have generic
      entrants, which usually eases the PA burden considerably.

    **Verify every agent at the TRICARE Formulary Search before counseling**, and start the PA early
    — specialty authorisations are the usual cause of a months-long gap between decision and first
    dose. For an active-duty patient that gap is disease activity you cannot get back.

## Symptomatic management

Symptom control determines function more than any DMT does. Ask about all of it — patients
under-report.

| Symptom | First-line |
|---|---|
| **Spasticity** | Baclofen 5–10 mg TID titrated (max ~80 mg/day); tizanidine 2–4 mg TID (sedation, hypotension, LFTs); stretching and PT throughout. Intrathecal baclofen or botulinum toxin for focal/severe |
| **Fatigue** | Screen for **sleep apnoea, depression, thyroid, anaemia, and deconditioning first** — treat what you find. Exercise has the best evidence. Amantadine 100 mg BID, modafinil 100–200 mg daily |
| **Neurogenic bladder** | **Post-void residual first** — it determines the pathway. Retention → intermittent catheterisation. Storage/urgency → antimuscarinic or **mirabegron** (fewer cognitive effects); botulinum toxin for refractory detrusor overactivity. Treat UTIs promptly — they trigger pseudo-relapses |
| **Neuropathic pain / Lhermitte** | Gabapentin, pregabalin, duloxetine, amitriptyline |
| **Trigeminal neuralgia** | Common in MS and often bilateral — see [trigeminal neuralgia](../headache/trigeminal-neuralgia.md). Carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine first |
| **Depression** | Prevalent and under-treated; **suicide risk is elevated in MS.** SSRI/SNRI plus psychotherapy. Screen at every visit |
| **Cognitive impairment** | Processing speed most affected. Formal neuropsych testing when duty or employment is in question |
| **Gait / walking speed** | **Dalfampridine 10 mg BID** (extended release) — improves walking in a responder subset. **Contraindicated with seizure history or renal impairment** |
| **Heat intolerance (Uhthoff)** | Cooling strategies, activity timing, hydration. **Operationally decisive** — see the military box |

!!! military "Military medicine — multiple sclerosis"
    *Framework — verify against current **AR 40-501**, **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2**, **DoDI 1332.18**, and
    service aeromedical policy before counseling. See the full process on
    [Profiles, MEB/MAR2 & deployability](../military/deployability.md).*

    - **Retention:** **AR 40-501 para 3-31e** names **"multiple sclerosis, optic neuritis,
      transverse myelitis, and similar demyelinating disorders"** directly among the causes for
      referral to the **DES**. The DoD floor is **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.f** — "chronic or
      recurrent demyelinating processes" — which is gated by **5.26.a** (must persist despite
      appropriate treatment **and** impair duty performance). **Cite both.**
    - **Referral is expected, and expected early.** Under AR 40-501 3-3d, a profiling provider who
      identifies a Chapter 3 condition **should initiate a permanent profile at the time of
      identification**, which is what prompts the DES referral. A confirmed MS diagnosis is not a
      wait-and-see profile.
    - **Deployability:** MS is **deployment-limiting** on several independent grounds, and it is
      worth naming them separately because they persist even in a patient doing well:
        - **Heat intolerance (Uhthoff's phenomenon)** — a fundamental incompatibility with hot
          climates, MOPP gear, and sustained exertion. This alone is often decisive.
        - **Infusion-dependent therapy** — an every-6-month infusion requirement is not supportable
          in most deployed settings. *Self-injected ofatumumab is the DMT most compatible with
          field conditions — worth weighing when the patient may remain in service.*
        - **Monitoring burden** — MRI surveillance, labs, JCV index, immunoglobulins.
        - **Unpredictable relapse** with visual, motor, or cognitive deficit.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary during the diagnostic work-up and acute relapse;
      **permanent** once the diagnosis is established. Write **functional** limits in Section 4 —
      heat exposure, sustained exertion, load carriage, vision, alertness — not just "MS."
    - **EPTS / LOD:** MS onset frequently predates diagnosis by years, so **EPTS is genuinely
      contested** in service members. Document the first attributable symptom carefully, review
      pre-accession records, and address service aggravation explicitly. This determination has
      large downstream consequences for the Soldier.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** MS is **disqualifying for flying duty.** Assume permanent
      suspension and confirm the class-specific standard.
    - **What carries the board:** relapse dates and objective deficits, MRI lesion burden and new
      activity over time, EDSS or timed 25-foot walk and 9-hole peg, documented DMT trials, and
      concrete duty impact. See the [worked example](../military/deployability.md) of profile
      language that carries versus language that gets bounced.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

**What MS actually is.** An adaptive-immune attack on CNS myelin with early and ongoing axonal
loss. The relapsing phase is driven by peripheral lymphocyte trafficking across the blood–brain
barrier — which is why drugs that deplete B cells or block trafficking work so well early. The
progressive phase is dominated by **compartmentalised inflammation behind a closed barrier**:
meningeal lymphoid aggregates, chronic active (rim) lesions, and microglial activation. That
compartmentalisation is precisely why peripherally acting DMTs lose traction in progressive disease,
and why brain-penetrant strategies like BTK inhibition were pursued.

**Epstein–Barr virus** is now regarded as a necessary, though not sufficient, cause — the 2022
Bjornevik cohort of over 10 million US military personnel showed a **32-fold increased risk of MS
after EBV seroconversion**, with no comparable signal for other viruses. The military relevance is
direct: that landmark study was built on Department of Defense serum repository samples.

**Why "early and effective" won the argument.** Relapse-associated worsening and PIRA both
contribute to disability, but the inflammatory window in which treatment demonstrably alters the
trajectory is early. Axonal loss is not recoverable, brain volume loss proceeds silently, and
patients who reach an EDSS milestone rarely come back. The observational literature consistently
favours early high-efficacy therapy; **DELIVER-MS** and **TREAT-MS** are the randomised tests, and
they had not reported primary results as of July 2026.

**Where the field is heading.** Two frontiers are worth knowing about even though neither is
standard care. **BTK inhibitors** are the first serious attempt to modulate CNS-compartmentalised
disease — the *Lancet Neurology* 2026 class review describes them as potential harbingers of
treatments that reach processes behind an intact blood–brain barrier, and fenebrutinib's three
positive phase 3 trials are the strongest evidence yet that the strategy works. **CD19-directed
therapy** is the other: anti-CD20 agents spare CD20-negative plasmablasts and plasma cells, and
CD19-targeted monoclonals or **CAR T cells** reach that compartment. Case series in autoimmune
disease have reported dramatic benefit, and MS trials are beginning. Neither belongs in a
counselling conversation yet, but both explain where the next decade of DMTs comes from.

**Key trials and evidence:**

- **OPERA I/II** — ocrelizumab vs interferon beta-1a in RRMS; superior relapse and MRI outcomes.
  Established anti-CD20 as front-line therapy.
- **ORATORIO** — ocrelizumab in PPMS; the first positive trial in primary progressive disease.
- **ASCLEPIOS I/II** — ofatumumab vs teriflunomide; brought subcutaneous self-administered
  high-efficacy therapy into practice.
- **AFFIRM / SENTINEL** — natalizumab efficacy, and the trials that surfaced **PML**.
- **CARE-MS I/II** — alemtuzumab; durable effect, secondary autoimmunity.
- **EXPAND** — siponimod in SPMS; benefit concentrated in **active** disease.
- **ULTIMATE I/II** — ublituximab; **5-year extension data**, *JAMA Neurology* 2026.
- **OVERLORD-MS** — **rituximab non-inferior to ocrelizumab** in newly diagnosed relapsing MS
  (*NEJM*, July 2026); more infections with rituximab. The first anti-CD20 head-to-head.
- **HERCULES** — tolebrutinib in non-relapsing SPMS; **31% reduction in risk** of 6-month confirmed
  disability progression (HR 0.69). **US outcome: Complete Response Letter, December 2025.**
- **GEMINI 1/2** — tolebrutinib **failed** vs teriflunomide in relapsing MS.
- **PERSEUS** — tolebrutinib **failed** in PPMS; PPMS registration abandoned.
- **FENtrepid** — **fenebrutinib non-inferior to ocrelizumab in PPMS** (HR 0.88), ACTRIMS 2026.
- **FENhance 1/2** — fenebrutinib vs teriflunomide in relapsing MS; **51.1%** and **58.5%** ARR
  reductions, AAN 2026.
- **OPERETTA II** — ocrelizumab vs fingolimod in paediatric RRMS; basis of the May 2026 approval.
- **EVOLVE-MS-2** — diroximel vs dimethyl fumarate; **46% fewer** days with moderate-or-worse GI
  symptoms.
- **Bjornevik et al., Science 2022** — EBV and MS causality, from the DoD serum repository.
- **2024 revisions of the McDonald criteria**, *Lancet Neurology*, September 2025.
