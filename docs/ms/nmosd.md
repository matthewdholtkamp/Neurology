# Neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD)

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** an antibody-mediated astrocytopathy — **not a variant of MS** — in which AQP4-IgG
attacks aquaporin-4 water channels on astrocytes, producing severe optic neuritis and
longitudinally extensive myelitis with **poor recovery and high relapse-related disability**.

**Who this is for:** anyone evaluating optic neuritis or myelitis. The entire reason this page
exists is that **treating NMOSD as MS can blind or paralyse the patient.**

!!! danger "🚩 Why this distinction is urgent"
    **Disability in NMOSD is relapse-driven.** Unlike MS, there is no meaningful progressive phase —
    patients accumulate disability in **discrete, devastating attacks.** A single untreated
    myelitis can end ambulation; a single severe optic neuritis can end vision. Preventing the
    next attack is the whole game.

    **Several MS drugs make NMOSD worse.** **Interferon-beta**, **natalizumab**, and **fingolimod**
    have all been associated with severe NMOSD exacerbations. Starting one in a misdiagnosed
    patient is an iatrogenic catastrophe.

    **Therefore: send cell-based serum AQP4-IgG in every optic neuritis and every myelitis, and
    do not start an MS DMT until it is back.**

## When to suspect it over MS

| Feature | Points to NMOSD |
|---|---|
| **Myelitis** | **Longitudinally extensive** — ≥3 contiguous vertebral segments. Central cord, often with swelling and enhancement |
| **Optic neuritis** | **Severe, bilateral or rapidly sequential**, posterior/chiasmal, poor visual recovery, steroid-dependent |
| **Area postrema syndrome** | **Intractable hiccups, nausea, or vomiting** lasting days — a highly specific NMOSD presentation and often the *first* attack. Frequently misread as GI disease |
| **Brain MRI** | Often normal early, or lesions in AQP4-rich regions — periependymal around third/fourth ventricles, hypothalamus, dorsal brainstem, area postrema |
| **CSF** | **Pleocytosis can be marked** (>50 cells, sometimes neutrophilic); oligoclonal bands usually **absent** (~15–30%) |
| **Demographics** | Female predominance is **strong** (~9:1), median onset older than MS, over-represented in Black and Asian populations |
| **Coexisting autoimmunity** | Lupus, Sjögren, thyroid disease, myasthenia gravis |
| **Recovery** | **Poor** — incomplete recovery from attacks is the norm |

!!! warning "Seronegative NMOSD"
    Roughly **10–25%** of clinically definite NMOSD is **AQP4-IgG negative.** A negative test does
    **not** exclude the diagnosis.

    In seronegative patients, **send MOG-IgG** — a substantial share prove to be
    **[MOGAD](mogad.md)**, which is a different disease with a different prognosis and treatment.
    **That is the key point about the 10–25% figure: much of it is not seronegative NMOSD at all,
    it is unrecognised MOGAD.**

    **True double-negative NMOSD** (negative for both AQP4-IgG and MOG-IgG) is a diagnosis of
    careful exclusion requiring the stricter 2015 clinical criteria — and it looks biologically
    different. A **2026 systematic review** found it **highly relapsing (73.6% relapsed)** with a
    female-to-male ratio of only about **1.5:1**, against roughly **9:1** in AQP4-positive disease.
    Do not assume it behaves like seropositive NMOSD; treat it aggressively (see below), but
    recognise the evidence base is thin.

    **Test with a cell-based assay, not ELISA.** ELISA generates false positives at low titre that
    have caused real harm. And **draw before steroids/PLEX** where feasible — treatment suppresses
    titres and produces false negatives.

## Diagnosis (2015 international consensus criteria)

**With AQP4-IgG positive:** ≥1 core clinical characteristic + positive cell-based assay +
exclusion of alternatives.

**Without AQP4-IgG (or unknown status):** stricter — **≥2 core characteristics** from ≥1 attack,
with **at least one being optic neuritis, longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis, or area
postrema syndrome**, plus **dissemination in space**, plus supporting MRI findings, plus exclusion
of alternatives.

**The six core clinical characteristics:**

1. Optic neuritis
2. Acute myelitis
3. **Area postrema syndrome** — otherwise unexplained hiccups, nausea, vomiting
4. Acute brainstem syndrome
5. Symptomatic narcolepsy or acute diencephalic syndrome with NMOSD-typical MRI lesions
6. Symptomatic cerebral syndrome with NMOSD-typical brain lesions

## The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Recognise the pattern** — LETM, severe/bilateral optic neuritis, or area postrema syndrome.
2. **Send cell-based AQP4-IgG and MOG-IgG immediately**, before steroids where possible.
3. **Treat the attack hard and early** — steroids **and** PLEX. Do not wait to see if steroids work
   in a severe attack.
4. **Start maintenance therapy without waiting for a second attack.** In AQP4-positive NMOSD,
   the first attack is sufficient indication.
5. **Choose an FDA-approved agent** over off-label rituximab where access permits.
6. **Screen for meningococcal disease risk** before any complement inhibitor.
7. **Screen for coexisting autoimmunity.**
8. **Start the military disposition** — this is a DES referral condition.

## Acute attack treatment

!!! orderset "Step 1 — steroids AND plasma exchange, together"
    - **Methylprednisolone 1000 mg IV daily × 5 days.**
    - **PLEX 5–7 exchanges over 10–14 days**, typically every other day.

    **The key difference from MS:** in NMOSD, **PLEX is not rescue therapy — it is first-line
    alongside steroids** for any severe attack. Do not run a full steroid course, wait, reassess,
    and only then consider exchange. Earlier PLEX predicts better visual and motor outcomes, and
    the therapeutic window is measured in days.

    For a severe attack — poor acuity, dense myelitis — **start both in parallel.**

!!! orderset "Step 2 — if refractory"
    - **IVIG 0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days** where PLEX is unavailable or contraindicated.
    - Escalate maintenance therapy immediately; a breakthrough attack means the current regimen
      has failed.
    - Consider **prolonged oral steroid taper** as a bridge while maintenance therapy takes effect —
      unlike in MS, bridging matters here because the relapse risk is immediate and high.

## Maintenance therapy — prevent the next attack

**Four monoclonal antibodies are FDA-approved for AQP4-IgG-seropositive NMOSD.** In a disease where
disability is entirely relapse-driven, this is the intervention that changes the patient's life.

| Agent | Target | Dosing | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Eculizumab (Soliris)** | **C5** complement | IV **every 2 weeks** | PREVENT trial. **Meningococcal vaccination mandatory** |
| **Ravulizumab (Ultomiris)** | **C5** complement | IV **every 8 weeks** | **Approved 2024** (CHAMPION-NMOSD). Same target as eculizumab, engineered for a longer half-life — **q8wk instead of q2wk** |
| **Inebilizumab (Uplizna)** | **CD19** B cells | IV **every 6 months** | N-MOmentum. Broader B-cell depletion than anti-CD20 (includes plasmablasts) |
| **Satralizumab (Enspryng)** | **IL-6 receptor** | **Subcutaneous**, monthly after loading | SAkuraSky/SAkuraStar. Self-administered — the most deployable-friendly option |

!!! note "Eculizumab biosimilars do not carry the NMOSD indication"
    Two interchangeable eculizumab biosimilars are FDA-approved — **Bkemv (eculizumab-aeeb)** and
    **Epysqli (eculizumab-aagh)** — but both were approved under a **"skinny label"** covering only
    **PNH and atypical HUS.** **NMOSD remains a Soliris-only indication** among eculizumab products,
    as does generalised myasthenia gravis.

    This matters for formulary navigation: a payer or pharmacy substituting a biosimilar for an
    NMOSD patient would be dispensing off-label. Specify the reference product.

**Off-label but genuinely useful options:**

| Agent | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Tocilizumab** | **IL-6 receptor** (same as satralizumab) | **TANGO** (*Lancet Neurol* 2020, phase 2, open-label, n=118): superior to azathioprine in highly relapsing NMOSD — **median time to first relapse 78.9 vs 56.7 weeks, P=0.0026.** Real-world data show ARR falling from ~1.9 to ~0.1. Included in the **2026 MENACTRIMS guidelines** alongside the approved agents, and a **substantially cheaper** route to IL-6 blockade when satralizumab PA is denied |
| **Rituximab** | CD20 | **375 mg/m² weekly × 4**, or **1000 mg × 2 doses two weeks apart**; re-dose **every 6 months** or guided by **CD19/CD20 B-cell reconstitution.** See the caveats below |
| **Mycophenolate mofetil** | Antiproliferative | Widely used globally; 5-year relapse-free survival ~**51%** |
| **Azathioprine** | Antiproliferative | Slow onset — requires steroid bridging for months; 5-year relapse-free survival ~**35%** |

!!! warning "How good are the approved agents *really* — read the comparative data carefully"
    The often-quoted figure is **5-year relapse-free survival of 100% for the C5 inhibitors,
    inebilizumab, and satralizumab versus 69.7% for rituximab** (with MMF **51.1%** and
    azathioprine **35.3%**). That comes from **Bilodeau et al., 2026** — a **single-centre
    retrospective cohort** at Mass General Brigham (176 patients, 2000–2024, median 9 years'
    follow-up), **not head-to-head randomised data.**

    **The nuance matters, and it cuts against a simple "approved beats rituximab" reading.** When
    the same study combined efficacy *with* safety and tolerability into a composite
    relapse/serious-infection/treatment-limiting-event-free endpoint:

    - **C5 inhibitors 91%** vs **rituximab 55%** at 5 years — the C5 inhibitors clearly separate.
    - **Inebilizumab 38%** and **satralizumab 79%** (3-year data), and **neither was statistically
      superior to rituximab** on the composite: **inebilizumab HR 1.23 (95% CI 0.24–3.12)**,
      **satralizumab HR 1.01 (95% CI 0.16–2.68)**.

    **So: the C5 inhibitors have the strongest comparative case. For inebilizumab and satralizumab,
    the advantage over rituximab is a relapse-prevention signal that does not survive into a
    composite including harms** — in a small single-centre cohort with wide confidence intervals.
    Do not overstate it to a patient or a formulary reviewer.

    Rituximab's own cost is concrete: in that cohort **28% of rituximab treatment periods involved
    a serious infection**, 34% a mild infection, and 13% hypogammaglobulinaemia. Long-term
    anti-CD20 exposure can produce **hypogammaglobulinaemia requiring indefinite IVIG replacement**
    — monitor immunoglobulins on every patient, indefinitely.

!!! note "Indirect ranking — the network meta-analysis"
    A **Bayesian network meta-analysis** (*J Neurol*, 2025; 8 RCTs, 851 patients, 84% seropositive)
    ranked **ravulizumab first** for time to relapse — **HR 0.00 (95% CrI 0.00–0.03), SUCRA 0.99**,
    a 98% probability of being the superior agent — followed by **eculizumab (0.76)**,
    **tocilizumab (0.49)**, **rituximab (0.47)**, and **inebilizumab (0.45)**.

    These are **indirect comparisons across trials with different populations and endpoints**, not
    head-to-head data, and the ravulizumab point estimate of exactly 0.00 reflects **zero
    adjudicated relapses** in CHAMPION-NMOSD rather than infinite efficacy. Treat the ranking as
    directional support for complement inhibition, not as a league table.

!!! danger "Stopping or switching a complement inhibitor — rebound risk"
    A provider who successfully stabilises a patient on eculizumab needs to know this **before**
    any future switch, including one forced by formulary change or deployment.

    **Disease reactivation clusters in the first ~3 months after eculizumab is stopped**, including
    in patients relapse-free for years. Severe attacks have been reported **around five half-lives
    after the last dose** — eculizumab's half-life is only about **11 days**, so protection is
    gone within weeks. One published case describes a severe attack **11 weeks** after the final
    infusion during a switch to satralizumab.

    Other series report uneventful switches, so this is a **real risk rather than an inevitability**
    — but it must be planned for:

    - **Never stop a complement inhibitor without an overlapping bridge** to the next agent.
    - **Account for onset lag** — anti-CD20 and IL-6 agents take weeks to months to protect.
    - Consider **steroid cover** through the transition.
    - **Ravulizumab's half-life is roughly 5× longer than eculizumab's** (~64 vs ~11 days), which
      gives a considerably wider and more forgiving window if a switch away from complement
      inhibition becomes necessary.

!!! warning "Seronegative NMOSD — no approved options"
    **All four FDA-approved agents are indicated only for AQP4-IgG-positive disease.** For
    seronegative patients the mainstays remain **rituximab, mycophenolate mofetil, and
    azathioprine** — this is a genuine unmet need, not a preference.

    **Treat aggressively anyway.** A 2025 multicentre study found high-efficacy immunosuppression
    reduced relapse risk comparably in both groups — **HR 0.27 in AQP4-positive and HR 0.21 in
    seronegative** disease. Seronegativity is not a reason to under-treat.

!!! danger "Before any complement inhibitor — meningococcal risk"
    Eculizumab and ravulizumab block terminal complement and produce a **profound, lasting
    susceptibility to *Neisseria meningitidis*.** Both carry a **boxed warning**.

    - **Vaccinate against meningococcus (ACWY and B) at least 2 weeks before the first dose.**
    - If treatment cannot wait 2 weeks, vaccinate and give **antibiotic prophylaxis** to cover
      the interval.
    - Give the patient a **wallet card** and explicit instructions: **any fever is an emergency.**
    - Revaccinate on schedule for the duration of therapy.

!!! warning "Do not use in NMOSD"
    **Interferon-beta, natalizumab, and fingolimod** are associated with **worsening NMOSD** and
    are contraindicated. If a patient labelled "MS" deteriorates unexpectedly after starting one of
    these, **send AQP4-IgG immediately** — you may be watching a misdiagnosis declare itself.

    **Extend the caution to the whole S1P class** — siponimod, ozanimod, ponesimod. The published
    case-series evidence is largely for fingolimod, but the mechanism of concern (lymphocyte
    **sequestration** rather than depletion) is shared across the class. There is no reason to
    take the risk when effective alternatives exist.

!!! tip "TRICARE — NMOSD prescribing"
    All four approved agents are **high-cost biologics requiring prior authorisation**, with a
    neurologist prescriber requirement and documentation of AQP4 seropositivity. The infused agents
    (eculizumab, ravulizumab, inebilizumab) typically run through the **medical benefit**;
    subcutaneous **satralizumab** through **specialty pharmacy**.

    **Start the authorisation the day you make the diagnosis.** In a relapse-driven disease, an
    authorisation delay is not administrative friction — it is unprotected time during which an
    attack can cost vision or ambulation. Bridge with steroids if there is any lag.
    **Verify current criteria at the TRICARE Formulary Search.**

## Monitoring and long-term care

- **Immunoglobulins** — check at baseline and periodically on any B-cell-depleting agent.
  Hypogammaglobulinaemia accumulates over years and can require **indefinite IVIG replacement**.
- **Meningococcal vaccination status** — track and revaccinate on schedule for the whole duration
  of complement inhibition.
- **Relapse surveillance** — clinical, with MRI as indicated. Attacks are stepwise, so document
  **visual acuity, fields, and objective motor/sensory findings** at each visit; you cannot
  reconstruct increments retrospectively.
- **Screen for coexisting autoimmunity** — lupus, Sjögren, thyroid disease, myasthenia gravis.

!!! note "GFAP — an emerging relapse biomarker"
    **Serum/plasma glial fibrillary acidic protein**, released from damaged astrocytes, is under
    study for confirming and predicting relapse — which is mechanistically apt, since NMOSD is an
    astrocytopathy. In a real-world IL-6-blockade cohort, **baseline plasma GFAP >220 pg/mL** was
    associated with substantially higher subsequent relapse risk (reported OR ~20).

    **Not yet standard of care** and not a substitute for clinical judgement, but worth knowing
    about as it moves toward the clinic.

!!! tip "Pregnancy — plan it deliberately"
    With a **female-to-male ratio near 9:1**, most NMOSD patients are women of childbearing age,
    and this conversation should happen early rather than at the positive test.

    - The **2026 MENACTRIMS guidelines** recommend planning conception after **≥12 months of
      disease stability**, and **resuming treatment early postpartum** — the postpartum period
      carries elevated relapse risk.
    - Safety data across agents are **limited**; several appear relatively safe in pregnancy but
      none is well characterised. Coordinate with maternal-fetal medicine.
    - **Do not simply stop therapy on a positive pregnancy test** — an unplanned withdrawal,
      especially of a complement inhibitor, carries the rebound risk described above. Plan the
      transition.

!!! note "Inebilizumab now carries indications beyond NMOSD"
    Inebilizumab (Uplizna) has since been approved for **IgG4-related disease** and for
    **generalised myasthenia gravis** (AChR- or MuSK-positive adults). Not directly relevant to
    NMOSD management, but it can change formulary positioning and the PA pathway — worth knowing
    when arguing an authorisation.

!!! military "Military medicine — NMOSD"
    *Framework — verify against current **AR 40-501**, **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2**, and **DoDI 1332.18**.
    See [Profiles, MEB/MAR2 & deployability](../military/deployability.md).*

    - **Retention:** **AR 40-501 para 3-31e** covers **"multiple sclerosis, optic neuritis,
      transverse myelitis, and similar demyelinating disorders"** — NMOSD falls squarely within it,
      and its two defining syndromes are named in the paragraph explicitly. The DoD floor is
      **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.f**, gated by **5.26.a**. Residual visual or motor deficit also
      engages **5.26.c** (CNS sequelae) and, for diplopia, **AR 40-501 3-7b**.
    - **Deployability: assume non-deployable.** NMOSD is materially less compatible with the
      operational environment than MS:
        - **Relapse-driven catastrophic disability** — an attack in theatre can cost vision or
          ambulation before evacuation completes.
        - **Infusion-dependent maintenance** on a fixed schedule (q2wk to q6mo).
        - **Complement inhibitors create a standing meningococcal risk** that is difficult to manage
          forward and dangerous in a crowded field environment.
        - *If retention is being contemplated, **satralizumab** is the only self-administered
          subcutaneous option and the only regimen with any field plausibility.*
    - **Profile:** **permanent**, with functional limits reflecting visual acuity, gait, bladder,
      and heat tolerance.
    - **MEB/DES:** refer on diagnosis. Document attack dates and residual deficits objectively —
      **visual acuity and fields, EDSS or timed walk** — since NMOSD disability is stepwise and the
      board needs the increments, not a narrative.
    - **Aeromedical:** disqualifying for flying duty.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

**The mechanism explains the phenotype.** AQP4 is the dominant water channel on astrocyte
end-feet, and it is most densely expressed exactly where NMOSD strikes: the optic nerves, the
central grey matter of the spinal cord, and the circumventricular organs — including the **area
postrema**, which lacks a blood–brain barrier and is therefore directly exposed to circulating
antibody. That anatomical fact is why intractable hiccups and vomiting are a *neurologic* presenting
syndrome, and why the attack is an **astrocytopathy** with secondary demyelination rather than a
primary attack on myelin. Complement activation following AQP4-IgG binding drives the necrotic,
cavitating lesions that account for the poor recovery — and is precisely why C5 inhibition works
so well.

**The therapeutic era changed the disease.** Before 2019, NMOSD was managed with off-label
immunosuppression and carried a grim trajectory. Four approved biologics later, relapse-free
survival at five years approaches 100% in trial and comparative cohorts. Few neurologic diseases
have been transformed so completely in so short a span — which is exactly why the misdiagnosis
question matters so much. The cost of calling NMOSD "MS" is no longer a mislabelled chart; it is a
forfeited, highly effective treatment.

**Key trials:**

- **PREVENT** — eculizumab; dramatic relapse reduction in AQP4-positive NMOSD.
- **CHAMPION-NMOSD** — ravulizumab; extended q8-week dosing, **FDA approval 2024**.
- **N-MOmentum** — inebilizumab (anti-CD19).
- **SAkuraSky / SAkuraStar** — satralizumab (anti-IL-6R), with and without background therapy.
- **Wingerchuk et al., 2015** — international consensus diagnostic criteria.
