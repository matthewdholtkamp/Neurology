# Cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA)

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** amyloid-β deposition in cortical and leptomeningeal small vessels — the **leading
cause of *lobar* spontaneous ICH in older adults**, and a driver of vascular cognitive impairment.
The two decisions that matter: **recognize it** (lobar bleed, microbleeds, superficial siderosis on
MRI) and **keep it away from anticoagulation**, because it re-bleeds.

!!! danger "🚩 Recognize the CAA phenotypes — and don't reflexively anticoagulate/antiplatelet"
    - **Lobar intracerebral hemorrhage** in an older adult (cortical/subcortical, often recurrent) —
      the classic presentation (contrast with deep/hypertensive bleeds).
    - **Transient focal neurological episodes ("amyloid spells")** — recurrent, **stereotyped,
      spreading** sensory/motor/visual symptoms (often from **cortical superficial siderosis**). They
      **mimic TIA — but anticoagulating or thrombolysing them can be catastrophic** (high ICH risk).
      Image before you treat as ischemia.
    - **Cognitive impairment** — CAA contributes to dementia (and co-exists with Alzheimer's).
    - **Convexity (non-aneurysmal) SAH** in the elderly.

## Diagnose — MRI markers & Boston criteria 2.0

!!! orderset "Get an MRI with a blood-sensitive sequence (GRE or SWI)"
    **Hemorrhagic markers (strictly *lobar* distribution):** lobar **cerebral microbleeds**, **cortical
    superficial siderosis (cSS)**, lobar ICH, convexity SAH. **Non-hemorrhagic markers (Boston 2.0):**
    **severe enlarged perivascular spaces in the centrum semiovale** and **multispot white-matter
    hyperintensities.**

    - **Boston criteria v2.0 (2022)** added cSS and the **non-hemorrhagic white-matter features** —
      now allowing a diagnosis of **probable CAA even without a hemorrhage.** Deep microbleeds point
      *away* from CAA (toward hypertensive small-vessel disease). **Know its blind spot:** validation
      shows **~75% sensitivity / 95% specificity overall — but only ~55% sensitivity for *non-ICH*
      (cognitive) presentations** vs ~90% for ICH. CAA can be advanced enough to impair cognition
      *before* it meets the hemorrhagic criteria.
    - **Edinburgh CT criteria (when there's no MRI):** **subarachnoid blood + finger-like projections**
      on CT (spec ~87%, sens ~81%) support CAA in a lobar bleed — and they're now **prognostic**: an
      IPD meta-analysis tied the Edinburgh risk tiers to **5-year recurrent-ICH rates of ~12% / 16% /
      26%** (low/intermediate/high). Best used as a **rule-in** tool.
    - **CSF biomarkers (adjunct):** CAA lowers **both Aβ42 *and* Aβ40** (Alzheimer's lowers Aβ42
      only) — useful to distinguish the two and to flag concomitant CAA before anti-amyloid therapy.
    - **CAA-related inflammation (CAA-ri):** subacute encephalopathy, seizures, or headache with
      **asymmetric confluent white-matter vasogenic edema** on MRI — **steroid-responsive**;
      recognize it because it's treatable (immunosuppression), unlike CAA itself.

## The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Suspect CAA** from a lobar bleed, amyloid spells, or lobar microbleeds/cSS.
2. **MRI with GRE/SWI**; apply **Boston 2.0**; consider CAA-ri if there's vasogenic edema.
3. **Aggressive blood-pressure control** — the one intervention that clearly lowers recurrence.
4. **Antithrombotic decision — risk-stratify** (bleeding phenotype vs. thrombotic need); it's *not*
   a blanket "never."
5. **For AF needing stroke prevention:** weigh **DOAC vs. LAAO vs. antiplatelet** by re-bleed risk.
6. **CAA-ri → immunosuppression** (steroids, below); **screen before anti-amyloid dementia drugs**
   (ARIA risk).

## Management

!!! orderset "No CAA-specific drug — manage the bleeding risk (and don't over-restrict)"
    - **Blood pressure control is the dominant lever** — long-term **<130/80** reduces recurrent ICH.
    - **Anticoagulation is *high-risk, not absolutely forbidden* — stratify** (2026 NEJM review). CAA
      re-bleeds at **~7–8%/year** vs ~1% for non-CAA ICH, amplified by cortical superficial siderosis.
        - **AF + microbleeds only (no ICH, no cSS):** a **DOAC may be reasonable.** (COCROACH IPD
          meta-analysis: OAC after ICH in AF cut ischemic MACE **HR 0.27** with **no statistically
          significant** rise in hemorrhage.)
        - **Prior lobar ICH, disseminated cSS, or multiple bleeds:** **avoid anticoagulation** →
          **LAAO**, antiplatelet monotherapy, or a trial. Pooled LAAO data in AF + prior ICH: ischemic
          stroke and recurrent-ICH each **~2%/year** (~4% in the CAA/parenchymal-hemorrhage subgroup);
          RCTs vs. DOAC are still needed.
        - **If you anticoagulate, use a DOAC (not warfarin)**, and if resuming after an ICH, the
          risk–benefit balance is best around **~7–8 weeks** post-bleed.
    - **Antiplatelets — safer than long assumed:** **RESTART** showed antiplatelet *resumption* after
      ICH did **not** significantly increase recurrent ICH (aHR ~0.51); the 2022 AHA/ASA guideline
      rates resumption **"reasonable/may be considered."** Use for a clear vascular indication.
    - **Statins:** CAA-specific data are mixed; individualize (don't reflexively continue high-intensity
      statin after a lobar bleed without weighing it).
    - **CAA-related inflammation → immunosuppress (see box).**

    !!! info "Recurrence — stratify, don't just say "low""
        **Highest risk (~7.4%/year):** multifocal/disseminated **cSS**, multiple prior ICHs, **APOE
        ε2 or ε4**, higher BP; **cSS progression** on serial MRI independently predicts new ICH (HR
        ~3.8), and baseline cSS / hemorrhagic presentation predict recurrence (OR ~3.4 / ~5.0).
        **Lowest risk (~6% over 5 years):** probable CAA on **microbleeds alone** (no ICH, no cSS).

    !!! warning "Thrombolysis in a CAA patient — cortical siderosis is the danger marker"
        In the **AcT** thrombolysis analysis, **cortical superficial siderosis burden** was the
        dominant harmful marker — **each affected sulcus raised symptomatic ICH (aOR ~3.9)** and
        mortality. Notably, meeting **Boston v2.0** was *not* associated with hemorrhagic outcomes
        (the older hemorrhagic-only criteria were) — so the **non-hemorrhagic v2.0 markers don't carry
        the same bleeding risk**; **cSS does.** Factor cSS into thrombolysis decisions.

!!! orderset "CAA-related inflammation (CAA-ri) — treat it, it works"
    CAA-ri (subacute encephalopathy/seizures/headache + asymmetric vasogenic edema) is the treatable
    face of CAA. Immunosuppression **clearly helps** (JAMA Neurol series): treated → **94% clinical vs
    50% untreated** improvement, and **far less recurrence (26% vs 71%, HR ~0.19).**

    - **First episode:** **IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day × 3–5 days → oral prednisone ~60 mg tapered
      over months.**
    - **Recurrent/refractory:** cyclophosphamide, mycophenolate, or rituximab.

!!! warning "The ARIA connection — screen (and know the thresholds) before anti-amyloid antibodies"
    The **anti-amyloid dementia mAbs (lecanemab, donanemab) cause ARIA** (edema/effusion **ARIA-E**,
    microhemorrhage/siderosis **ARIA-H**) — and underlying CAA amplifies it. Get a **baseline GRE/SWI
    MRI** and weigh:

    - **APOE ε4 homozygotes:** ARIA-E ~**33–45%** (vs ~5–10% in non-carriers), with reported fatal
      cases — **the EMA excluded ε4 homozygotes** from lecanemab's authorization.
    - **Pre-treatment microhemorrhages (≥2–4)** raise ARIA-E (~OR 2.5); **cortical superficial
      siderosis** raises it (~OR 2.2) and is generally a **contraindication.**
    - **Elevated blood pressure (MAP >107)** is the sole *modifiable* ARIA risk factor — control it.

    This is the practical bridge between CAA and the anti-amyloid dementia workup.

## Disposition

Acute lobar ICH → see the [intracerebral hemorrhage page](intracerebral-hemorrhage.md) (ICU, BP,
reversal). Otherwise outpatient neurology with **BP optimization, antithrombotic-risk counseling**,
cognitive assessment, and a clear note in the chart that **anticoagulation is high-risk.**

!!! military "Military medicine — cerebral amyloid angiopathy"
    *Framework — verify against current **AR 40-501**, **DoDI 6130.03**, and aeromedical policy.*

    - **Older/retiree and veteran population:** CAA is largely a disease of older adults — more
      relevant to **retiree/VA care and MEB dispositions** than to young active-duty members.
    - **Retention:** CAA presenting as **intracerebral hemorrhage** falls under **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2
      para 5.26.b(1)**; persistent sequelae (severe headaches, seizures, cognitive deficit) under
      **5.26.c** — Army **AR 40-501 3-31f/k** → **DES** where duty is impaired.
    - **Key counseling point:** flag in the record that **anticoagulation is high-risk** — it affects
      any future AF/VTE management decision.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **CAA is the top cause of lobar ICH in the elderly** — recognize the phenotypes (lobar bleed,
  amyloid spells, lobar microbleeds, cortical superficial siderosis).
- **Boston criteria v2.0 (2022)** added **cSS and non-hemorrhagic markers** (diagnose without a
  hemorrhage) — but it's **insensitive for cognitive (non-ICH) presentations (~55%).** **Edinburgh CT
  criteria** help when there's no MRI and now carry **prognostic** weight.
- **Anticoagulation is high-risk, not absolutely banned — stratify.** Microbleeds-only AF may take a
  **DOAC** (COCROACH); prior ICH / disseminated cSS → **LAAO** or antiplatelet. **Warfarin is out** in
  favor of DOACs. **Antiplatelet resumption is reasonable** (RESTART — no significant ICH excess).
- **Recurrence is *not* uniformly "low"** — ~7.4%/year in the high-risk (cSS/multiple ICH/APOE)
  group; ~6%/5 y in microbleeds-only. **cSS is the key danger marker** — also for **thrombolysis**
  (AcT: each affected sulcus ↑ symptomatic ICH).
- **CAA-related inflammation is immunosuppression-responsive** — treat it (94% vs 50% improve;
  steroids → taper).
- **CAA drives ARIA risk** — screen APOE ε4 homozygotes / microbleeds / cSS before anti-amyloid mAbs.
- **On the horizon:** vascular Aβ deposits begin **30–40 years before the first ICH** (long
  preclinical window); the **first disease-modifying trial** — an **APP-targeting siRNA** — is
  enrolling sporadic and Dutch-type CAA.

**Key sources:** Greenberg SM, *Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy* (NEJM 2026 — comprehensive review);
Boston criteria v2.0 (Charidimou et al., Lancet Neurol 2022); CAA imaging reviews;
2022 AHA/ASA spontaneous ICH guideline; anti-amyloid mAb ARIA data.
