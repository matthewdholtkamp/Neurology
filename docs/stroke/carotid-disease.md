# Carotid artery disease

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** atherosclerotic stenosis of the extracranial internal carotid — a fixable source of
stroke. The whole decision turns on **two questions: is it *symptomatic*, and *how tight*?** —
because **symptomatic high-grade stenosis is a revascularize-within-2-weeks emergency**, while
asymptomatic disease is increasingly a **best-medical-therapy** call.

!!! danger "🚩 Symptomatic carotid stenosis is time-critical"
    **"Symptomatic"** = an **ipsilateral TIA, ischemic stroke, or amaurosis fugax (transient monocular
    blindness)** in the **last 6 months.** These patients have a **high early recurrence risk** —
    **revascularize within 2 weeks**, the window where benefit is greatest. Don't let a "stable"
    carotid lesion sit while you arrange elective surgery.

## Grade the stenosis

- **Screen/measure with carotid duplex ultrasound**, **confirm with CTA or MRA** (NASCET method:
  narrowest lumen vs. the normal distal ICA).
- **Symptomatic categories:** **70–99%** (clear benefit), **50–69%** (benefit in selected patients),
  **<50%** (medical therapy only). **Asymptomatic:** typically discussed at **≥70%.**

!!! info "Look beyond the stenosis number — plaque morphology and two easy-to-miss lesions"
    Stroke risk depends on **stenosis severity *and* plaque morphology** (ESVS/SVS 2022–23).

    - **Vulnerable-plaque features** — **intraplaque hemorrhage (IPH)**, ulceration, a lipid-rich
      necrotic core on **MRI vessel-wall imaging** — independently raise recurrent-stroke risk (PARISK).
      **A *symptomatic* low-grade (<50%) plaque with IPH/ulceration can be the culprit** even though it
      doesn't meet a revascularization threshold — an evolving area; treat medically and consider
      vessel-wall imaging in "cryptogenic" ipsilateral events.
    - **Carotid web** — a shelf-like intimal projection at the carotid bulb; a cause of **recurrent
      stroke in the young** (notably younger women) that's **easily missed** on routine studies —
      look for it on CTA in an otherwise-cryptogenic anterior-circulation stroke; antithrombotic ±
      revascularization.
    - **Near-occlusion** — revascularization benefit is **uncertain/reduced**; often managed medically.

## The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Is it symptomatic?** (ipsilateral TIA/stroke/amaurosis in 6 months) — if yes, this is urgent.
2. **Grade the stenosis** (duplex → CTA/MRA).
3. **Start best medical therapy for everyone** (statin, antiplatelet, BP, risk factors) — *now.*
4. **Symptomatic 70–99% (or selected 50–69%): revascularize within 2 weeks** — CEA first-line.
5. **Asymptomatic ≥70%:** shared decision — intensive medical therapy vs. revascularization by risk /
   life expectancy / procedural risk.
6. **Choose the procedure** (CEA vs CAS vs TCAR) by anatomy, age, and surgical risk.

## Best medical therapy — for everyone (symptomatic or not)

!!! orderset "Start this in every carotid patient"
    - **High-intensity statin — atorvastatin 80 mg**, target **LDL <70** (add ezetimibe/PCSK9 as needed).
    - **Antiplatelet** — aspirin (or clopidogrel); short-course **DAPT** only in the acute
      minor-stroke/high-risk-TIA window (see [prevention](prevention.md)).
    - **BP control toward <130/80**, **diabetes** management, **smoking cessation**, diet/exercise.
    - This is the backbone — revascularization is *added* to it, never a substitute.

## Revascularize — who, when, and how

!!! orderset "Symptomatic stenosis"
    - **70–99% → revascularize, ideally within 2 weeks** of the event (**CEA** first-line for
      standard-risk patients). Benefit falls off with delay.
    - **50–69% → revascularize in *selected* patients** — greater benefit in **men, older patients,
      hemispheric (vs retinal) events**; individualize.
    - **<50% → medical therapy only.**
    - **Timing caveat:** avoid revascularizing a **large acute infarct** in the first ~48 h
      (hemorrhage risk) — coordinate with stroke neurology.

!!! orderset "Asymptomatic stenosis (≥70%) — a shared decision"
    - **Intensive medical therapy is the default comparator** and is often sufficient. Offer
      **revascularization** when peri-procedural stroke/death risk is **<3%** and life expectancy is
      **>5 years**, weighing plaque features and progression.
    - *Evolving evidence:* the **CREST-2** program (reported 2025) tested revascularization **added to
      intensive medical management** in asymptomatic ≥70% stenosis — **carotid stenting + IMM reduced
      stroke vs IMM alone, while endarterectomy added no significant benefit over IMM.** Interpret as
      **newly-reported and practice-shifting-but-not-yet-fully-digested** — confirm against the
      published guideline update before quoting.

!!! orderset "Procedure choice — CEA vs CAS vs TCAR"
    - **Carotid endarterectomy (CEA)** — first-line for most; lower peri-procedural **stroke** than
      transfemoral stenting (especially **age >70**), at the cost of slightly more **MI**.
    - **Transfemoral carotid artery stenting (CAS)** — for **high surgical risk / hostile neck /
      re-stenosis**, or younger patients; higher peri-procedural stroke in the elderly.
    - **Transcarotid artery revascularization (TCAR)** — stenting via a small neck incision with
      **flow reversal**; **lower stroke than transfemoral CAS** in registries (RCT-level data still
      maturing); FDA-approved for standard-risk since 2022.

## Disposition

Symptomatic high-grade stenosis → **urgent vascular surgery / neuro-interventional referral (target
<2 weeks)** with medical therapy started immediately. Asymptomatic → optimize medical therapy and a
shared-decision revascularization discussion. All → the full
[secondary-prevention plan](prevention.md).

!!! military "Military medicine — carotid disease"
    *Framework — verify against current **AR 40-501**, **DoDI 6130.03**, and aeromedical policy.*

    - **Population skew:** symptomatic atherosclerotic carotid disease is largely an **older /
      retiree / veteran** issue; in young active-duty stroke, think **dissection** first
      ([dissection page](cervical-artery-dissection.md)).
    - **Retention:** carotid disease that causes **stroke/TIA** is a cerebrovascular condition under
      **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.b** (stroke b(3); **vascular stenosis b(2)**; recurrent TIA b(6)
      *unless etiology identified and definitively treated*), Army **AR 40-501 3-31f** — a
      **revascularized, asymptomatic** carotid with no residual deficit may retain per specialty review.
    - **Deployability / aeromedical:** symptomatic disease and the peri-procedural period are
      deployment- and flight-status-limiting/waiver-dependent.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **Symptomatic 70–99% stenosis = revascularize within 2 weeks** (CEA first-line; NASCET/ECST) —
  early benefit is large and time-sensitive.
- **50–69% symptomatic** benefits *selected* patients (men, older, hemispheric events); **<50%**
  is medical only.
- **Best medical therapy for everyone** — high-intensity statin (LDL <70), antiplatelet, BP <130/80,
  risk-factor control — and it's the yardstick asymptomatic disease is measured against.
- **CEA vs CAS vs TCAR:** CEA lowest peri-procedural stroke (esp. >70); CAS for high surgical risk;
  **TCAR** (flow reversal) outperforms transfemoral stenting in registries.
- **Asymptomatic disease is shifting** — intensive medical therapy is highly competitive; **CREST-2**
  (2025) suggests stenting + IMM lowers stroke vs IMM alone with no added CEA benefit (interpret
  cautiously, very recent).
- **Beyond the % — morphology matters:** **intraplaque hemorrhage/ulceration** (MRI vessel-wall
  imaging) raises risk and can make a **symptomatic <50% plaque** the culprit; don't miss a **carotid
  web** in young cryptogenic stroke; **near-occlusion** gains little from revascularization.

**Key sources:** NASCET/ECST (symptomatic CEA); CREST (CEA vs CAS); CREST-2 (asymptomatic, 2025);
ESVS 2023 and SVS carotid guidelines; TCAR registry data.
