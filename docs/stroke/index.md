# Acute ischemic stroke

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** sudden focal neurologic deficit from arterial occlusion — **"time is brain."** Every
minute of a large-vessel occlusion loses ~1.9 million neurons. Your job on the code is to move
fast, in order: **last-known-well → glucose → CT → blood pressure → reperfusion decision.**

> *The ER consult usually sounds like:* "Older patient, weak on one side, started a couple hours
> ago — they're in the scanner now, no NIHSS yet, no labs." Your value is imposing the checklist on
> the chaos. Be fast and a little abrupt; it saves brain.

!!! danger "🚩 The clock is the whole game — establish these first"
    - **Last known well (LKW), not "time found."** The entire reperfusion algorithm keys off this
      number. Nail it from the patient, family, EMS, or phone records.
    - **Check a glucose immediately** — hypo/hyperglycemia is the great stroke mimic. It is the
      **only mandatory lab** before thrombolysis.
    - **Get the patient to non-contrast CT now** (+ CTA head/neck if no contraindication) — you are
      excluding hemorrhage and finding a large-vessel occlusion.
    - **Don't over-treat blood pressure.** In a possible reperfusion candidate the target is
      **<185/110**; otherwise leave it alone (permissive hypertension) unless **>220/120**.
    - **Mimics:** hypoglycemia, seizure/Todd's, complex migraine, Bell's palsy, functional,
      toxic-metabolic. But **a focal deficit is a stroke until proven otherwise** — don't stall the
      code chasing a mimic.

## Acute stroke checklist — what I want done, in order

!!! orderset "Code stroke — the sequence (target door-to-needle ≤30 min)"
    1. **LKW + glucose** — the two numbers that gate everything.
    2. **Focused exam + NIHSS** (compact scale below). Note anticoagulant/antiplatelet use.
    3. **STAT non-contrast CT head; CTA head & neck** (skip CTA only if true contrast contraindication
       — most kidneys tolerate it, don't wait on a creatinine in a code).
    4. **Labs** (don't wait on them except glucose): CBC/platelets, PT/INR/aPTT, BMP, troponin, type
       & screen, hCG if applicable; **ECG + telemetry**.
    5. **Two large-bore IVs.** Manage **BP to <185/110** *only if* pursuing lytic (labetalol 10–20 mg
       IV or nicardipine gtt 5–15 mg/h).
    6. **Reperfusion decision** — thrombolysis? thrombectomy? both? (below). **Consent in parallel,
       don't let it delay the bolus.**
    7. **Weigh the patient** (dosing is weight-based; don't guess high).
    8. **If LVO:** activate neuro-interventional / arrange transfer **immediately** — in parallel with
       giving lytic ("drip and ship").

### Compact NIHSS (rapid field version)

A fast, practical aid — not a substitute for the formal 15-item NIHSS you'll document.

| Domain | Quick check |
|---|---|
| **1a–1c LOC** | Alert / drowsy / stupor; month + age; blink eyes + squeeze hand |
| **2 Gaze** | Eyes midline, partial gaze palsy, or forced deviation |
| **3 Visual fields** | Full / partial / complete / bilateral hemianopia |
| **4 Facial palsy** | Symmetric / minor / partial / complete |
| **5–6 Motor arm/leg** | Arm at 90° (10 s), leg at 30° (5 s): drift → falls → no movement, each limb |
| **7 Ataxia** | Finger-nose, heel-shin — out of proportion to weakness |
| **8 Sensory** | Pinprick: normal / mild / severe loss |
| **9 Language** | Aphasia: none / mild / severe / mute-global |
| **10 Dysarthria** | Normal / slurred / unintelligible |
| **11 Extinction/neglect** | None / one modality / profound |

*NIHSS ≤3 = "minor"; ≥6 with cortical signs (gaze, aphasia, neglect) predicts a **large-vessel
occlusion** — get the CTA and call for thrombectomy.*

## Imaging & the reperfusion fork

- **Non-contrast CT:** rules out hemorrhage; look for early ischemic change / ASPECTS.
- **CTA head & neck:** finds the **LVO** (ICA, M1/M2, basilar) and any tandem/dissection.
- **CT perfusion (or MRI DWI/FLAIR):** needed for **extended-window** decisions (late thrombolysis
  and 6–24 h thrombectomy) — quantifies **core vs. salvageable penumbra**.

Two independent decisions, made in parallel — **a patient can get both:**

## Reperfusion — Step 1: IV thrombolysis

!!! orderset "IV thrombolysis — offer within 4.5 h of LKW (Class 1)"
    **Two equivalent agents (2026 AHA/ASA — both Class 1); tenecteplase is the practical default:**

    - **Tenecteplase (TNK) 0.25 mg/kg IV, single bolus (max 25 mg)** — one push, no infusion, no
      line to babysit; easier for transfers. **Preferred where available.**
    - **Alteplase (tPA) 0.9 mg/kg IV (max 90 mg)** — **10% as a bolus over 1 min, remainder over
      60 min.**

    **Before you push, confirm the four:** *"Do I have the CT, the glucose, the blood pressure
    (<185/110), and the ECG?"* Weight-based dose — **weigh, don't eyeball.**

    !!! danger "Absolute contraindications (do NOT give lytic)"
        - **Hemorrhage on CT**, or extensive early infarct >⅓ MCA territory.
        - **BP that won't come below 185/110** despite treatment.
        - **Glucose <50** and deficit resolves with correction.
        - **Platelets <100k, INR >1.7, elevated aPTT / therapeutic heparin ≤48 h, or a DOAC ≤48 h**
          (unless specific reversal/normal assay).
        - **Ischemic stroke, intracranial/spinal surgery, or serious head trauma within 3 months;**
          known intracranial neoplasm (intra-axial), AVM, or aneurysm at risk; **active internal
          bleeding**; infective endocarditis; suspected **aortic dissection**.

    !!! note "Historically 'absolute,' now RELATIVE (weigh case-by-case — the 2026 guideline liberalized these)"
        Rapidly-improving or mild-but-disabling deficits, **seizure at onset** (if deficit is from
        the stroke, not the postictal state), major surgery <14 d, GI/GU bleed <21 d, recent MI,
        pregnancy/early postpartum, recent arterial puncture. **Don't reflexively exclude — discuss
        with stroke/neuro.**

    **The "million-dollar presentation" (say this to consent/activate):** *"\_\_-year-old with
    \[risk factors] presents with acute \[deficit/syndrome], LKW \[time]. BP \_\_, NIHSS \_\_, glucose
    \_\_, INR \_\_, platelets \_\_. CT shows \[no bleed], CTA shows \[occlusion/patent]. No lytic
    contraindication."*

    !!! quote "Consent, in plain language (benefit is at 90 days, not at the bedside)"
        Thrombolysis is the best chance of returning to independence after stroke — roughly a **~10%
        absolute increase in good outcome at 3 months.** The risk is bleeding: about **6% symptomatic
        intracranial hemorrhage** vs ~1% without. **Overall mortality is not increased.** The benefit
        is not seen immediately — it shows up over the following weeks.

!!! orderset "Extended-window thrombolysis (perfusion-guided) — 'reasonable' (not first-line)"
    When >4.5 h or wake-up/unknown onset **with a favorable perfusion/DWI–FLAIR mismatch:**

    - **Wake-up / unknown onset:** DWI–FLAIR mismatch (WAKE-UP) **or** perfusion mismatch → lytic is
      reasonable up to **~9 h from the sleep midpoint**.
    - **4.5–9 h from LKW** with salvageable penumbra on automated perfusion (EXTEND) → reasonable.
    - Always weigh **thrombectomy** in parallel for an LVO — it's often the better late option.

## Reperfusion — Step 2: endovascular thrombectomy (LVO)

!!! orderset "Mechanical thrombectomy — for large-vessel occlusion; give lytic too if eligible"
    Thrombolysis and thrombectomy are **not either/or** — a lytic-eligible LVO patient gets **both**
    (don't wait to see if the lytic works). Activate neuro-IR / transfer the moment CTA shows an LVO.

    - **Anterior LVO (ICA, M1 ± proximal M2), 0–6 h:** thrombectomy, **Class I** (with ASPECTS
      generally ≥6).
    - **6–16 h:** **Class I** if **DAWN or DEFUSE-3** clinical–imaging mismatch met (perfusion/DWI).
    - **16–24 h:** **Class IIa** by DAWN criteria.
    - **Large-core infarct (low ASPECTS 3–5):** still benefits — **SELECT2, ANGEL-ASPECT** (2023)
      expanded eligibility; select with imaging, involve neuro-IR.
    - **Basilar-artery occlusion:** thrombectomy up to **~12 h (ATTENTION)** and **~24 h (BAOCHE)** —
      posterior strokes are devastating and under-treated; **don't anchor on a low NIHSS.**

## After reperfusion — the post-lytic order set

!!! orderset "Post-thrombolysis orders (first 24 h)"
    - **ICU/stroke-unit monitoring ≥24 h.** Neuro checks + **BP q15 min ×2 h → q30 min ×6 h → q1 h**
      to complete 24 h.
    - **Keep BP <180/105 ×24 h** (labetalol pushes ± nicardipine gtt).
    - **Hold all antithrombotics ×24 h** — no aspirin/clopidogrel/heparin/prophylactic anticoagulation.
    - **Avoid for 24 h:** central lines, arterial sticks, NG tube, urinary catheter, LP (or any
      non-compressible puncture).
    - **A statin is fine to start** (high-intensity — see secondary prevention).
    - **Repeat non-contrast CT at 24 h** before starting antithrombotics. **Any acute
      decline → STAT CT** (think symptomatic hemorrhage).

!!! danger "Symptomatic ICH after thrombolysis — stop and reverse"
    Sudden decline, new headache, vomiting, or BP spike within 24 h:

    1. **STOP the lytic infusion.** **STAT non-contrast CT.**
    2. **Labs:** CBC/platelets, PT/INR/aPTT, **fibrinogen**, type & cross; repeat q2–4 h until stable.
    3. **Cryoprecipitate 10 units IV** (replaces fibrinogen — first-line); recheck fibrinogen, **goal
       >150 mg/dL**, repeat as needed.
    4. **Tranexamic acid 1 g IV** (or aminocaproic acid) as antifibrinolytic.
    5. **Platelets** if thrombocytopenic; correct any coagulopathy.
    6. **Neurosurgery** for possible decompression/EVD; treat elevated ICP; frequent neuro checks.

## Blood pressure — the rules change by pathway

| Situation | Target | How |
|---|---|---|
| **Before thrombolysis** | **<185/110** | Labetalol 10–20 mg IV, or nicardipine 5–15 mg/h gtt |
| **After thrombolysis (24 h)** | **<180/105** | Same; nicardipine/clevidipine gtt if labile |
| **Post-thrombectomy** | Individualized (often **<180**, lower if fully reperfused) | Avoid over-lowering a still-occluded vessel |
| **No reperfusion (permissive HTN)** | **Treat only if >220/120**, then lower ~15% | Restart home meds ~24–48 h once stable |

## Not a reperfusion candidate? Start antiplatelet therapy

!!! orderset "Antiplatelet — after 24 h if lysed, or now if not lysed (and CT shows no bleed)"
    - **Aspirin 160–325 mg** within 24–48 h (rectal/NG if dysphagic) — the one proven early drug for
      everyone not getting a lytic.
    - **Minor stroke (NIHSS ≤3) or high-risk TIA (ABCD² ≥4): short-course DAPT.** Start **aspirin +
      clopidogrel** (clopidogrel 300–600 mg load → 75 mg/day; ASA 81 mg) for **21 days**, then
      **single-agent** — the benefit is concentrated in the first 21 days (CHANCE/POINT); longer
      DAPT just adds bleeding.
        - **Ticagrelor + aspirin** (THALES) is an alternative, useful with **CYP2C19 loss-of-function**
          (clopidogrel non-responders).

## Secondary prevention — driven by the mechanism (start before discharge)

!!! orderset "Every patient"
    - **High-intensity statin — atorvastatin 80 mg** (or rosuvastatin 20–40); **target LDL <70**
      (add ezetimibe ± PCSK9 to get there). Start in hospital.
    - **BP control** (restart/optimize once stable), **diabetes** management, **smoking cessation**,
      diet/exercise, **dysphagia screen before anything PO**, DVT prophylaxis, early mobilization.

=== "Cardioembolic (atrial fibrillation)"
    - **Anticoagulate — a DOAC** (apixaban/rivaroxaban/dabigatran/edoxaban) preferred over warfarin
      for non-valvular AF.
    - **Timing:** modern trials (**ELAN, OPTIMAS, TIMING**) show **early DOAC start is safe** — no
      need for the old rigid "1-3-6-12" wait. Reasonable to start **within a few days** (sooner for
      small strokes, a bit later for large infarcts / hemorrhagic transformation). Individualize on
      infarct size and bleeding risk.
    - **Left-atrial-appendage occlusion** if anticoagulation is truly contraindicated.

=== "Large-artery atherosclerosis"
    - **Antiplatelet + high-intensity statin (LDL <70).**
    - **Symptomatic carotid stenosis 70–99% (or 50–69% in selected men):** **CEA or CAS**, best
      **within 2 weeks.**
    - **Intracranial atherosclerosis:** short-course DAPT (SAMMPRIS) + aggressive risk-factor control;
      avoid stenting first-line.

=== "Small-vessel (lacunar)"
    - Antiplatelet + **aggressive BP and lipid control**; short DAPT for the minor-stroke window only.

=== "ESUS / cryptogenic"
    - **Prolonged rhythm monitoring** (≥30-day or implantable) to catch occult AF.
    - **PFO** in a patient **<60 with an otherwise-cryptogenic embolic stroke** → **closure** (RESPECT/
      CLOSE) with antiplatelet.
    - **Hypercoagulable/young-stroke work-up** as indicated.

## TIA — a treated emergency, not "a pass"

- **ABCD² score** stratifies early stroke risk; a TIA carries a substantial **90-day stroke risk**,
  front-loaded to the **first 48 h.**
- **Expedited work-up same day:** vessel imaging (CTA/MRA neck+head), MRI (up to half of "TIAs" show
  infarct), ECG/telemetry, echo.
- **Start secondary prevention immediately** — **21-day DAPT for high-risk TIA (ABCD² ≥4)**, statin,
  and mechanism-directed therapy. Don't send a real TIA home without a plan.

## Supportive care & admission

!!! orderset "Stroke-unit admission bundle"
    - **Dysphagia screen before any PO** (meds, food, water) — aspiration is a top early killer.
    - **Glucose 140–180**; treat fever (worsens outcome); **normoxia** (O₂ only if <94%).
    - **DVT prophylaxis** — **intermittent pneumatic compression** immediately; pharmacologic
      prophylaxis once bleeding risk allows (and ≥24 h post-lytic).
    - **Early mobilization + therapy** (PT/OT/SLP); swallow eval; secondary-prevention teaching.
    - **Telemetry ≥24–48 h** hunting for AF.

## Disposition

Stroke-unit or ICU (all post-lytic/post-thrombectomy). Transfer any **LVO** to a thrombectomy-capable
center emergently. Admit TIAs or observe with an **expedited same-day work-up** — the early recurrence
risk is real.

!!! military "Military medicine — stroke / cerebrovascular disease"
    *Framework — verify against current **AR 40-501**, **DoDI 6130.03**, **DoDI 1332.18**, the
    **JTS Clinical Practice Guideline "Management of Stroke and Cerebrovascular Emergencies in the
    Deployed Setting" (03 Jul 2024)**, and service aeromedical policy before counseling.*

    - **Deployed setting:** stroke in theater is a **telemedicine + evacuation** problem — thrombolysis
      and thrombectomy are rarely available forward. Follow the **JTS deployed-stroke CPG**: rapid
      recognition, glucose, CT if available, BP management, and **urgent MEDEVAC** to a capability.
    - **Young-stroke work-up matters more here:** service members skew young — pursue **dissection**
      (blast/blunt/neck trauma), **PFO**, hypercoagulable states, endocarditis, and drug use; the
      etiology drives both treatment and the board.
    - **Deployability:** an acute stroke/TIA is **non-deployable** during work-up and recovery;
      residual deficit or a high-risk unfixed source is deployment-limiting.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary during recovery; **permanent** for residual
      neuro/cognitive deficit or seizure.
    - **Retention / MEB:** stroke with **persistent functional deficit, cognitive impairment, or
      seizures** generally falls below retention standards → **DES (MEB/PEB)**. Document NIHSS/mRS,
      deficits, etiology, and the secondary-prevention plan.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** a history of stroke/TIA is **disqualifying for flying duty**,
      and most secondary-prevention regimens (anticoagulation especially) are independently
      waiver-dependent. Confirm the exact standard for the class involved.

    !!! tip "TRICARE — secondary-prevention prescribing"
        The acute drugs (tenecteplase, alteplase, thrombectomy) are inpatient — not formulary-gated.
        For **outpatient secondary prevention**, high-intensity statins, aspirin, and clopidogrel are
        **generic/formulary**; **DOACs** are covered (a **preferred DOAC** exists on the Uniform
        Formulary — **verify the current preferred agent and any PA at the TRICARE Formulary Search**,
        as DoD P&T revises tiers).

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **The whole field is time + vessel.** Two parallel decisions: **thrombolysis** (drug, ≤4.5 h or
  perfusion-selected later) and **thrombectomy** (large-vessel occlusion, out to 24 h). Eligible
  patients get **both.**
- **Tenecteplase has arrived.** The **2026 AHA/ASA guideline** makes **TNK 0.25 mg/kg** and
  **alteplase 0.9 mg/kg** co-equal Class 1 within 4.5 h; TNK's single bolus is operationally simpler,
  especially for transfers.
- **Late windows opened up.** **DAWN** and **DEFUSE-3** established 6–24 h thrombectomy with
  perfusion mismatch; **SELECT2/ANGEL-ASPECT** extended it to **large-core** infarcts; **ATTENTION/
  BAOCHE** to **basilar** occlusion. **WAKE-UP/EXTEND** support perfusion-guided late thrombolysis.
- **Short DAPT, not long.** For minor stroke / high-risk TIA, **21 days** of aspirin+clopidogrel
  (CHANCE/POINT) beats aspirin alone; beyond 21 days you only add bleeding. **THALES** offers
  ticagrelor+ASA.
- **Anticoagulate AF earlier than we used to.** **ELAN/OPTIMAS/TIMING** show early DOAC initiation is
  safe — the rigid 1-3-6-12 wait is out; individualize on infarct size.
- **Chase LDL <70** with a high-intensity statin (± ezetimibe/PCSK9); treat the **mechanism**, not
  just "a stroke."
