# Cervical artery dissection

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** a tear in the wall of the **carotid or vertebral artery** in the neck — the **leading
cause of stroke in the young and middle-aged.** The tell is **pain first, ischemia later**: neck/head
pain (± Horner's or pulsatile tinnitus) that **precedes a TIA or stroke by hours to days** — a window
where recognition prevents the stroke.

!!! danger "🚩 Young patient + neck/head pain + a local sign → image the vessels"
    Suspect dissection with:

    - **New neck or head/face pain** (often unilateral) after **trauma — even trivial** (MVC, sports,
      neck manipulation), heavy lifting, or spontaneously.
    - **Local signs before the stroke:** **partial Horner's** (ptosis + miosis, *carotid*), **pulsatile
      tinnitus**, a lower-cranial-nerve palsy, or **posterior neck pain** (*vertebral*).
    - Then **ischemia** — TIA/stroke in the corresponding territory, classically **hours to days later.**
    - **Connective-tissue clues:** fibromuscular dysplasia, Ehlers-Danlos, Marfan, family history.

    **Get dedicated vessel imaging now** — you're trying to catch it in the pain-before-stroke window.

## Diagnose

!!! orderset "Imaging — find the intramural hematoma / flap"
    - **CTA neck+head** or **MRI/MRA with fat-saturation** — look for the **crescent sign**
      (intramural hematoma), tapered ("flame") occlusion, or a pseudoaneurysm. Fat-sat MRI is
      especially good for the mural hematoma.
    - **DSA** is the historical gold standard — reserve for ambiguous cases or planned intervention.
    - **Screen for a connective-tissue disorder** if multivessel, recurrent, or young/atypical.

## The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Suspect it** in any young/middle-aged stroke or unexplained neck pain + local sign.
2. **Confirm** on CTA or fat-sat MRI/MRA.
3. **Antithrombotic — antiplatelet or anticoagulation** (they're equivalent; below), for **3–6 months.**
4. **Acute stroke from dissection?** Standard reperfusion still applies (thrombolysis/thrombectomy).
5. **Re-image at ~3–6 months**; stop or continue based on healing and residual risk.
6. **Counsel** on trauma/manipulation avoidance; screen for connective-tissue disease.

## Treatment

!!! orderset "Antithrombotic — antiplatelet vs anticoagulation is a wash; most get antiplatelet"
    - **Either antiplatelet OR anticoagulation is acceptable** — the two RCTs found **no significant
      difference** in stroke prevention: **CADISS** (recurrent stroke very low, no difference) and
      **TREAT-CAD** (aspirin did not formally prove non-inferior to a VKA, but event rates were low).
    - **Default to an antiplatelet** (aspirin) for most — simpler and safer. **Consider anticoagulation**
      (heparin→DOAC/warfarin) for higher-risk features: a **free-floating thrombus, near-occlusion,
      or recurrent ischemia** on an antiplatelet.
    - **Duration ~3–6 months**, then **re-image** and reassess — most dissections **heal/recanalize**;
      stop antithrombotics or step to long-term single antiplatelet based on the vessel and risk factors.
    - **Intracranial dissection** (higher SAH risk) is managed differently — individualize with
      neurology/neurosurgery.

!!! orderset "If it presents as an acute stroke — reperfuse normally"
    - **Extracranial dissection is NOT a contraindication to IV thrombolysis** — treat an acute
      ischemic stroke by the usual [reperfusion algorithm](index.md).
    - **Thrombectomy** for a large-vessel occlusion from the dissection.
    - **Endovascular stenting** is reserved for **refractory ischemia despite medical therapy** or an
      **enlarging/symptomatic pseudoaneurysm.**

## Disposition

Admit acute presentations (stroke unit; monitor for extension/ischemia). Prognosis is generally
**good** — recurrence is low and most heal. Outpatient neurology follow-up with **repeat vessel
imaging** and connective-tissue evaluation where indicated.

!!! military "Military medicine — cervical artery dissection"
    *Framework — verify against current **AR 40-501**, **DoDI 6130.03**, and aeromedical policy.*

    - **Fits the force:** young service members, **trauma/blast, load-bearing, combatives, and neck
      manipulation** are all mechanisms — keep dissection high on the differential for a service member
      with neck/head pain and any focal sign.
    - **Retention:** a dissection that causes **stroke/TIA** is a cerebrovascular condition under
      **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2 para 5.26.b** (stroke b(3); recurrent TIA b(6) *unless etiology identified
      and definitively treated*), Army **AR 40-501 3-31f** — a healed dissection with a treated cause
      and no residual deficit may retain per specialty review; residual deficit or recurrence trends to
      the **DES.**
    - **Deployability / aeromedical:** non-deployable during work-up/treatment; anticoagulation and a
      stroke history are **deployment- and flight-status-limiting/waiver-dependent.**

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **The #1 cause of stroke in the young** — and the pain **precedes** the stroke, so recognition in
  the neck-pain window is prevention.
- **Diagnose with fat-sat MRI/MRA or CTA** (crescent sign / intramural hematoma); DSA only if
  ambiguous.
- **Antiplatelet ≈ anticoagulation** — **CADISS** and **TREAT-CAD** show no clear winner; **default to
  an antiplatelet**, reserve anticoagulation for high-risk features, treat ~3–6 months, then re-image.
- **Don't withhold thrombolysis/thrombectomy** for an acute stroke from an extracranial dissection.
- **Prognosis is good** — most recanalize; recurrence is low. Screen for connective-tissue disease in
  the atypical/recurrent case.

**Key sources:** CADISS (Lancet Neurol 2015); TREAT-CAD (Lancet Neurol 2021); dissection
meta-analyses; AHA/ASA stroke-in-the-young guidance.
