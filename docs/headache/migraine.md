# Migraine

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** recurrent, disabling headache — usually lateralized, throbbing, moderate-to-severe,
worse with activity, with photophobia/phonophobia, nausea, and sometimes aura. For a general
provider: if a headache patient reaches neurology with a normal exam and no red flags, treat as
migraine — it is almost never "just tension."

!!! danger "🚩 Before you call it migraine"
    Clear the [headache red flags](index.md) first. Thunderclap onset, fever/neck stiffness,
    focal deficit, papilledema, new headache after 50, new headache in pregnancy, or a
    **change in an established pattern** all mean *work it up as a secondary headache first.*

## Rapid approach

1. **Exclude secondary headache** (red-flag screen). Normal exam + no red flags → proceed.
2. **Diagnose by phenotype** (ICHD-3, below). Imaging is **not** required for typical migraine
   with a normal exam.
3. **Treat the attack** — stratified care: match drug strength to attack severity, treat early.
4. **Decide on prevention** — if attacks are frequent or disabling, or acute meds are overused.
5. **Screen for and unwind medication overuse.**
6. **Disposition:** almost always outpatient. Avoid admitting known migraineurs.

## Diagnosis (ICHD-3, summarized)

**Migraine without aura:** ≥5 attacks lasting 4–72 h with **≥2 of** {unilateral, pulsating,
moderate/severe, aggravated by routine activity} **and ≥1 of** {nausea/vomiting, *or* photophobia
**and** phonophobia}, not better explained by another diagnosis.

**Migraine with aura:** ≥2 attacks with fully reversible aura (visual, sensory, speech, motor,
brainstem, or retinal) spreading over ≥5 min and/or in succession, each symptom 5–60 min, usually
followed by headache. **Motor aura = hemiplegic migraine; brainstem aura** — both are red flags to
distinguish from TIA/stroke on first presentation and both **contraindicate triptans/ergots**.

**Chronic migraine:** headache ≥15 days/month for >3 months, with migrainous features ≥8 days/month.
Screen for **medication overuse** in every chronic patient.

## Focused H&P + exam

- **Attack profile:** frequency (headache days/month), duration, disability (MIDAS/HIT-6 if outpatient),
  aura, triggers, and — critically — **how many days/month they take acute medication** (MOH screen).
- **Exam:** vitals with BP, **fundoscopy**, cranial nerves, visual fields, focal screen, gait. Normal
  between attacks. Any fixed deficit → re-open the secondary work-up.
- **Diary:** have the patient keep a headache diary; it drives both prevention decisions and
  (for service members) profile and disability documentation.

## Workup

Typical migraine with a normal exam needs **no imaging or labs**. Image (MRI preferred) only for
red flags, atypical/persistent aura, or a change in pattern. Do not order EEG for migraine.

## Acute (abortive) treatment

Principles: **treat early**, match agent to severity (stratified care), pre-treat nausea, and
**cap acute-medication days to reduce MOH risk** (see below).

=== "First-line"
    - **NSAIDs:** naproxen 500 mg, ibuprofen 400–600 mg, or **ketorolac** (IM/IV) for severe/ER.
    - **Triptans:** sumatriptan 50–100 mg PO / 6 mg SC / 10–20 mg nasal; rizatriptan 10 mg ODT;
      others. Take at onset; may repeat once after ≥2 h; **max ~2 treatment days/week**.
    - **Combination:** a **triptan + naproxen** together aborts more attacks than either alone —
      a reasonable default for moderate/severe attacks.
    - **Antiemetic adjunct:** metoclopramide or prochlorperazine improves the headache itself,
      not just the nausea.

=== "If triptans fail / are contraindicated"
    Triptans and ergots are **contraindicated** in coronary/cerebrovascular disease, uncontrolled
    hypertension, and **hemiplegic or brainstem-aura migraine**. Alternatives that do **not**
    cause vasoconstriction:

    - **Gepants (CGRP receptor antagonists), acute:** ubrogepant 50–100 mg, rimegepant 75 mg ODT,
      or **zavegepant 10 mg nasal**. Safe in vascular disease; **do not cause MOH**.
    - **Ditan:** lasmiditan 50–200 mg — no vasoconstriction, but sedating with a **driving
      restriction (≥8 h)**; watch serotonergic combinations.
    - **DHE** (nasal/SC/IV) for prolonged or refractory attacks (see status protocol).

=== "Avoid"
    - **Opioids and butalbital-containing combinations** — poor efficacy, high MOH and dependence
      risk. Do not start them; work to taper patients already on them.

## Preventive treatment

**Start prevention** (AHS consensus) for **≥4 migraine headache days/month**, or **≥2 days/month
with significant disability** despite acute treatment — and regardless of frequency for **atypical
aura** (hemiplegic, brainstem) or migraine **complications** (persistent aura, migrainous infarction,
status migrainosus). Also start when acute treatment is overused/contraindicated, or by patient
preference. **Set expectations:** give any preventive an adequate trial (**8–12 weeks at target
dose**), track response with the diary, and taper when stable.

=== "Oral first-line (well-established, low cost)"
    Pick by comorbidity and side-effect fit — let the patient help choose:

    - **Beta-blockers:** propranolol, metoprolol, timolol.
    - **Topiramate:** titrate 25 mg/week toward 100 mg/day. *(Weight loss, paresthesias, altered
      taste of carbonated drinks, cognitive slowing, kidney stones; teratogenic.)*
    - **Amitriptyline / nortriptyline** — helpful with insomnia or comorbid tension-type/anxiety.
    - **Candesartan** or **valproate/divalproex** (avoid valproate in people who may become pregnant).

=== "CGRP-targeted (a first-line option — AHS 2024)"
    - **Anti-CGRP monoclonal antibodies:** erenumab (receptor), fremanezumab, galcanezumab (SC,
      monthly/quarterly), eptinezumab (IV, quarterly). Well tolerated; watch constipation
      (erenumab) and injection-site reactions.
    - **Oral gepants for prevention:** **atogepant** (daily) or **rimegepant** (every other day) —
      rimegepant uniquely doubles as acute + preventive.
    - **Per the 2024 American Headache Society position statement, CGRP-targeting therapies are now
      a first-line option** — there is **no requirement to fail two trials of older oral preventives
      first.** Choose between an oral first-line agent and a CGRP therapy based on the patient,
      comorbidities, contraindications, and access (see Discover).

=== "Chronic migraine"
    - **OnabotulinumtoxinA (PREEMPT protocol):** **155 units across 31 fixed sites every 12 weeks**
      (up to **195 units/39 sites** with the optional "follow-the-pain" additions) — **for chronic
      migraine only** (≥15 headache days/month), not episodic.
    - CGRP mAbs and Botox are both first-tier for chronic migraine and can be combined in
      refractory cases.

=== "Non-drug"
    - Regular sleep, meals, hydration, aerobic exercise, trigger awareness, limiting caffeine,
      and treating comorbid mood/sleep disorders.
    - **Neuromodulation devices** (e.g., e-TNS, remote electrical neuromodulation, sTMS,
      non-invasive vagus nerve stimulation) — useful when drugs are contraindicated or unwanted.

## Order set — status migrainosus / ER

!!! orderset "ER protocol for the refractory/prolonged attack"
    After red flags are excluded. Give in stepwise phases; **avoid opioids/butalbital**.

    **Phase 1 (give together):**

    - IV fluids — normal saline bolus (e.g., 1 L)
    - **Metoclopramide 10 mg IV** *or* **prochlorperazine 10 mg IV** — **plus diphenhydramine
      25 mg IV** to prevent akathisia/dystonia
    - **Ketorolac 30 mg IV**
    - **Magnesium sulfate 1–2 g IV** (especially migraine with aura)

    **Phase 2 (if inadequate):**

    - **Valproate (e.g., 500–1000 mg IV)** *or* **DHE 0.5–1 mg IV** (pre-treat nausea; DHE
      contraindicated in vascular disease, recent triptan, pregnancy)

    **Before discharge:**

    - **Dexamethasone 10 mg IV/IM once** — reduces early headache recurrence
    - Speak to the nurse directly to sequence meds; if admitted, add scheduled antiemetic,
      sleep hygiene, and a clear acute-med cap.

## Medication overuse headache (MOH)

Suspect when headaches are frequent **and** acute meds are used often — roughly **≥10 days/month**
for triptans/ergots/opioids/combination analgesics, or **≥15 days/month** for simple NSAIDs/
acetaminophen. Management: **start a preventive**, **cap or withdraw the overused agent**, bridge
symptoms (e.g., NSAID/antiemetic course ± short steroid taper), and counsel. **Gepants do not cause
MOH** and are useful acute agents in this setting.

## Disposition

Migraine is an **outpatient diagnosis.** Treat the attack, start/adjust prevention, arrange
follow-up, and give a written acute-med limit. Admission is rarely helpful for a known migraineur.

!!! military "Military medicine — migraine"
    *Standards below are quoted from **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1 (accession) / Vol 2 (retention)** and
    **AR 40-501 (27 June 2019)**; the DES process is governed by **DoDI 1332.18 / DoDM 1332.18**.
    Confirm current editions and service-specific aeromedical policy before you counsel or document.*

    - **Deployability:** most controlled migraine is deployable. **Frequent/incapacitating** attacks,
      or a regimen not maintainable in theater, can be deployment-limiting. Consider environment:
      **topiramate** (heat intolerance, cognitive effects), sedating TCAs, and **CGRP mAbs**
      (cold-chain/refrigeration and resupply) all matter downrange.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary profile during titration or a flare; **permanent
      profile** for chronic/refractory disease with functional limits documented.
    - **EPTS / LOD:** migraine often predates service — document **onset and pre-service history**;
      it affects existed-prior-to-service and line-of-duty determinations.
    - **Retention — DoD & Army:** the joint standard (**DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2, para 5.26.g**) places
      migraine, tension, **or** cluster headaches with **frequent incapacitating attacks** below
      retention standards. **AR 40-501 para 3-31g** operationalizes "frequent" for the Army as
      **incapacitating attacks interfering with duty or social activities ≥3 days/month** →
      **mandatory neurologist referral**, up to **6 months** of prophylaxis, and **referral to the
      DES** (MEB/PEB) if not adequately controlled at 6 months (or directly, if unlikely to respond).
    - **Accession — DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1, para 6.26.e:** for *applicants*, a history in the prior
      24 months of headaches that caused missed activity >2×/12 months, required prescription meds
      >2×/12 months, or needed prophylaxis is **disqualifying for entry** (relevant to recruiting/
      waivers).
    - **Document what the standard turns on:** a **headache diary** showing ≥3 incapacitating
      days/month, the **neurology referral**, the prophylaxis trial and its outcome, and concrete
      **duty impact**.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** a **history of chronic, recurrent, or incapacitating headaches
      — including ocular or acephalic migraine with visual disturbance — is disqualifying for flying
      duty** (AR 40-501, ch. 4). Many migraine medications are independently disqualifying/waiver-
      dependent; confirm current aeromedical policy before prescribing.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

*Written from primary guidelines and trials; source articles are a reading list, not copied.*

**What's changed and what matters now:**

- **Stratified beats step care.** Match acute-drug strength to attack severity from the start;
  a **triptan + NSAID** combination outperforms either agent alone for moderate/severe attacks.
- **CGRP therapies are now first-line for prevention.** The **2024 American Headache Society
  position statement** (Charles et al., *Headache*) makes anti-CGRP mAbs (erenumab, fremanezumab,
  galcanezumab, eptinezumab) and oral gepants (atogepant, rimegepant) **a first-line option** and
  **removed the prior requirement to fail two 8-week trials of older oral preventives first.** They
  are "a first-line option," not "the" only first-line — choose between an oral agent and a CGRP
  therapy by patient factors and access.
- **Gepants and ditans expand acute options.** Gepants (ubrogepant, rimegepant, zavegepant) and the
  ditan lasmiditan work **without vasoconstriction**, making them options in cardiovascular/
  cerebrovascular disease where triptans are contraindicated. **Gepants do not cause MOH.**
- **OnabotulinumtoxinA (PREEMPT)** remains a first-tier preventive for **chronic** migraine only.
- **Avoid opioids and butalbital** — they worsen the disease and drive medication-overuse headache.
- **Prevention only works if trialed adequately** — 8–12 weeks at target dose before judging failure.

**Key sources to cite (verify current versions):**

- ICHD-3 diagnostic criteria (International Headache Society).
- American Headache Society consensus/position statements on integrating CGRP-targeted therapies
  and on the initiation of preventive treatment.
- PREEMPT trials — onabotulinumtoxinA for chronic migraine.
- Pivotal trials of anti-CGRP mAbs and gepants (e.g., STRIVE/ARISE, and the ubrogepant/rimegepant/
  atogepant/lasmiditan registration trials).
