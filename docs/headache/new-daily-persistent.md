# New daily persistent headache

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** a headache that is **daily and unremitting from the moment of onset** — the patient
can often name the **exact day** it started — persisting **> 3 months**, in someone **without a
prior significant headache history**. It is a **diagnosis of exclusion**, and the exclusion work is
the whole game.

!!! danger "🚩 NDPH is where secondary headaches hide — work it up thoroughly"
    A distinct-onset daily headache demands an active search for a secondary cause before you accept
    NDPH:

    | Consider | Screen with |
    |---|---|
    | **CSF pressure disorders** (low: leak/SIH; high: IIH) | MRI brain ± spine, **LP with opening pressure**, fundoscopy |
    | **Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis** | MRV / CTV |
    | **Giant cell arteritis** (older patients) | ESR/CRP |
    | Cervicogenic / craniocervical | Cervical exam, imaging |
    | Infectious / post-infectious (incl. post-viral) | History, targeted labs/LP |
    | Sphenoid sinusitis, intracranial mass | MRI |

## Rapid approach

1. **Confirm the ICHD-3 profile:** daily and continuous **within 24 hours of onset**, present
   **> 3 months**, distinct remembered start, no prior chronic headache.
2. **Exclude secondary causes** (table above) — **MRI/MRV + LP with opening pressure** are the
   high-yield tests.
3. **Classify the phenotype** (migraine-like vs tension-type-like) once secondary causes are out.
4. **Treat by phenotype** and **set realistic expectations** — NDPH is often refractory.

## Diagnosis (ICHD-3)

Persistent headache, **daily from onset (or within 24 h)**, present **> 3 months**, not better
accounted for by another diagnosis. Many patients report a **trigger around onset** — an infection
(classically viral), a stressful life event, or surgery.

## Workup

The most thorough of the primary-headache work-ups: **MRI brain (± spine), MRV/CTV, LP with
opening pressure**, and **ESR/CRP** in older patients. Normal studies are what allow the diagnosis
to stand.

## Management

- **Treat the phenotype:** migraine-like NDPH → the [migraine](migraine.md) preventive/acute
  ladder; tension-type-like → amitriptyline and non-drug measures.
- **Address perpetuators:** sleep, mood, medication overuse (screen every visit).
- **Set expectations:** NDPH is notoriously treatment-resistant; some post-infectious cases remit,
  others persist — honesty up front helps.
- **Avoid opioids/butalbital.**

## Disposition

Outpatient, with a completed secondary work-up documented and phenotype-directed treatment plus
follow-up. Consider a headache-specialist referral for refractory cases.

      NDPH is a diagnosis of exclusion and any board will scrutinize the work-up.
    - **Deployability:** a **daily unremitting** headache is frequently deployment-limiting while
      active and being characterized.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary during work-up/treatment trials; permanent if it
      persists with functional limitation.
    - **EPTS / LOD:** the distinct onset date helps establish timing relative to service.
    - **Retention / MEB:** refractory, disabling NDPH after an adequate work-up and treatment trials
      may warrant board referral; document frequency, failed trials, and duty impact.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** an active daily headache and its medications are generally
      **disqualifying/waiver-dependent**.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **NDPH is defined by its onset pattern, not its character** — daily and continuous from a
  remembered starting point.
- **It is fundamentally a diagnosis of exclusion:** the priority is ruling out **CSF-pressure
  disorders, CVST, and GCA** with MRI/MRV, LP with opening pressure, and inflammatory markers.
- **Treatment is phenotype-directed and often refractory;** some post-infectious cases remit.
- **Set expectations early** and avoid opioids/butalbital.
