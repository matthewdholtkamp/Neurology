# Cluster headache & the trigeminal autonomic cephalalgias

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** the TACs are **severe, strictly unilateral** headaches with **ipsilateral cranial
autonomic features** (tearing, conjunctival injection, ptosis/miosis, rhinorrhea, eyelid edema).
They are separated from one another — and from migraine — mainly by **attack duration, frequency,
and treatment response.** Get these right and the treatment falls out of the diagnosis.

!!! danger "🚩 Red flags — image every new TAC at least once"
    Despite the classic teaching that self-resolving attacks are benign, a **new TAC deserves an
    MRI brain** (with attention to the **pituitary and cavernous sinus**) to exclude a secondary
    mimic — pituitary lesions and posterior-fossa pathology can reproduce a TAC phenotype.
    Image urgently for: **fixed Horner's/other cranial-nerve deficit, abnormal exam, side-locked
    but atypical features, or thunderclap onset.**

## Diagnose by the clock (ICHD-3)

| Disorder | Attack length | Frequency | Signature | Treatment key |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cluster headache** | 15–180 min | 1 every other day → 8/day | Agitated/restless; circadian & seasonal bouts | O₂ + SC/nasal triptan; **verapamil** prevention |
| **Paroxysmal hemicrania** | 2–30 min | >5/day (>half the time) | Female-predominant | **Absolute response to indomethacin** |
| **SUNCT / SUNA** | 1–600 sec | ≥1/day, often many | Cutaneous triggers (touch, chewing) | Lamotrigine; IV lidocaine for crisis |
| **Hemicrania continua** | continuous | continuous + exacerbations | Background pain with autonomic flares | **Absolute response to indomethacin** |

*If a strictly unilateral headache with autonomic features responds completely to indomethacin,
it is paroxysmal hemicrania or hemicrania continua — a diagnosis you don't want to miss because it
is so treatable (the "indomethacin test").*

## Rapid approach

1. Confirm strictly **unilateral** pain with **ipsilateral autonomic** features and a **normal
   interictal exam**.
2. **Image once** (MRI, pituitary views) to exclude a secondary cause.
3. **Time the attacks** → place them in the table above.
4. **Cluster:** at the *same visit*, start **all three** — an **abortive** (O₂ + SC/nasal triptan),
   a **transitional bridge**, and a **preventive**. Clusters are too disabling to titrate slowly.
5. **Short-lasting + autonomic:** run the **indomethacin trial** before anything else.

## Cluster headache — the prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Confirm + image once** (MRI with pituitary views).
2. **Abortive:** high-flow **oxygen** + **sumatriptan 6 mg SC** (or nasal triptan) — script both,
   including a home oxygen setup.
3. **Bridge:** a **prednisone taper** *or* a **greater occipital nerve block** to cover the gap
   until the preventive works.
4. **Preventive:** **verapamil** (with the ECG protocol) — start the same day.
5. **Episodic cluster not controlled on verapamil:** add **galcanezumab 300 mg SC monthly**.
6. **Refractory / chronic:** neurology referral; lithium, topiramate, or combinations;
   occipital-nerve stimulation in specialist hands.

### Acute (abortive) treatment

!!! orderset "Step 1 — abort the attack (give oxygen AND a triptan; they're complementary)"
    Both are **Level A**. Oral drugs are useless — attacks peak in minutes.

    - **High-flow oxygen: 100% at 12–15 L/min via a true non-rebreather mask, 15–20 min, sitting
      forward.** NNT ≈ 2. *The commonest "oxygen failure" is a setup failure* — a standard nasal
      cannula or a mask without a reservoir bag won't work. Insist on a **non-rebreather** (or a
      demand valve where available).
    - **Sumatriptan 6 mg SC** — fastest and most effective (relief in ~15 min). Max 2 injections/24 h.
    - **Zolmitriptan 5–10 mg nasal** or **sumatriptan 20 mg nasal** — when SC isn't available or is
      declined (10 mg zolmitriptan NNT ≈ 2–3).
    - **Non-invasive vagus nerve stimulation (gammaCore)** — evidence-based **adjunct/abortive for
      episodic** cluster; useful when triptans are contraindicated or overused.

    !!! warning "Do not use for acute cluster"
        **Oral triptans, opioids, and simple analgesics** — all too slow for an attack that peaks in
        minutes. Opioids additionally worsen the course.

### Transitional bridge (start with the preventive, same visit)

!!! orderset "Step 2 — bridge the gap while the preventive ramps up"
    A preventive takes 1–2+ weeks to work; a bridge suppresses attacks now. Pick one:

    - **Greater occipital nerve block (GON block)** — suboccipital corticosteroid ± local anesthetic
      on the symptomatic side; **RCT-supported** and fast. First choice if you can do it.
    - **Prednisone taper** — e.g., **60–100 mg/day × 5 days, then taper over ~2–3 weeks.** Widely
      used and effective as a bridge; not for chronic maintenance (steroid toxicity).

### Prevention

!!! orderset "Step 3 — verapamil first-line, with a mandatory ECG protocol"
    - **Verapamil** — start **240 mg/day**, titrate by ~80 mg every ~1–2 weeks to effect (commonly
      **360–480 mg/day**; specialists go higher). Use it for the whole bout.
    - **ECG protocol (non-negotiable):** **baseline ECG**, then **before each dose increase** and
      **~10–14 days after**, watching for **PR prolongation, heart block, and bradycardia**. Verapamil
      conduction effects are **dose-dependent and can appear late**.
    - **Constipation** is the dose-limiting nuisance — start a bowel regimen.

    === "If verapamil is inadequate or contraindicated"
        - **Galcanezumab (Emgality) 300 mg SC monthly** — FDA-approved for **episodic** cluster
          (note: **300 mg**, not the 120 mg migraine dose; the **chronic**-cluster trial was
          negative). Give at the start of the bout and continue through it.
        - **Lithium** — effective (esp. chronic cluster); needs **level, renal, and thyroid
          monitoring**.
        - **Topiramate** — alternative/add-on.
        - **Melatonin 10 mg qHS** — reasonable adjunct, exploits the circadian biology.

    !!! info "Clusters remit — plan for it"
        In **episodic** cluster, stop the preventive once the bout ends and the patient is
        attack-free for ~2 weeks; **restart at the first sign of the next bout.** In **chronic**
        cluster, maintain continuously.

### Paroxysmal hemicrania · hemicrania continua · SUNCT/SUNA

!!! orderset "Short-lasting/continuous TACs — run the indomethacin trial first"
    - **Paroxysmal hemicrania & hemicrania continua → indomethacin trial.** Uptitrate
      **25 mg TID → 50 mg TID (up to 75 mg TID / ~225 mg/day)**, each step held a few days, **with GI
      protection (PPI)**. A **complete** response confirms the diagnosis and is the treatment; taper
      to the lowest effective dose. Consider **"indotest"** (IM indomethacin 50–100 mg) where the oral
      trial is ambiguous.
    - **SUNCT / SUNA** — do **not** respond to indomethacin. **Lamotrigine** (titrate slowly for
      rash), then **gabapentin, topiramate, or oxcarbazepine**. **IV lidocaine** (monitored) for a
      status-like crisis; occipital-nerve or SPG procedures in refractory cases.

## Order set — quick reference

!!! orderset "Acute cluster attack (copy-paste)"
    - **Oxygen 100% at 12–15 L/min via non-rebreather × 15–20 min, sitting forward**
    - **Sumatriptan 6 mg SC** (or zolmitriptan 5–10 mg nasal if SC unavailable)
    - Before discharge: start a **GON block or prednisone bridge** + **verapamil (with baseline ECG)**,
      script **home oxygen**, and arrange **neurology follow-up**.

!!! tip "TRICARE — cluster prescribing"
    - **Verapamil, prednisone, lithium, topiramate** are **generic/formulary, no PA.**
    - **Galcanezumab (Emgality)** is the **preferred, no-PA CGRP** on TRICARE and carries the
      **episodic-cluster** indication — the frictionless CGRP choice here too (dose **300 mg** for
      cluster).
    - **Home oxygen** requires a prescription documenting the **cluster diagnosis and flow rate
      (12–15 L/min, non-rebreather)**; arrange the setup explicitly — coverage/logistics are the
      usual failure point, not efficacy. *Verify current DME coverage with the regional contractor.*

## Disposition

Outpatient. The wins are diagnostic (don't miss an indomethacin-responsive TAC) and logistical
(get the patient home oxygen and a verapamil-plus-ECG plan started the same day).

!!! military "Military medicine — cluster & TACs"
    *Standards below are quoted from **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1/Vol 2** and **AR 40-501 (27 June 2019)**;
    the DES process is governed by **DoDI 1332.18 / DoDM 1332.18**. Confirm current editions and
    aeromedical policy.*

    - **Deployability:** an **active cluster bout** is severely disabling and typically
      deployment-limiting — the regimen (home/therapeutic **oxygen dependence**, **verapamil with
      serial ECGs**, injectable triptans, refrigerated CGRP) is hard to sustain in theater. Between
      bouts, many are functional.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary profile during a bout; permanent profile for **chronic
      cluster** or refractory TACs with duty impact.
    - **EPTS / LOD:** document onset and periodicity.
    - **Retention — DoD & Army:** both the joint standard (**DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2, para 5.26.g**) and
      **AR 40-501 para 3-31g** explicitly name "migraine, tension, **or cluster** headaches" —
      **frequent incapacitating attacks** (Army: interfering with duty/social activities ≥3 days/
      month) require **neurologist referral**, permit up to **6 months** of prophylaxis, and mandate
      **referral to the DES** (MEB/PEB) if not controlled at 6 months. A cluster bout easily meets
      the frequency threshold.
    - **Accession — DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1, para 6.26.g:** a **history of cluster headaches** is
      **disqualifying for entry**.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** active cluster and its medications (verapamil, triptans,
      lithium, topiramate) are generally **disqualifying/waiver-dependent**, and a history of
      chronic/recurrent/incapacitating headache is disqualifying for flying duty (AR 40-501, ch. 4).

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **The indomethacin-responsive headaches (PH, HC) are the ones you must not miss** — a complete
  response is both treatment and diagnosis.
- **Cluster acute therapy = oxygen + subcutaneous/nasal triptan** (both **Level A**); oral agents
  are too slow. Oxygen fails most often for **setup** reasons — insist on a non-rebreather.
- **Verapamil is first-line prevention** and mandates **ECG surveillance** for dose-dependent
  conduction block. **Galcanezumab 300 mg monthly** is FDA-approved for **episodic** cluster (the
  chronic-cluster trial was negative).
- **Transition with a GON block or a short prednisone taper** while the preventive ramps up.
- **Non-invasive vagus nerve stimulation** has a role as an adjunct/abortive in episodic cluster.
- **Always image a new TAC once** to exclude pituitary/structural mimics.

cluster-headache guideline; the galcanezumab episodic-cluster trial; nVNS cluster trials.
