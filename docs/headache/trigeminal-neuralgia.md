# Trigeminal neuralgia

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** paroxysmal, **electric-shock** facial pain in one or more trigeminal divisions
(usually **V2/V3**), triggered by innocuous stimuli — light touch, chewing, talking, brushing
teeth, cold air. Volleys of stabbing pain with pain-free intervals. **Multiple sclerosis can
present with trigeminal neuralgia — always keep it on the table.**

!!! danger "🚩 Red flags for a *secondary* cause — image everyone"
    Features that point away from classical TN and toward a structural or demyelinating cause:

    - **Sensory loss** or other cranial-nerve deficit on exam
    - **Bilateral** symptoms
    - **Age < 40** at onset
    - Poor response to sodium-channel-blocking drugs

    Any of these — and honestly, all first presentations — warrant **MRI** to look for
    **neurovascular compression, a tumor, or an MS plaque** at the root entry zone.

## Diagnose & classify (ICHD-3)

- **Phenotype:** brief (a fraction of a second to 2 min), **shock-like/stabbing**, triggered,
  division-specific pain with **normal sensation between attacks**. A "concomitant continuous pain"
  variant exists and predicts a harder course.
- **Classify:** *classical* (proven neurovascular compression **with morphological change** — atrophy
  or displacement of the nerve), *secondary* (MS, tumor, AV malformation), or *idiopathic* (no cause
  found).

## Rapid approach

1. **Confirm the phenotype** (above) with a normal interictal exam.
2. **MRI brain** — thin cuts through the **pontine cistern / course of CN V** for **vascular
   compression, demyelination (MS), or tumor**.
3. **Start a sodium-channel blocker** (Step 1) — titrate to pain freedom.
4. **Refer for surgery early** if pain isn't controlled or the drug isn't tolerated — don't grind
   through five drugs first.

## The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Confirm phenotype + MRI** (exclude MS/tumor, characterize neurovascular compression).
2. **Start carbamazepine or oxcarbazepine**, titrate to effect (Step 1). **Screen HLA-B\*15:02**
   before carbamazepine in at-risk ancestry.
3. **Inadequate or not tolerated?** Add or switch to a **second-line agent** (Step 2) — lamotrigine,
   gabapentin/pregabalin, baclofen, or botulinum toxin A.
4. **Acute pain crisis (can't eat/drink/take pills):** admit for **IV fosphenytoin or lidocaine** +
   rehydration (crisis order set).
5. **Refractory or poorly tolerated on adequate medical therapy:** **refer for surgery** —
   **microvascular decompression is first-line surgery** for classical TN with neurovascular
   compression; ablative procedures for poor surgical candidates.
6. **MS plaque on MRI:** pivot to the demyelinating work-up — the TN is a symptom of the larger
   diagnosis.

## Workup

**MRI is mandatory** — it both excludes secondary causes (especially **MS** and posterior-fossa
tumors) and characterizes neurovascular compression for surgical planning. Baseline **CBC, sodium,
LFTs** before starting carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine; **HLA-B\*15:02** genotyping before carbamazepine
in patients of **Han Chinese, Thai, or other Southeast Asian ancestry** (SJS/TEN risk).

## Management

!!! orderset "Step 1 — first-line: a sodium-channel blocker (start here for everyone)"
    Both are first-line (EAN 2019). Start low, titrate to **pain freedom**, then hold the lowest
    effective dose.

    - **Carbamazepine — start 100–200 mg BID**, titrate by ~100–200 mg every few days; usual
      effective **600–800 mg/day** (range **200–1200 mg/day**), divided. Most effective agent; least
      well tolerated. **Monitor CBC, sodium (hyponatremia), LFTs**; it has an ECG effect and **major
      CYP-inducing drug interactions**. **Screen HLA-B\*15:02** first in at-risk ancestry.
    - **Oxcarbazepine — start 300 mg BID**, titrate to **600–1800 mg/day** divided. **Better
      tolerated with comparable efficacy** and fewer interactions — a reasonable default — but
      **hyponatremia is more common** (check sodium).

    → Pain not controlled, or intolerable side effects → **Step 2** (and start thinking about
    surgical referral in parallel).

!!! orderset "Step 2 — add-on / second-line (alone or added to Step 1)"
    Per EAN 2019, any of these may be used **alone or as add-on** when first-line fails or isn't
    tolerated:

    - **Lamotrigine** — titrate **slowly** (25 mg, up over weeks to ~200–400 mg/day) for **rash/SJS**
      risk; good add-on evidence.
    - **Gabapentin / pregabalin** — well tolerated; useful with the continuous-pain variant.
    - **Baclofen** — **5–10 mg TID up to ~60–80 mg/day**; classic add-on to carbamazepine (taper on
      discontinuation).
    - **Botulinum toxin type A** — subcutaneous/submucosal injection; an evidence-based option for
      refractory TN, useful in poor surgical candidates.
    - *(Phenytoin and lacosamide are further sodium-channel options.)*

!!! orderset "Acute TN crisis — can't eat, drink, or keep pills down (admit)"
    A severe exacerbation is a genuine emergency (dehydration, weight loss, unable to take oral meds).

    - **Admit** for rehydration and IV therapy.
    - **IV fosphenytoin** — rescue loading (commonly ~**10–20 mg PE/kg**) reduces pain within ~2 h;
      cardiac monitoring.
    - **IV lidocaine** — ~**5 mg/kg over 1 h**, monitored — relief up to ~24 h; buys time to
      up-titrate an oral agent or arrange surgery.
    - Up-titrate/optimize the oral sodium-channel blocker in parallel; involve neurology/neurosurgery.

!!! orderset "Step 3 — refractory → procedural (refer early; don't over-medicate)"
    **Offer surgery when pain is not sufficiently controlled medically, or medication is poorly
    tolerated** — early referral beats stacking five drugs.

    - **Microvascular decompression (MVD)** — **first-line surgery** for **classical** TN with
      confirmed neurovascular compression; the most **durable** result (open posterior-fossa surgery).
    - **Ablative / less-invasive** (for poor surgical candidates or secondary TN): **stereotactic
      radiosurgery (Gamma Knife)**, **percutaneous rhizotomy** (radiofrequency, glycerol, or balloon
      compression). Lower invasiveness, but **higher recurrence and sensory-loss/anesthesia-dolorosa**
      trade-offs — counsel accordingly.

!!! tip "TRICARE — TN prescribing"
    **Carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, lamotrigine, gabapentin, pregabalin, and baclofen** are all
    **generic, on-formulary, no PA** — the entire medical ladder is low-friction. **Botulinum toxin
    type A for TN is off-label** and will need a **PA with specialist justification**. Surgical
    referral (MVD/radiosurgery) goes through normal specialty-care authorization.

## Disposition

Outpatient, unless the patient is in a **pain crisis** (admit — see order set). Escalate to
neurosurgery/ENT when medication fails or isn't tolerated. If the MRI shows an **MS plaque**, pivot
to the demyelinating work-up — the TN is a symptom of the larger diagnosis.

!!! military "Military medicine — trigeminal neuralgia"
    *Framework only — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 6130.03, DoDI 1332.18, and aeromedical
    policy.*

    - **The MS question dominates disposition.** If TN is **secondary to MS**, the MS drives
      retention, profile, and board decisions — work it up accordingly.
    - **Deployability:** limited during titration; sodium-channel blockers cause **sedation,
      cognitive slowing, hyponatremia**, and need lab monitoring — relevant to duty and austere
      settings. A pain crisis is non-deployable.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary during titration or crisis; permanent for refractory
      disease or post-procedural limitations.
    - **Retention / MEB:** refractory TN with duty impact, or TN secondary to a disqualifying
      condition (MS, tumor), warrants board referral. Document drug trials, imaging, and any surgery.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine and uncontrolled TN are generally
      **disqualifying/waiver-dependent**.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **Sodium-channel blockers (carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine) are first-line** (EAN 2019: CBZ
  200–1200 mg/day; OXC 300–1800 mg/day); **oxcarbazepine is usually better tolerated** with
  comparable efficacy but more hyponatremia.
- **Second-line/add-on:** lamotrigine, gabapentin, pregabalin, baclofen, **botulinum toxin A**,
  phenytoin — alone or as add-on.
- **MRI for everyone** — to catch **MS** and structural causes and to plan surgery.
- **Refer for surgery early** when medical therapy fails or isn't tolerated.
  **Microvascular decompression gives the most durable relief** in classical TN with neurovascular
  compression; radiosurgery and rhizotomy trade durability for lower invasiveness.
- **Acute crisis = admit** for IV fosphenytoin or lidocaine and rehydration.
- **Screen HLA-B\*15:02** before carbamazepine in Southeast Asian ancestry (SJS/TEN).

**Key sources:** ICHD-3 (TN criteria); EAN 2019 trigeminal neuralgia guideline; AAN/EFNS 2008
guideline; microvascular decompression outcome series.
