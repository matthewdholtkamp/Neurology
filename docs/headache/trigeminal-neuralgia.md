# Trigeminal neuralgia

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** paroxysmal, **electric-shock** facial pain in one or more trigeminal divisions
(usually **V2/V3**), triggered by innocuous stimuli — light touch, chewing, talking, brushing
teeth, cold air. Volleys of stabbing pain with pain-free intervals. **Multiple sclerosis can
present with trigeminal neuralgia — always keep it on the table.**

!!! danger "🚩 Red flags for a *secondary* cause — image everyone"
    Features that point away from classical TN and toward a structural or demyelinating cause:

    - **Sensory loss** or other cranial-nerve deficit on exam
    - **Bilateral** symptoms
    - **Age < 40** at onset
    - Poor response to sodium-channel-blocking drugs

    Any of these — and honestly, all first presentations — warrant **MRI** to look for
    **neurovascular compression, a tumor, or an MS plaque** at the root entry zone.

## Rapid approach

1. **Confirm the phenotype:** brief, shock-like, triggered, division-specific pain with normal
   sensation between attacks.
2. **MRI brain** — order thin cuts through the **pontine cistern / course of CN V** to assess for
   **vascular compression, demyelination (MS), or tumor**.
3. **Classify (ICHD-3):** *classical* (proven neurovascular compression with morphologic change),
   *secondary* (MS, tumor, AV malformation), or *idiopathic*.
4. **Start a sodium-channel blocker;** refer for a procedure if refractory.

## Workup

**MRI is mandatory** — it both excludes secondary causes (especially **MS** and posterior-fossa
tumors) and characterizes neurovascular compression for surgical planning. Routine bloods only as
needed for drug monitoring.

## Management

=== "First-line"
    - **Carbamazepine** or **oxcarbazepine** — the anchor drugs. Titrate to effect.
      Monitor: **CBC, sodium (hyponatremia), LFTs**; carbamazepine also needs ECG and has notable
      drug interactions (and HLA-B\*15:02 screening in at-risk ancestries for SJS/TEN risk).
    - Oxcarbazepine is often **better tolerated** with similar efficacy.

=== "Add-on / second-line"
    - **Lamotrigine** (slow titration), **baclofen**, **gabapentin/pregabalin** — as add-ons or
      when first-line is not tolerated.

=== "Refractory → procedural (refer NSGY/ENT)"
    - **Microvascular decompression** — most **durable** option for classical TN with confirmed
      neurovascular compression; open posterior-fossa surgery.
    - **Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife)** or **percutaneous rhizotomy** — less invasive,
      good for poor surgical candidates; higher recurrence/sensory-loss trade-offs.

## Disposition

Outpatient. Escalate to neurosurgery/ENT when medication fails or is not tolerated. If the MRI
shows an **MS plaque**, pivot to the demyelinating work-up — the TN is a symptom of the larger
diagnosis.

!!! military "Military medicine — trigeminal neuralgia"
    *Framework only — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 1332.18, and aeromedical policy.*

    - **The MS question dominates disposition.** If TN is **secondary to MS**, the MS drives
      retention, profile, and board decisions — work it up accordingly.
    - **Deployability:** limited during titration; sodium-channel blockers cause **sedation,
      cognitive slowing, hyponatremia**, and need lab monitoring — relevant to duty and austere
      settings.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary during titration; permanent for refractory disease or
      post-procedural limitations.
    - **Retention / MEB:** refractory TN with duty impact, or TN secondary to a disqualifying
      condition (MS, tumor), warrants board referral. Document trials and imaging.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** carbamazepine/oxcarbazepine and uncontrolled TN are generally
      **disqualifying/waiver-dependent**.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **Sodium-channel blockers (carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine) are first-line;** oxcarbazepine is
  usually **better tolerated** with comparable efficacy.
- **MRI for everyone** — to catch **MS** and structural causes and to plan surgery.
- **Microvascular decompression gives the most durable relief** in classical TN with neurovascular
  compression; radiosurgery and rhizotomy trade durability for lower invasiveness.
- **New TN under 40, bilateral, or with sensory loss** should raise suspicion for a secondary cause.
