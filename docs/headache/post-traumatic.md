# Post-traumatic headache

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** headache that develops in close temporal relation to head and/or neck trauma —
the most common symptom after concussion/mild TBI, and a signature problem in the military
population (blast, vehicle, fall, and blunt mechanisms). Most persistent post-traumatic
headache (PTH) is **phenotypically migraine or tension-type**, so you treat the phenotype
while managing the comorbidities that keep it going.

!!! danger "🚩 Red flags — this is a headache *after trauma*, so exclude structure first"
    | Pattern | Worry | Do now |
    |---|---|---|
    | Worsening/persistent severe headache, vomiting, ↓ or fluctuating consciousness | Expanding **subdural / epidural / contusion / SAH** | **Non-contrast CTH now** |
    | On **anticoagulant/antiplatelet** or coagulopathy | Delayed intracranial hemorrhage (low threshold) | **CTH**, reverse if bleeding |
    | Focal deficit, new seizure, worsening cognition | Structural lesion, evolving injury | CT → MRI |
    | Neck pain/trauma, Horner's, pulsatile tinnitus | **Cervical artery dissection** | CTA/MRA head & neck |
    | Positional headache after LP or trauma | CSF leak / intracranial hypotension | MRI; consider blood patch |
    | Second head impact before recovery | **Second-impact syndrome** (rare, catastrophic) | Remove from activity; urgent eval |

## Rapid approach

1. **Exclude a structural injury** — mechanism, anticoagulation, and exam drive whether you
   image. Use a validated decision rule for acute head injury imaging.
2. **Define the timeline (ICHD-3):** headache developing **within 7 days** of the injury (or of
   regaining consciousness / the ability to report). **Acute PTH** resolves within 3 months;
   **persistent PTH** lasts > 3 months.
3. **Classify the phenotype** — migraine-like vs tension-type-like — and treat accordingly.
4. **Find and treat the persistence drivers:** sleep disruption, mood (depression, PTSD),
   cervicogenic contribution, vestibular dysfunction, and **medication overuse**.
5. **Avoid opioids/butalbital and cap acute meds** — PTH is a high-risk setting for MOH.

## Diagnosis (ICHD-3)

PTH is a **secondary** headache *attributed to trauma or injury to the head and/or neck*. The
diagnosis is defined by the **temporal relationship** (onset ≤ 7 days), not by a specific
headache character — which is why the individual phenotype (migrainous vs tension-type) guides
treatment. Persistent PTH (> 3 months) is where most of the clinical work lives.

## Focused H&P + exam

- **Injury:** mechanism (blast/blunt/vehicle/fall), loss of consciousness, post-traumatic
  amnesia, number and timing of prior concussions.
- **Headache:** phenotype, frequency, acute-med days/month (MOH screen), triggers.
- **Comorbidity screen — the key to persistent PTH:** sleep, mood/**PTSD**, neck pain,
  dizziness/vestibular symptoms, cognition, photophobia.
- **Exam:** full neuro exam, **cervical spine and myofascial** exam, and a **vestibular/oculomotor**
  screen. Fixed deficits reopen the structural work-up.

## Workup

Image acutely (**CT**) for the red flags above or per a validated head-injury rule; **MRI** for
persistent, atypical, or progressive presentations. Uncomplicated concussion with a normal exam
does not need routine imaging. There is no blood or imaging test that "rules in" PTH.

## Management

Treat the **phenotype**, then dismantle the **perpetuators**.

=== "Treat the phenotype"
    - **Migraine-like PTH:** use the [migraine](migraine.md) playbook — stratified acute therapy
      (NSAID/triptan, or a **gepant/lasmiditan** where triptans are unsuitable) and, if frequent,
      a **preventive** (amitriptyline/nortriptyline, topiramate, propranolol; CGRP-targeted agents
      where first-line fails).
    - **Tension-type-like PTH:** analgesic sparing, amitriptyline, and address neck/sleep drivers.

=== "Treat the perpetuators"
    - **Sleep:** treat insomnia/OSA first — poor sleep alone sustains PTH.
    - **Mood / PTSD:** co-treat; PTH and PTSD strongly track together in service members.
    - **Cervicogenic:** targeted physical therapy; consider occipital/greater-occipital nerve blocks.
    - **Vestibular:** vestibular rehabilitation for dizziness/oculomotor symptoms.
    - **Medication overuse:** cap/withdraw overused acute meds; start a preventive.

=== "Avoid"
    - **Opioids and butalbital** — ineffective and drive MOH and dependence.
    - Prolonged strict rest — brief relative rest, then a **graded return to activity**.

## Order set / acute flare

!!! orderset "Refractory PTH flare (migraine phenotype)"
    Use the [status migrainosus ER protocol](migraine.md#order-set-status-migrainosus-er)
    (IV fluids + antiemetic + diphenhydramine + ketorolac + magnesium; dexamethasone before
    discharge). **No opioids.** Ensure a structural cause has been excluded first.

## Disposition

Outpatient with clear **return precautions** for the red flags above. For concussion, use a
**graded, supervised return-to-activity** progression rather than open-ended rest, and arrange
multidisciplinary follow-up (headache + sleep + behavioral health + vestibular/PT) for persistent
cases.

!!! military "Military medicine — post-traumatic headache / concussion (mTBI)"
    *Framework only — **verify against the current VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guideline for
    mild TBI, DoDI 6490.11, AR 40-501, and applicable aeromedical policy.** mTBI is protocolized
    in DoD; follow the mandated pathway, not just this page.*

    - **Event-driven protocol:** after a potentially concussive event (blast, vehicle, fall,
      blunt), DoD policy mandates evaluation and a mandatory recovery period — **screen with
      MACE2** and manage per the deployed-concussion protocol before return to duty.
    - **Deployability:** acute concussion is temporarily non-deployable pending clearance;
      **persistent PTH** with cognitive, sleep, or mood comorbidity can be deployment-limiting.
      Multiple concussions raise the stakes for return-to-duty decisions.
    - **Return to activity:** use the **Progressive Return to Activity** stages; symptom
      recurrence means step back a stage.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary profile during recovery; permanent profile for
      persistent, functionally limiting PTH/post-concussive syndrome.
    - **EPTS / LOD:** combat/duty-incurred TBI is typically **in the line of duty** — document
      the event, mechanism, and timeline carefully; it drives both LOD and VA rating.
    - **Retention — AR 40-501:** a migraine-phenotype PTH that is incapacitating ≥3 days/month
      falls under **para 3-31g** (neurology referral → up to 6 months prophylaxis → DES if
      uncontrolled). Residual **persistent severe headaches** after adequate treatment are captured
      by **para 3-31k** (other neurologic conditions) → **referral to the DES** (MEB/PEB). Document
      objective frequency, failed trials, neuropsych findings, and duty impact.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty (AR 40-501 ch. 4, table 4-2):** head-injury standards are graded
      by **how long the headache/post-traumatic syndrome persists** — e.g., **PTH resolving within
      ~14 days** causes a temporary aviation suspension (≈1–3 months by class), while **post-
      traumatic syndrome persisting beyond ~6 weeks** causes **permanent disqualification / 2-year
      termination of aviation service**. Confirm the exact tier in table 4-2 for the class involved.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

*Written from primary guidelines and trials; source articles are a reading list, not copied.*

- **Treat the phenotype, not the label.** Persistent PTH is most often **migraine-like**; when it
  is, it responds best to migraine-directed acute and preventive therapy.
- **Persistence is multifactorial.** Sleep disturbance, mood/PTSD, cervicogenic pain, vestibular
  dysfunction, and medication overuse perpetuate PTH — outcomes improve when these are treated
  alongside the headache, which is why **multidisciplinary** care is the standard for persistent PTH.
- **Rest is not the treatment.** Brief relative rest followed by a **graded return to activity**
  beats prolonged rest.
- **Avoid opioids/butalbital;** guard against medication-overuse headache, which is common here.
- **CGRP-targeted therapies** are of active interest for PTH given its migrainous biology; use
  established migraine indications and follow the evidence as it matures.
