# Post-traumatic headache

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** headache that develops in close temporal relation to head and/or neck trauma —
the most common symptom after concussion/mild TBI, and a signature problem in the military
population (blast, vehicle, fall, and blunt mechanisms). Most persistent post-traumatic
headache (PTH) is **phenotypically migraine or tension-type**, so you treat the phenotype
while managing the comorbidities that keep it going.

!!! danger "🚩 Red flags — this is a headache *after trauma*, so exclude structure first"
    | Pattern | Worry | Do now |
    |---|---|---|
    | Worsening/persistent severe headache, vomiting, ↓ or fluctuating consciousness | Expanding **subdural / epidural / contusion / SAH** | **Non-contrast CTH now** |
    | On **anticoagulant/antiplatelet** or coagulopathy | Delayed intracranial hemorrhage (low threshold) | **CTH**, reverse if bleeding |
    | Focal deficit, new seizure, worsening cognition | Structural lesion, evolving injury | CT → MRI |
    | Neck pain/trauma, Horner's, pulsatile tinnitus | **Cervical artery dissection** | CTA/MRA head & neck |
    | Positional headache after LP or trauma | CSF leak / intracranial hypotension | MRI; consider blood patch |
    | Second head impact before recovery | **Second-impact syndrome** (rare, catastrophic) | Remove from activity; urgent eval |

## Rapid approach

1. **Exclude a structural injury** — mechanism, anticoagulation, and exam drive whether you
   image. Use a validated decision rule for acute head injury imaging.
2. **Define the timeline (ICHD-3):** headache developing **within 7 days** of the injury (or of
   regaining consciousness / the ability to report). **Acute PTH** resolves within 3 months;
   **persistent PTH** lasts > 3 months.
3. **Classify the phenotype** — migraine-like vs tension-type-like — and treat accordingly.
4. **Find and treat the persistence drivers:** sleep disruption, mood (depression, PTSD),
   cervicogenic contribution, vestibular dysfunction, and **medication overuse**.
5. **Avoid opioids/butalbital and cap acute meds** — PTH is a high-risk setting for MOH.

## Diagnosis (ICHD-3)

PTH is a **secondary** headache *attributed to trauma or injury to the head and/or neck*. The
diagnosis is defined by the **temporal relationship** (onset ≤ 7 days), not by a specific
headache character — which is why the individual phenotype (migrainous vs tension-type) guides
treatment. Persistent PTH (> 3 months) is where most of the clinical work lives.

## Focused H&P + exam

- **Injury:** mechanism (blast/blunt/vehicle/fall), loss of consciousness, post-traumatic
  amnesia, number and timing of prior concussions.
- **Headache:** phenotype, frequency, acute-med days/month (MOH screen), triggers.
- **Comorbidity screen — the key to persistent PTH:** sleep, mood/**PTSD**, neck pain,
  dizziness/vestibular symptoms, cognition, photophobia.
- **Exam:** full neuro exam, **cervical spine and myofascial** exam, and a **vestibular/oculomotor**
  screen. Fixed deficits reopen the structural work-up.

## Workup

Image acutely (**CT**) for the red flags above or per a validated head-injury rule; **MRI** for
persistent, atypical, or progressive presentations. Uncomplicated concussion with a normal exam
does not need routine imaging. There is no blood or imaging test that "rules in" PTH.

## Management

### The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Exclude structural injury** (red-flag table / validated head-CT rule) — always first.
2. **Classify the phenotype** — migraine-like or tension-type-like.
3. **Treat the phenotype** on the matching migraine/tension ladder (Step 1).
4. **Dismantle the perpetuators in parallel** — sleep, mood/PTSD, cervicogenic, vestibular, and
   **medication overuse** (Step 2). *This is where persistent PTH is won or lost.*
5. **Prescribe aerobic exercise — it's a treatment, not just "return to activity"** (protocol box
   below); brief relative rest, then progress. Not open-ended rest.
6. **Refractory?** Nerve blocks, CGRP therapy, and IV bridges (Step 3); build a **multidisciplinary**
   team (headache + sleep + behavioral health + vestibular/PT).
7. **Avoid opioids/butalbital** throughout — PTH is a high-risk MOH setting.

!!! note "Who persists — and why to treat early"
    Most acute PTH resolves, but a substantial fraction chronifies (**TRACK-TBI:** ~60% acute PTH at
    2 weeks → **~52% still present at 3 months → ~29% at 12 months**). **Risk factors for persistence:**
    **female sex, prior migraine history, fewer years of education, acute PTH lasting >30 consecutive
    days, HIT-6 >45, and cognitive symptoms** (difficulty concentrating — the strongest single
    association, OR >3), plus comorbid anxiety/depression/sleep disorders.

    **Treat early — don't wait for it to become "persistent."** Scheduled analgesics in the **first
    ~72 h** may reduce acute severity; **starting a preventive within ~4–6 weeks** may prevent central
    sensitization/chronification (and preclinical data show **early onabotulinumtoxinA fully prevented**
    the transition to persistent PTH, while delayed dosing only partly did). Be most aggressive in the
    high-risk patients above.

!!! orderset "Step 1 — treat the phenotype (there is no PTH-specific drug — borrow the ladder)"
    **Lifestyle first (2025 AAPM&R):** regular sleep schedule, no skipped meals, hydration, limit
    alcohol, low-intensity aerobic exercise, and relaxation strategies — start these at the first visit.

    **Prophylaxis threshold (2025 AAPM&R):** offer a daily preventive when headaches are **≥10 days/
    month or disabling.**

    - **Migraine-like PTH** → the [migraine acute + preventive ladders](migraine.md):
        - **Acute:** stratified — NSAID → **triptan**, or a **gepant/lasmiditan** where triptans are
          unsuitable. **Cap acute days; prefer a gepant** (no MOH) given the high overuse risk here —
          *but note the gepant preference is extrapolated from migraine; there are **no PTH-specific
          gepant efficacy data**.*
        - **Only positive acute-PTH RCT:** **metoclopramide 10 mg + diphenhydramine 25 mg IV** beat
          placebo (a modest ~1.4-point pain improvement) — a reasonable **first-line ED** combination
          for an acute PTH attack.
        - **Preventive (if frequent/disabling):** **amitriptyline or nortriptyline 10–50 mg qHS**
          (helps comorbid sleep/mood — often the best first pick in PTH); **venlafaxine (SNRI)**;
          **candesartan (ARB)**; or **propranolol**; escalate to **CGRP therapy** or
          **onabotulinumtoxinA** (below) if first-line fails.
    - **Tension-type-like PTH** → **amitriptyline/nortriptyline** + analgesic sparing, and treat the
      neck and sleep drivers directly.

    !!! warning "Prefer cognitively-neutral preventives — go easy on topiramate/valproate"
        **Topiramate and valproate are not first-line for PTH** — their **cognitive side effects
        compound the TBI patient's existing cognitive complaints** (Lancet Neurology algorithm; not
        among the 2025 AAPM&R named options). **Prefer amitriptyline, venlafaxine, candesartan, or
        propranolol.**
        **Prazosin** is a useful military-population option — emerging PTH evidence with minimal side
        effects and a **dual benefit for comorbid PTSD nightmares** (generic/formulary).

!!! orderset "Prescribe sub-threshold aerobic exercise — the best non-drug evidence in concussion/PTH"
    Exercise is a **specific therapy**, not generic advice — start it **early, even while symptomatic.**

    - **Start within 24–72 h** with light activity (walking); waiting for symptoms to fully resolve is
      *worse*. Subthreshold aerobic exercise **sped recovery (median 13 vs 17 days)** and **cut
      persisting symptoms (21% vs 32%)** vs stretching, with a **large effect size (g ≈ 1.0).**
    - **Dose it to a heart-rate target:** the HR at the patient's **symptom-exacerbation threshold**
      (Buffalo-type test), or simply **start at ~50% of age-predicted max HR (220 − age)**, 20 min/day,
      and titrate up as tolerated — stay **below** the level that flares symptoms.
    - **Works for persisting PTH too**, and **regardless of initial symptom burden.** For service
      members, this doubles as a structured, documentable **return-to-duty** progression.

!!! orderset "Step 2 — dismantle the perpetuators (every visit; this is the real work)"
    - **CBT for headache — the strongest non-drug evidence, and it's from *veterans*.** In the
      **McGeary RCT (JAMA Neurol 2022)** of post-9/11 combat veterans with PTH+PTSD, **8 sessions of
      headache-specific CBT cut headache disability (HIT-6 −3.4) AND improved PTSD symptoms.**
      Crucially, **treating the PTSD alone (cognitive processing therapy) improved PTSD but NOT the
      headache** — so **refer for headache-specific CBT, not just PTSD therapy.**
    - **Sleep:** treat insomnia/OSA **first** — poor sleep alone sustains PTH.
    - **Mood / PTSD:** co-treat; PTH and PTSD strongly track together in service members (**prazosin**
      helps PTSD nightmares — see Step 1).
    - **Cervicogenic (a distinct PTH subtype):** **manual therapy of the neck** ("Good Practice,"
      2025 AAPM&R) + targeted **physical therapy**; **greater/lesser occipital nerve blocks** for
      occipital tenderness.
    - **Vestibular:** **cervical + vestibular physical therapy** — facilitates return to activity
      (HR ~3.9 vs rest) when dizziness/neck pain/headache persist >10 days.
    - **Medication overuse:** cap or withdraw overused acute meds and start a preventive
      ([MOH page](medication-overuse.md)).

!!! orderset "Step 3 — refractory persistent PTH (specialist / multidisciplinary)"
    Evidence is largely extrapolated from migraine — set expectations. Reasonable options:

    - **Nerve blocks — consider these *earlier*, not just when refractory.** PTH-specific data are
      strong: **~88% average pain improvement immediately, ~73% sustained at 6 months** (File 2025),
      and GON blocks historically help **PTH more than migraine (72% vs 54%)**. Fast, low-risk,
      repeatable — a high-yield early move.
    - **CGRP therapies** — worth trying, but note the PTH-specific signal: the **fremanezumab (ligand)**
      phase-2 RCT was **negative** (a *non-significant* reduction in moderate-to-severe headache days),
      while **erenumab (receptor)** showed an **open-label** benefit — so if you use a CGRP for PTH,
      the **receptor**-targeting agents (erenumab, or an oral gepant) are the more rational bet
      (low-certainty).
    - **OnabotulinumtoxinA — the only treatment with a *positive PTH-specific RCT*.** In military
      veterans (Zirovich 2021 crossover), headaches/week **fell ~43% vs *worsened* ~35% on placebo**
      (p<0.001), with less pain severity. Combined with the preclinical "early dosing prevents
      chronification" signal, **consider it earlier — don't wait for a strict chronic-migraine
      phenotype** (though that's still what on-label TRICARE coverage needs).
    - **Repetitive TMS** — small RCTs show short-term benefit (≈58% got ≥50% headache reduction;
      60% vs 10% return-to-work in one pilot), **but the VA/DoD mTBI CPG suggests *against* it**
      (small trials, no sustained benefit at 4–6 months, seizure-risk concern) — **reserve for
      specialist/research settings.**
    - **IV bridge** for a severe refractory flare — see the acute-flare box.

!!! orderset "Acute refractory PTH flare (migraine phenotype)"
    Use the [status migrainosus ER protocol](migraine.md#order-set-status-migrainosus-er)
    (dopamine antagonist + NSAID + nerve block; magnesium; **dexamethasone** before discharge).
    **No opioids or butalbital.** **Ensure a structural cause has been excluded first** — a
    post-traumatic patient with a *new* or escalating headache gets imaged, not just medicated.

!!! warning "Avoid"
    - **Opioids and butalbital** — ineffective and drive MOH and dependence.
    - **Prolonged strict rest** — brief relative rest, then a **graded return to activity**.

!!! tip "TRICARE — PTH prescribing"
    Prescribe by phenotype: **amitriptyline, nortriptyline, venlafaxine, candesartan, propranolol,
    and prazosin** are all **generic/formulary, no PA** — the TCAs are an efficient first pick
    (headache + sleep + mood), and prazosin is a two-for-one with PTSD nightmares. **Topiramate is
    formulary but cognitively costly in TBI** — not first-line here. For a CGRP, **galcanezumab
    (Emgality) is the preferred, no-PA** option — but the PTH evidence favors **receptor**-targeting
    agents, and **erenumab (Aimovig) / atogepant (Qulipta)** are **PA-gated + neurologist**.
    **OnabotulinumtoxinA** needs the chronic-migraine phenotype for on-label coverage. Nerve blocks,
    manual/vestibular PT, and **headache-specific CBT** go through normal specialty/rehab referral.

## Disposition

Outpatient with clear **return precautions** for the red flags above. For concussion, use a
**graded, supervised return-to-activity** progression rather than open-ended rest, and arrange
multidisciplinary follow-up (headache + sleep + behavioral health + vestibular/PT) for persistent
cases.

!!! note "Concussion return-to-play/duty essentials (2025 NEJM review)"
    - **Return to school/cognitive work *before* return to sport/full duty** — early, graded cognitive
      activity speeds recovery; prolonged absence delays it.
    - **Cervical + vestibular PT** facilitates return (HR ~3.9 vs rest) when dizziness/neck pain/
      headache persist >10 days.
    - **SCOAT6** (Sport Concussion Office Assessment Tool-6) is a free office-assessment aid (awaiting
      prospective validation).
    - **Blood biomarkers (GFAP, total tau) are NOT ready for routine clinical use** — don't adopt
      prematurely.

!!! military "Military medicine — post-traumatic headache / concussion (mTBI)"
    *Standards below are quoted from **DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1/Vol 2**, **AR 40-501**, the **VA/DoD mTBI
    CPG**, and **DoDI 6490.11**; the DES process is governed by **DoDI 1332.18 / DoDM 1332.18**.
    mTBI is protocolized in DoD — follow the mandated pathway, not just this page.*

    - **Event-driven protocol:** after a potentially concussive event (blast, vehicle, fall,
      blunt), DoD policy mandates evaluation and a mandatory recovery period — **screen with
      MACE2** and manage per the deployed-concussion protocol before return to duty.
    - **Deployability:** acute concussion is temporarily non-deployable pending clearance;
      **persistent PTH** with cognitive, sleep, or mood comorbidity can be deployment-limiting.
      Multiple concussions raise the stakes for return-to-duty decisions.
    - **Return to activity:** use the **Progressive Return to Activity** stages; symptom
      recurrence means step back a stage.
    - **Profile (DA Form 3349):** temporary profile during recovery; permanent profile for
      persistent, functionally limiting PTH/post-concussive syndrome.
    - **EPTS / LOD:** combat/duty-incurred TBI is typically **in the line of duty** — document
      the event, mechanism, and timeline carefully; it drives both LOD and VA rating.
    - **Retention — DoD & Army:** the joint standard (**DoDI 6130.03 Vol 2, para 5.26.h**) places
      **TBI with persistent sequelae** — including **severe headaches**, sensory/cognitive/motor
      impairment, or seizures — below retention standards. For the Army, a migraine-phenotype PTH
      incapacitating ≥3 days/month falls under **AR 40-501 para 3-31g**, and residual **persistent
      severe headaches** under **para 3-31k** → **referral to the DES** (MEB/PEB). Document objective
      frequency, failed trials, neuropsych findings, and duty impact.
    - **Accession — DoDI 6130.03 Vol 1, para 6.26:** for *applicants*, **persistent post-concussive
      symptoms interfering with normal activities or lasting > 1 month** (headache, dizziness,
      cognitive/memory problems), a history of head trauma with persistent focal deficit, or **≥3
      mild brain injuries**, are **disqualifying for entry**.
    - **Aeromedical / special duty (AR 40-501 ch. 4, table 4-2):** head-injury standards are graded
      by **how long the headache/post-traumatic syndrome persists** — e.g., **PTH resolving within
      ~14 days** causes a temporary aviation suspension (≈1–3 months by class), while **post-
      traumatic syndrome persisting beyond ~6 weeks** causes **permanent disqualification / 2-year
      termination of aviation service**. Confirm the exact tier in table 4-2 for the class involved.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

*Written from primary guidelines and trials; source articles are a reading list, not copied.*

- **Treat the phenotype, not the label.** Persistent PTH is most often **migraine-like**; when it
  is, it responds best to migraine-directed acute and preventive therapy.
- **Persistence is multifactorial.** Sleep disturbance, mood/PTSD, cervicogenic pain, vestibular
  dysfunction, and medication overuse perpetuate PTH — outcomes improve when these are treated
  alongside the headache, which is why **multidisciplinary** care is the standard for persistent PTH.
- **Rest is not the treatment — prescribed aerobic exercise is.** Subthreshold aerobic exercise
  started **within 24–72 h** speeds recovery (median 13 vs 17 days) and cuts persisting symptoms
  (21% vs 32%), effect size **g ≈ 1.0** — dose it to ~50% age-predicted max HR or a symptom threshold.
  The strongest non-drug evidence in concussion/PTH.
- **Nerve blocks may be *especially* effective in PTH** (~88% improve, ~73% sustained at 6 months;
  better than in migraine) — consider them **early**, not just when refractory.
- **The only positive acute-PTH RCT is metoclopramide + diphenhydramine** (modest ~1.4-point benefit)
  — a reasonable ED go-to for an attack. **Gepants are the theoretical acute ideal but unproven in PTH.**
- **Treat early and identify who's at risk.** ~52% of acute PTH persists at 3 months (TRACK-TBI);
  female sex, prior migraine, acute PTH >30 days, HIT-6 >45, and cognitive symptoms predict
  persistence. Scheduled early analgesia + preventive within 4–6 weeks may prevent chronification.
- **The first formal PTH treatment guidance arrived (2025 AAPM&R Action Collaborative):** lifestyle
  first, **preventive at ≥10 headache days/month or disabling**, named options **amitriptyline,
  venlafaxine, candesartan, and CGRP inhibitors**, and **manual therapy for cervicogenic** PTH.
- **OnabotulinumtoxinA is the only positive PTH-specific RCT** (Zirovich 2021, veterans: ~43% fewer
  headaches vs worsening on placebo) — consider it earlier than "chronic-migraine only."
- **Headache-specific CBT is the strongest non-drug evidence** (McGeary 2022: HIT-6 −3.4 + PTSD
  benefit in veterans) — and **treating PTSD alone did not fix the headache.**
- **Avoid opioids/butalbital**, and **avoid topiramate/valproate first-line** (cognitive cost in TBI).
- **CGRP evidence is mechanism-relevant:** **fremanezumab (ligand)** phase-2 negative vs **erenumab
  (receptor)** open-label positive — prefer receptor-targeting agents if using a CGRP.
- **The evidence base is still thin** — a 2026 systematic review found only 2 low-bias RCTs (1
  PTH-specific); nearly all treatment is extrapolated.
