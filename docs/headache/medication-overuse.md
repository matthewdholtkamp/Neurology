# Medication overuse headache

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** in a patient with a **pre-existing primary headache**, frequent use of acute
headache medication drives headaches to **≥15 days/month** — both a complication and a
self-perpetuating engine. Recognizing it is half the treatment; the other half is **starting an
effective preventive while unwinding the overused drug.**

!!! danger "🚩 Don't diagnose MOH until you've excluded a new secondary headache"
    A patient whose headaches suddenly escalated may have a new process (mass, CVST, intracranial
    hypertension). If the **pattern changed** or there are any [red flags](index.md), work it up
    before attributing everything to medication overuse.

## Recognize the thresholds (ICHD-3: use over >3 months)

| Acute medication | Overuse threshold |
|---|---|
| Simple analgesics (NSAIDs, acetaminophen) | **≥15 days/month** |
| Triptans | **≥10 days/month** |
| Ergots | **≥10 days/month** |
| Opioids | **≥10 days/month** |
| Butalbital-containing combinations | **≥10 days/month** (worst offender) |
| Combination analgesics | **≥10 days/month** |

**Gepants (ubrogepant, rimegepant, atogepant) do not cause MOH** and carry **no monthly cap** —
they are the acute agent of choice in this population.

## Rapid approach

1. **Count acute-med days** at every headache visit — the screening question people forget.
2. **Exclude a new secondary headache** if the pattern changed.
3. **Start an effective preventive** — *don't wait for withdrawal to finish.*
4. **Address the overused agent** — stop or cap it, with a plan matched to the drug class.
5. **Bridge** the withdrawal window and **educate** — MOH recurs without a durable behavior change
   and a capped acute plan.
6. **Follow up** at a defined interval to confirm improvement.

---

## The prescriptive flow (what I want done, in order)

1. **Confirm MOH** (threshold table) and **rule out a new secondary headache** if the pattern changed.
2. **Start a preventive now** — pick by comorbidity, or go straight to a **CGRP therapy**, which
   works *even with ongoing overuse* (Step 1).
3. **Convert the acute plan:** stop the abrupt-stoppable culprits, **taper** the ones that need it,
   and hand the patient a **capped** rescue plan — **prefer a gepant** (Step 2).
4. **Bridge** the first few weeks (Step 3).
5. **Educate + set a follow-up** (diary, written acute-day cap, return visit).
6. **Severe (opioid/barbiturate) or refractory:** structured withdrawal / infusion program.

## Management

!!! orderset "Step 1 — start an effective preventive (do this first, don't wait)"
    - **Pick a migraine preventive by comorbidity** — topiramate, amitriptyline/nortriptyline,
      propranolol/metoprolol, candesartan, valproate (see the
      [migraine preventive ladder](migraine.md#preventive-treatment-the-ladder) for doses/titration).
    - **CGRP-targeted therapies (mAbs *and* gepants) work even while the patient is still overusing**
      and have transformed MOH care — reasonable **early**, and often the cleanest path. As migraine
      frequency falls, patients **naturally reduce** acute-med days, so **forced abrupt withdrawal is
      frequently unnecessary** when a CGRP mAb is on board.
    - On TRICARE, the frictionless CGRP is **galcanezumab (Emgality) — preferred, no PA** (see box).

!!! orderset "Step 2 — convert the acute plan (stop or cap the culprit)"
    | Overused class | How to stop | Why |
    |---|---|---|
    | **NSAIDs, triptans, ergots** | **Can stop abruptly** | No dangerous withdrawal syndrome |
    | **Opioids** | **Taper** | Withdrawal symptoms; relapse risk |
    | **Butalbital / barbiturates** | **Taper (careful)** | Abrupt stop risks **seizures** |
    | **Benzodiazepine co-use** | **Taper** | Withdrawal seizures |

    - Replace with a **capped rescue plan: a non-overused acute agent limited to ≤2 days/week.**
    - **Prefer a gepant** (ubrogepant/rimegepant) for rescue — no MOH, no monthly cap. Rimegepant
      also doubles as a preventive.

!!! orderset "Step 3 — bridge the withdrawal window (2–4 weeks)"
    Symptoms flare transiently as the overused drug comes off. Any of:

    - **Scheduled NSAID:** e.g., **naproxen 500 mg BID × 2–4 weeks** (if not the overused agent),
      with an **antiemetic** (metoclopramide or prochlorperazine) as needed.
    - **Greater occipital nerve block** — fast, opioid-sparing, pregnancy-safe.
    - **Prednisone taper** (e.g., **60 mg tapering over ~6 days**) — **evidence is mixed** (some
      negative RCTs); reasonable in selected patients, not routine.
    - **DHE** (per the [ER/status protocol](migraine.md#order-set-status-migrainosus-er)) for a
      severe withdrawal flare.

!!! warning "Avoid — and taper existing use"
    **Opioids and butalbital** are the **highest-risk culprits and the least effective** acute
    drugs. Don't start them; **prioritize tapering** patients already on them. They are also the
    classes most likely to require a structured withdrawal program.

!!! tip "TRICARE — MOH prescribing"
    - **Preventives:** oral agents (topiramate, amitriptyline, propranolol, candesartan) are
      generic/formulary, no PA. **Galcanezumab (Emgality) is the preferred, no-PA CGRP** — the go-to
      antibody when you want a CGRP early in MOH.
    - **Rescue:** triptans are formulary; the **gepants (Ubrelvy acute, Qulipta preventive, Nurtec)**
      require a **PA + neurologist** — but they're the ideal MOH rescue because they carry no cap.
    - **Bridge:** naproxen, prednisone, antiemetics are all generic/formulary.

## Disposition

Outpatient for most, with **clear written instructions, a capped acute plan, a diary, and a defined
follow-up.** A minority with severe overuse (**opioid/barbiturate**) or refractory disease benefit
from a **structured withdrawal / infusion program** (inpatient or day-hospital).

!!! military "Military medicine — medication overuse headache"
    *Framework only — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 6130.03, DoDI 1332.18, and aeromedical
    policy.*

    - **Common and under-recognized** — service members self-treat frequent headaches with OTC
      analgesics or leftover opioids/butalbital; screen acute-med days deliberately.
    - **Opioid/butalbital overuse** carries fitness-for-duty, drug-testing, and readiness
      implications — an added reason to avoid these classes and taper existing use.
    - **Deployability:** the **underlying primary headache** (usually migraine) drives
      deployability; treating MOH often restores function.
    - **Retention — AR 40-501 para 3-31g:** frame around the **underlying primary headache** once
      overuse is corrected — if incapacitating attacks still interfere with duty ≥3 days/month after
      an adequate neurology-directed trial (up to 6 months prophylaxis), the standard directs
      **referral to the DES** (MEB/PEB).
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** sedating acute meds and opioids/barbiturates are
      **disqualifying/waiver-dependent**; correcting overuse can support return to status.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **Both levers work:** starting a **preventive** and **reducing** the overused acute drug each
  improve MOH — and they work best together.
- **CGRP monoclonal antibodies and gepants are effective despite ongoing overuse.** Real-world and
  RCT data show mAbs cut headache and acute-med days **without forced abrupt withdrawal or
  hospitalization** — patients taper themselves as frequency falls. This has made painful,
  mandatory "detox" **largely optional** in the CGRP era.
- **Gepants carry no MOH risk and no monthly cap** — the preferred rescue in this population.
- **Opioids and butalbital are the highest-risk culprits** and should be avoided/tapered; they are
  the classes most likely to need a structured withdrawal.
- **Patient education and follow-up** are core — MOH relapses without a durable behavior change and
  a capped acute plan.

**Key sources:** ICHD-3 (MOH criteria); AHS guidance on MOH; MOTS treatment-strategy trial; trials
and real-world series of CGRP therapies in patients with acute-medication overuse.
