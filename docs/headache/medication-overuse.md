# Medication overuse headache

<span class="reviewed-stamp">Last reviewed: July 2026</span>

**One-liner:** in a patient with a **pre-existing primary headache**, frequent use of acute
headache medication drives headaches to **≥ 15 days/month** — both a complication and a
self-perpetuating engine. Recognizing it is half the treatment; the other half is starting a
preventive while unwinding the overused drug.

!!! danger "🚩 Don't diagnose MOH until you've excluded a new secondary headache"
    A patient whose headaches suddenly escalated may have a new process (mass, CVST, intracranial
    hypertension). If the **pattern changed** or there are any [red flags](index.md), work it up
    before attributing everything to medication overuse.

## Recognize the thresholds

Overuse is defined by **days/month of use over > 3 months**:

| Acute medication | Overuse threshold |
|---|---|
| Simple analgesics (NSAIDs, acetaminophen) | **≥ 15 days/month** |
| Triptans | **≥ 10 days/month** |
| Ergots | **≥ 10 days/month** |
| Opioids | **≥ 10 days/month** |
| Butalbital-containing combinations | **≥ 10 days/month** (worst offender) |
| Combination analgesics | **≥ 10 days/month** |

**Gepants (ubrogepant, rimegepant, atogepant) do not cause MOH** and are useful acute agents in
this population.

## Rapid approach

1. **Count acute-med days** every headache visit — this is the screening question people forget.
2. **Exclude a new secondary headache** if the pattern changed.
3. **Start an effective preventive** (don't wait for withdrawal to finish).
4. **Withdraw or cap the overused agent** — with a plan matched to the drug class.
5. **Bridge** the withdrawal period and **educate** — MOH recurs without behavior change.
6. **Follow up** at a defined interval to confirm improvement.

## Management

=== "Withdraw the culprit"
    - **NSAIDs/triptans/ergots:** can generally be **stopped abruptly**.
    - **Opioids, butalbital, benzodiazepines:** **taper** — abrupt cessation risks withdrawal
      (including seizures with barbiturates/benzos).
    - Replace with a **capped** acute plan (e.g., a non-overused agent limited to ≤ 2 days/week),
      and prefer a **gepant** where an acute option is still needed.

=== "Start prevention early"
    - Choose a migraine preventive by comorbidity (topiramate, amitriptyline, propranolol,
      candesartan).
    - **CGRP-targeted therapies (mAbs and gepants) are effective even with ongoing overuse** and
      have simplified MOH management — reasonable early when first-line fails.

=== "Bridge the gap"
    - A short course of a scheduled **NSAID** and **antiemetic** ± a **steroid taper** (evidence
      mixed) or **DHE** can ease the withdrawal window.

=== "Avoid"
    - **Opioids and butalbital** — they are the highest-risk culprits; don't reach for them, and
      prioritize tapering patients already on them.

## Disposition

Outpatient for most, with clear written instructions and follow-up. A minority with severe overuse
(opioid/barbiturate) or refractory disease benefit from a **structured withdrawal/infusion program**.

!!! military "Military medicine — medication overuse headache"
    *Framework only — verify against current AR 40-501, DoDI 1332.18, and aeromedical policy.*

    - **Common and under-recognized** — service members self-treat frequent headaches with OTC
      analgesics or leftover opioids/butalbital; screen acute-med days deliberately.
    - **Opioid/butalbital overuse** carries fitness-for-duty, drug-testing, and readiness
      implications — an added reason to avoid these classes and taper existing use.
    - **Deployability:** the **underlying primary headache** (usually migraine) drives
      deployability; treating MOH often restores function.
    - **Retention — AR 40-501 para 3-31g:** frame around the **underlying primary headache** once
      overuse is corrected — if incapacitating attacks still interfere with duty ≥3 days/month after
      an adequate neurology-directed trial (up to 6 months prophylaxis), the standard directs
      **referral to the DES** (MEB/PEB).
    - **Aeromedical / special duty:** sedating acute meds and opioids/barbiturates are
      **disqualifying/waiver-dependent**; correcting overuse can support return to status.

## 📚 Discover — evidence brief

- **Both levers work:** starting a **preventive** and **withdrawing** the overused acute drug each
  improve MOH — and they work best together.
- **CGRP monoclonal antibodies and gepants are effective despite ongoing overuse**, which has made
  management less dependent on painful abrupt withdrawal.
- **Opioids and butalbital are the highest-risk culprits** and should be avoided/tapered.
- **Patient education and follow-up** are core — MOH relapses without a durable behavior change and
  a capped acute plan.
